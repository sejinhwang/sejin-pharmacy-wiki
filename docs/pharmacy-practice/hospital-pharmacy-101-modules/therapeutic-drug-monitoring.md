# Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

Source: [Notion page](https://www.notion.so/c2cd168bd90841d6a4c9acc5d1abd955)

Status: Standalone draft migrated from Notion. Requires local policy and clinical guideline review before being treated as a final protocol.

## Overview

Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) is the use of measured medicine concentrations, interpreted together with clinical data, to individualise dosing.

In hospital pharmacy practice, TDM is most useful when:

- the medicine has a narrow therapeutic index
- clinical response is difficult to measure quickly
- patient pharmacokinetics are unpredictable, e.g. critical illness, rapidly changing renal function, renal replacement therapy, extremes of body size, or major fluid shifts

TDM is not simply “checking a level”. It is a clinical consult linking:

1. indication
2. sampling time
3. interpretation
4. dose adjustment
5. follow-up monitoring

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, the reader should be able to:

- describe the importance of TDM in hospital pharmacy practice
- identify when TDM is warranted
- explain the hospital pharmacist’s role in TDM
- perform a systematic TDM consult
- apply TDM principles to vancomycin, tobramycin, and lithium

## 1. Indications and Importance of TDM

### Why TDM matters

| Domain | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Efficacy | Improves probability of target attainment, especially for serious infection or narrow-index medicines. |
| Safety | Reduces avoidable toxicity, e.g. nephrotoxicity with vancomycin or aminoglycosides, and neurotoxicity with lithium. |
| Consistency | Supports standardised, auditable dosing in high-risk clinical areas. |
| Stewardship | Prevents unnecessary escalation, prolonged exposure, or avoidable adverse drug events. |

### When TDM is warranted

TDM is most useful when both of the following are true:

- concentration correlates with efficacy and/or toxicity
- there is high inter-patient pharmacokinetic variability

Practical triggers include:

- narrow therapeutic index
- renal dysfunction or rapidly changing renal function, including AKI recovery
- critical illness with altered volume of distribution
- augmented renal clearance
- renal replacement therapy
- extremes of body size where weight-based dosing is used
- suspected toxicity
- suspected non-adherence
- interacting medicines or physiological changes affecting clearance
- serious infection where underexposure risks treatment failure

## 2. Sampling Principles

Correct timing is essential. A precisely analysed sample collected at the wrong time can produce the wrong clinical decision.

Document the following before interpreting a level:

- medicine, dose, route, and interval
- last dose administration time
- infusion start and stop time, if IV
- sample collection time
- dose history, including loading doses, omitted doses, delayed doses, or held doses
- renal function trend and urine output where relevant

### Common sampling patterns

| Pattern | Typical use | Key caution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Trough / pre-dose level | Many medicines where pre-dose concentration is clinically interpretable | Must be a true pre-dose level. |
| Timed post-dose level | Aminoglycosides, Bayesian vancomycin workflows, selected protocols | Must match the local protocol timing. |
| Standardised 12-hour level | Lithium | Random lithium levels are difficult to interpret. |
| Early/non-steady-state level | Urgent toxicity, unstable renal function, severe infection | Interpret using clinical context; do not apply steady-state assumptions blindly. |

## 3. Role of the Hospital Pharmacist

### Core responsibilities

The pharmacist’s role spans the full TDM workflow:

- identify patients who require TDM and patients who do not
- select the correct test and sampling strategy
- support safe order entry and nursing communication
- interpret concentrations in clinical context
- assess timing validity before changing the dose
- recommend dose, interval, route, infusion strategy, and follow-up levels
- assess nephrotoxins and additive toxicity risks
- document the target, interpretation, recommendation, and monitoring plan

### System-level responsibilities

TDM is also a medicines-safety and systems role:

- build and maintain dosing protocols
- standardise order panels, e.g. level plus renal panel
- reduce wrong-time level errors
- support antimicrobial stewardship and high-alert medicine monitoring
- educate prescribers and nursing staff on correct sampling

## 4. Systematic TDM Consult Workflow

### Step 1: Clarify the clinical question

Ask:

- Why is the medicine being used?
- What is the desired outcome?
- Is therapy empiric, definitive, suppressive, or step-down?
- Is the goal efficacy, toxicity assessment, adherence assessment, or dose optimisation?

### Step 2: Gather dosing and sampling facts

Confirm:

- current regimen: dose, interval, route, infusion duration
- loading dose history
- missed, delayed, held, or refused doses
- last dose administration time
- level collection time
- whether the level is valid for the intended interpretation

### Step 3: Assess patient-specific pharmacokinetics

Review:

- SCr, eGFR/CrCl estimate, and renal function trend
- urine output where relevant
- renal replacement therapy status
- actual body weight and dosing weight approach
- fluid balance, oedema, critical illness, burns, sepsis, or large-volume resuscitation
- interacting medicines

### Step 4: Interpret the level in context

Classify the result:

- invalid or uninterpretable due to timing/dose history issue
- below target / likely underexposed
- within target
- above target / likely overexposed
- toxic or potentially toxic

Do not adjust the regimen until timing validity is assessed.

### Step 5: Make and communicate the dosing plan

The recommendation should specify:

- whether to continue, hold, stop, or change therapy
- new dose and interval, if applicable
- timing of next dose
- timing of next level
- renal panel / electrolytes / toxicity monitoring
- any medicine changes to reduce toxicity risk

### Step 6: Document follow-up

A useful TDM note should answer:

- What was the target?
- Was the level valid?
- What does the level mean?
- What dose change is recommended?
- What monitoring is required next?

## 5. Medicine-Specific Applications

## Vancomycin

### Why monitor

Vancomycin monitoring aims to optimise efficacy for serious Gram-positive infection while minimising nephrotoxicity.

### Exposure target concept

Vancomycin effect is best predicted by AUC/MIC. Many programmes use an AUC target range, commonly 400–600 mg·h/L for serious MRSA infection, depending on local guidance, MIC assumptions, and infection severity.

### Pharmacist approach

1. Confirm indication and severity:
   - SSTI
   - pneumonia
   - bacteraemia
   - endocarditis
   - bone/joint infection
   - CNS infection where applicable
2. Review renal function trend and nephrotoxins.
3. Confirm sampling strategy:
   - Bayesian/AUC workflow
   - two-level AUC calculation
   - trough-based monitoring where AUC tools are unavailable
4. Assess loading dose need when rapid target attainment is required.
5. Adjust maintenance dose using level validity, renal function, and clinical response.

### Common pitfalls

- interpreting a trough that is not a true trough
- chasing high troughs and increasing nephrotoxicity risk
- failing to re-check after AKI recovery
- ignoring missed doses or delayed infusions
- not reviewing concurrent nephrotoxins

### Monitoring points

- SCr and renal function trend
- urine output if clinically relevant
- concurrent nephrotoxins
- clinical response and culture results
- ongoing need for IV therapy and de-escalation opportunities

## Tobramycin and Other Aminoglycosides

### Why monitor

Aminoglycosides have concentration-dependent killing and clinically important nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity risks.

### Common dosing frameworks

| Framework | Typical use | Monitoring concept |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Extended-interval dosing | Many Gram-negative infection protocols | High peak exposure with low accumulation; often uses a single timed level and nomogram/protocol interpretation. |
| Traditional multiple-daily dosing | Selected situations depending on local protocol | Peak/trough approach may be used. |
| Synergy dosing | Selected Gram-positive infections | Lower exposure targets and careful duration review. |

### Pharmacist approach

1. Confirm indication, organism, and intended duration.
2. Verify dosing weight and renal function.
3. Confirm whether extended-interval dosing is appropriate under local criteria.
4. Ensure level timing matches the protocol.
5. Reassess daily if renal function is unstable.
6. Recommend de-escalation or discontinuation when cultures and clinical status permit.

### Common pitfalls

- prolonged aminoglycoside exposure without reassessment
- level drawn at the wrong post-dose time
- failure to respond to renal deterioration
- using aminoglycoside therapy when a safer definitive option is available

### Monitoring points

- daily renal function when inpatient and unstable
- urine output where relevant
- vestibular symptoms
- hearing changes
- concomitant nephrotoxins
- planned stop date or review date

## Lithium

### Why monitor

Lithium has a very narrow therapeutic index. Concentrations are strongly affected by renal function, hydration status, sodium balance, and interacting medicines.

### Sampling standard

Use a standardised 12-hour post-dose level unless local guidance specifies otherwise. Random lithium concentrations are difficult to interpret.

### Pharmacist approach

1. Confirm indication:
   - acute mania
   - maintenance therapy
   - augmentation strategy
2. Confirm target range per local guidance and patient context.
3. Verify the level is a true 12-hour level.
4. Screen for factors increasing lithium concentration:
   - dehydration
   - acute illness
   - sodium depletion
   - renal dysfunction
   - NSAIDs
   - ACE inhibitors
   - angiotensin II receptor blockers (AII receptor blockers)
   - thiazide diuretics
5. Recommend dose adjustment or temporary hold if toxicity or accumulation is suspected.

### Common pitfalls

- interpreting a random lithium level as if it were a 12-hour level
- starting NSAIDs, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, or AII receptor blockers without follow-up monitoring
- missing dehydration or AKI as a driver of toxicity
- failing to educate the patient about toxicity symptoms

### Toxicity monitoring

Watch for:

- nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea
- tremor
- ataxia
- confusion
- sedation
- coarse tremor
- seizures in severe toxicity

Escalate urgently if clinical toxicity is suspected, regardless of whether the level is pending.

## 6. Documentation Template

### TDM i-Vent / Progress Note Skeleton

**Reason for review:** TDM review for [medicine] in context of [indication].

**Data:** Current regimen [dose/route/interval]. Last dose [date/time]. Level [value] collected [date/time], approximately [x] hours after dose / pre-dose. Renal function [SCr/eGFR/CrCl trend]. Relevant factors: [missed doses, renal function change, interacting medicines, nephrotoxins, clinical status].

**Assessment:** Level appears [valid/invalid] for interpretation. Exposure is [below target/within target/above target/toxic] for [target]. Clinical context suggests [continue/adjust/hold/stop] therapy.

**Plan:** Recommend [new dose/interval/hold/continue]. Repeat level [timing]. Monitor [renal panel, SCr, urine output, toxicity symptoms, clinical response]. Communicated to [MRP/team/nursing] on [date/time].

## 7. Safety and Escalation

Escalate promptly when any of the following apply:

- suspected lithium toxicity
- rapidly rising SCr or oliguria while receiving nephrotoxic TDM medicines
- aminoglycoside accumulation or ototoxicity symptoms
- vancomycin overexposure with AKI
- invalid level leading to high-risk uncertainty in a critically ill patient
- serious infection with likely underexposure
