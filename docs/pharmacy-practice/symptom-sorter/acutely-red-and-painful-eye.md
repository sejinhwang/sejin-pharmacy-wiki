# Acutely Red And Painful Eye

## Overview

An acutely red and painful eye is sight-threatening until proven otherwise. Assess visual acuity, corneal clarity, pupil reaction, and fluorescein staining when possible.

Same-day specialist assessment is needed for suspected acute glaucoma, keratitis or corneal ulcer, iritis, orbital cellulitis, chemical injury, and penetrating injury.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Conjunctivitis
- Iritis or anterior uveitis
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma
- Keratitis or corneal ulcer
- Corneal abrasion or foreign body

### Occasional

- Episcleritis or scleritis
- Dry eye
- Trauma, contusion, penetrating injury, chemical burn, or ultraviolet arc eye
- Orbital cellulitis

### Rare But Important

- Carotico-cavernous fistula
- Gout or granulomatous disease affecting the eye
- Onchocerciasis
- Ocular tumour

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about onset, progression, pain, photophobia, discharge, reduced vision, halos, nausea, headache, contact lens use, and trauma.
- Ask specifically about high-velocity foreign body, chemical exposure, ultraviolet exposure, autoimmune disease, fever, and sinus symptoms.
- Check visual acuity in each eye.
- Assess pupils, cornea, fluorescein uptake, ciliary injection, diffuse redness, and eyelid eversion for foreign body.
- Look for proptosis, fever, pain on eye movement, and restricted eye movements.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Conjunctivitis | Iritis | Acute Glaucoma | Keratitis | Abrasion/Foreign Body |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Discharge | Yes | No | No | Possible | Possible |
| Reduced vision | No | Yes | Yes | Possible | Possible |
| Circumcorneal redness | No | Yes | Possible | Possible | No |
| Abnormal pupil | No | Possible | Yes | No | No |
| Hazy cornea or corneal staining | No | No | Yes | Possible | Possible |

## Investigations

- Primary care investigations are limited; the key checks are acuity, pupil, cornea, fluorescein, and trauma history.
- Swabs are rarely needed acutely unless severe infection or unusual discharge is present.
- Blood tests for inflammatory disease are usually not first-line in the acute presentation.
- Intraocular pressure measurement and slit-lamp assessment are specialist or equipped-clinic assessments.

## Red Flags

- Reduced visual acuity.
- Severe pain, headache, nausea, halos, or a fixed mid-dilated pupil.
- Photophobia with ciliary injection.
- Hazy cornea, corneal ulcer, or suspected herpetic keratitis.
- Contact lens wearer with painful red eye.
- Chemical injury or high-velocity foreign body.
- Proptosis, fever, painful eye movements, or restricted eye movements.
- Any uncertainty about glaucoma, keratitis, iritis, or penetrating injury.

## Top Tips

- Refer if there is serious doubt.
- Do not use mydriatics in an undiagnosed red eye.
- Do not use steroid eye drops unless directed by a specialist and herpetic keratitis has been excluded.
- After foreign body removal, review within 24 to 48 hours if symptoms persist or risk is higher.
