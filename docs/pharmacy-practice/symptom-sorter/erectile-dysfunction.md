# Erectile Dysfunction

## Overview

Erectile dysfunction is the partial or complete inability to achieve or maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual activity. It is common, often multifactorial, and may be raised indirectly.

Key tasks are to distinguish psychogenic, relationship, vascular, endocrine, neurologic, and medicine-related contributors, while recognising ED as a possible marker of cardiometabolic disease.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Depression or anxiety
- Excess alcohol
- Relationship or psychosexual dysfunction
- Vascular disease
- Iatrogenic causes, including prostate cancer treatment, antihypertensives, and antidepressants

### Occasional

- Testosterone deficiency
- Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
- Pelvic, spinal, or penile trauma
- Phimosis or tight frenulum
- Smoking
- Peyronie's disease
- Drug misuse

### Rare But Important

- Spinal cord compression
- Corpus cavernosum thrombosis
- Multiple sclerosis or other neurological disease
- Pituitary disease

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Persistent versus intermittent, situational versus generalised.
- Erections with masturbation, morning erections, and nocturnal erections.
- Libido, arousal, mood, relationship context, and stressors.
- Claudication and cardiovascular risk factors.
- Saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention or incontinence, leg weakness, or numbness.
- Galactorrhoea, headache, visual change, alcohol, recreational drugs, and medicines.

### Examination

- Cardiovascular assessment and peripheral pulses.
- Focused genital examination when indicated.
- Basic neurological screen when symptoms or red flags suggest it.

## Investigations

- Urinalysis.
- HbA1c or blood glucose.
- FBC, LFTs, lipid profile.
- Testosterone, prolactin, FSH, LH, and TSH when endocrine disease is possible.
- Specialist tests include penile Doppler, intracavernosal prostaglandin testing, vascular imaging, or MRI when indicated.

## Red Flags

- Sudden ED with saddle anaesthesia and sphincter disturbance.
- ED with leg weakness or urinary retention.
- Early morning headache or visual disturbance.
- Predictable loss of erection after a consistent time, suggesting organic disease.
- Cardiovascular risk factors needing assessment.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Review antihypertensives, antidepressants, prostate cancer therapies, alcohol, opioids, and recreational drugs.
- Do not assume anxiety is the cause; it may be the consequence.
- ED is an opportunity to assess diabetes, lipids, blood pressure, smoking, and exercise.

## Related Conditions

Diabetes, cardiovascular disease, anxiety and depression, medicine/substance effects, neurological red flags, endocrine disease, weight change, and infertility can all intersect with ED.
