# Hand And Wrist Pain

## Overview

Hand and wrist pain is often attributed to arthritis, but the differential also includes nerve entrapment, tendon disease, trauma, infection, and referred cervical root pain.

Distinguish mechanical or degenerative pain from inflammatory arthritis and neurological entrapment.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Osteoarthritis, especially first carpometacarpal, DIP, and PIP joints
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Trauma, including sprain, scaphoid fracture, and Colles fracture
- Rheumatoid arthritis, often symmetric MCP, PIP, and wrist involvement
- Tenosynovitis, including De Quervain tenosynovitis

### Occasional

- Ganglion
- Gout
- Raynaud phenomenon
- Paronychia, whitlow, and other local infection
- Repetitive strain injury
- Trigger finger
- Ulnar nerve entrapment
- Cervical root pain
- Complex regional pain syndrome

### Rare But Important

- Infected eczema
- Writer's cramp
- Peripheral neuropathy
- Dupuytren contracture
- Diabetic arthropathy
- Osteomyelitis
- Kienbock disease

## Clinical Assessment

- Localise the pain: radial, ulnar, palmar, dorsal, joint-specific, or tendon-line.
- Clarify quality: burning, tingling, aching, stiffness, sharp catching, locking, or instability.
- Ask about night waking, shaking the hand for relief, morning stiffness longer than 30 minutes, overuse, trauma, grip, and fine motor tasks.
- Examine swelling, bruising, deformity, wasting, and skin infection.
- Check snuffbox tenderness, first CMC tenderness or grind, boggy synovitis, tendon movement, range of movement, Phalen/Tinel tests, and Finkelstein test.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Osteoarthritis | Carpal Tunnel | Trauma | Rheumatoid Arthritis | Tenosynovitis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Numbness or tingling | No | Lateral 3.5 digits | Possible | No | No |
| Night symptoms | Possible | Common | Possible | Possible | Possible |
| Morning stiffness | Brief | No | No | Longer than 30 minutes | Possible |
| Swelling | Hard or bony | No | Bruising or local swelling | Soft or boggy | Local or linear |
| Trauma or overuse clue | No | Possible | Yes | No | Possible |

## Investigations

- X-ray is used for trauma, suspected fracture, and arthritis assessment.
- FBC, ESR, CRP, rheumatoid factor, and anti-CCP are selected when inflammatory arthritis is possible.
- Serum uric acid may support gout assessment outside the acute attack.
- Nerve conduction studies help confirm carpal tunnel or ulnar entrapment.
- Ultrasound can assess tendons, ganglia, and synovitis.
- MRI is used for occult fracture, scaphoid injury, Kienbock disease, or ligament injury.

## Red Flags

- Snuffbox tenderness after injury: treat as scaphoid fracture and immobilise even if initial X-ray is normal.
- Hot, swollen, very painful joint: septic arthritis or crystal arthritis needs urgent assessment.
- Neurovascular compromise after injury.
- Rapidly progressive deformity or loss of function.
- Deep infection, flexor sheath infection, or suspected osteomyelitis.

## Top Tips

- Carpal tunnel syndrome commonly wakes patients at night and they may shake the hand for relief.
- De Quervain pain is radial-sided and worsens with thumb movement or lifting a baby.
- Trigger finger causes catching, locking, or a palpable nodule.
- Dupuytren disease starts with painless palmar nodules or skin pitting before contracture.
