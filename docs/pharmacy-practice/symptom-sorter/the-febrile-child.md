# The Febrile Child

## Overview

Fever in a child is common and usually infectious, but serious illness may begin with fever alone. The key is to distinguish the well child from the ill child, assess dehydration, identify sepsis or meningitis features, look for a source, and safety-net clearly.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Viral URTI
- Gastroenteritis
- Otitis media
- Tonsillitis
- Chest infection

### Occasional

- UTI
- Bronchiolitis
- Croup
- Viral exanthems
- Appendicitis
- Cellulitis or abscess
- Glandular fever
- Post-immunisation fever

### Rare But Important

- Meningitis or meningococcal septicaemia
- Encephalitis
- Hepatitis
- HIV/AIDS
- Measles or rubella
- Epiglottitis
- Tuberculosis
- Malaria or other travel-related infection
- Septic arthritis or osteomyelitis
- Kawasaki disease

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about age, fever duration, intake, wet nappies or urine output, vomiting, diarrhoea, alertness, consolability, tone, cough, work of breathing, ear pain, sore throat, dysuria, abdominal pain, rash, immunisation, travel, and contacts.
- Examine appearance, interaction, capillary refill, hydration, respiratory rate, ENT, chest, abdomen, skin, joints, and limbs.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | URTI | Gastroenteritis | Otitis media | Tonsillitis | Chest infection |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Neck nodes | Possible | No | Possible | Yes | Possible |
| Significant pain | No | Possible | Yes | Yes | Possible |
| Chest signs | No | No | No | No | Yes |
| Diarrhoea | Possible | Yes | Possible | Possible | Possible |
| Cough | Yes | No | Possible | No | Yes |

## Investigations

- Often none in primary care.
- Urinalysis and urine culture are useful when UTI is possible.
- If unwell enough for blood tests, CXR, cultures, or lumbar puncture, admission is often appropriate.

## Red Flags

- Reduced urine output, prolonged capillary refill, or poor appearance.
- Floppy or drowsy infant or child.
- Irritable and inconsolable infant.
- Petechial rash.
- Temperature 38 C or higher in infants under 3 months.
- Temperature 39 C or higher in infants 3 to 6 months.

## Pharmacist Notes

- Confirm weight-based antipyretic dosing.
- Avoid duplicate paracetamol or ibuprofen products.
- Safety-net: deterioration despite antipyretics matters more than fever number alone.
- Document telephone advice, thresholds, and plan B clearly.
