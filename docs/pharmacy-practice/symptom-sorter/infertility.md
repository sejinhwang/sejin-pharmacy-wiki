# Infertility

## Overview

Most couples conceive within the first year of trying, and a further proportion conceive in the second year. Infertility is typically defined as failure to conceive after 2 years of regular unprotected intercourse, but earlier assessment is appropriate when age or risk factors are present.

Assess the couple as a unit; male and female factors are both common.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Unexplained infertility
- Male factor infertility
- Anovulation or ovulatory dysfunction
- Tubal factor infertility
- Endometriosis

### Occasional

- Hostile cervical mucus
- PCOS
- Amenorrhoea or hypomenorrhoea causes
- Uterine pathology, including fibroids or polyps
- Cervical inflammation, polyps, or stenosis
- Systemic illness such as anaemia or thyroid disease

### Rare But Important

- Congenital malformations of reproductive tract
- Sexual dysfunction
- Adrenocortical tumour
- Chromosomal abnormality
- Endometrial tuberculosis

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Duration trying and frequency/timing of intercourse.
- Previous pregnancies for either partner.
- Menstrual cycle regularity, amenorrhoea, oligomenorrhoea, dysmenorrhoea, and menorrhagia.
- PID, STI history, pelvic surgery, or endometriosis.
- Male STI, testicular problems, urogenital history, and varicocoele.
- Thyroid symptoms, systemic illness, smoking, alcohol, weight, and extremes of exercise.

### Examination

- Female pelvic examination when indicated.
- Look for PCOS, thyroid disease, pelvic mass, or fibroids.
- Male examination when varicocoele or testicular abnormality is possible.

## Investigations

- Semen analysis, repeated if initial sample is suboptimal.
- Serum progesterone 7 days before expected menses to confirm ovulation.
- FBC, FSH, LH, prolactin, TFTs, U&E, and testosterone when menses are irregular, amenorrhoea is present, or systemic symptoms exist.
- Specialist tests include pelvic ultrasound, tubal patency testing, laparoscopy and dye, and male endocrine or testicular imaging when indicated.

## Earlier Referral Triggers

- Female age over 35.
- Amenorrhoea.
- History of pelvic surgery or PID.
- Abnormal pelvic examination.
- Male urogenital problems, STI history, or varicocoele.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Review medicines with reproductive effects in both partners.
- Give preconception advice, including folic acid and rubella immunity where relevant.
- Avoid unnecessary hormone testing when cycles are normal except ovulation confirmation according to pathway.

## Related Conditions

Irregular periods, thyroid disease, low energy availability, obesity, PCOS, chronic disease, erectile dysfunction, genital infection, PID, and rare genital tuberculosis can contribute.
