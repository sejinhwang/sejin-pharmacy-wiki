# Anal Itching

## Overview

Anal itching, or pruritus ani, is common and usually benign. Patients may have already tried self-treatment, and some topical products or hygiene routines can worsen irritation.

The main tasks are to find an obvious local cause, screen for red flags, and review irritants and hygiene practices.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Fungal infection, including tinea or candidiasis
- Threadworms
- Haemorrhoids
- Perianal skin tags
- Anal fissure

### Occasional

- Poor hygiene or over-washing
- Chronic diarrhoea, faecal seepage, or faecal incontinence
- Perianal warts
- Streptococcal perianal infection in children
- Trauma from anal intercourse or foreign body insertion
- Psoriasis
- Diabetes with recurrent infection
- Chemical irritation from soaps, wipes, lubricants, bubble baths, topical products, or spicy foods
- Anorectal carcinoma

### Rare But Important

- Lichen sclerosus
- Crohn's perianal disease
- Rectovaginal fistula
- Rectal prolapse
- Sexually transmitted infections
- Serious causes of generalised pruritus

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Onset, duration, and whether symptoms are worse at night.
- Bowel symptoms: diarrhoea, constipation, leakage, bleeding, discharge, and pain on defaecation.
- Skin symptoms: soreness, rash, scaling, fissuring, or excoriation.
- Hygiene and irritants: over-washing, perfumed soaps, wipes, bubble baths, and topical products.
- Risk factors: diabetes, immunocompromise, recent antibiotics.
- Sexual history when STI risk is relevant.

### Examination

- Inspect for erythema, excoriation, warts, fissure, tags, haemorrhoids, and suspicious lesions.
- Consider digital rectal examination unless pain prevents it.
- If too painful, treat symptoms and arrange follow-up so the examination can be completed.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Fungal infection | Threadworms | Haemorrhoids | Skin tags | Fissure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rectal bleeding | No | Possible | Possible | No | Yes |
| Worse at night | Possible | Yes | Possible | Possible | No |
| Lump sensation | No | No | Possible | Possible | No |
| Rectal discharge | No | No | Possible | No | Possible |
| Painful defaecation | No | No | Possible | No | Yes |

## Investigations

- Usually none if an obvious benign cause responds to treatment.
- Skin swab if local infection is suspected.
- FBC, ESR, or CRP if IBD is suspected.
- HbA1c or fasting glucose in recurrent or prolonged cases.
- Proctoscopy when rectal pathology is suspected and within scope.

## Red Flags

- Suspicious anal lesion or mass.
- Persistent rectal bleeding, especially with bowel habit change or weight loss.
- Persistent rectal discharge.
- Features of lichen sclerosus or treatment failure.
- Severe pain preventing examination with no clear benign cause.
- Significant immunocompromise.
- STI risk with warts, ulcers, or discharge.

## Management Notes

- Stop irritants and simplify hygiene.
- Treat constipation, diarrhoea, or faecal seepage when they are the driver.
- Avoid prolonged unsupervised topical steroid use.
- Perianal warts should prompt sexual health assessment and contact tracing where appropriate.

## Related Conditions

Haemorrhoids, fissures, threadworms, fungal infection, irritant dermatitis, chronic diarrhoea, STI-related proctitis, Crohn's disease, malignancy, and anal swelling can all overlap with pruritus ani.
