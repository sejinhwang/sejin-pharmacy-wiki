# Jaundice In Adults

## Overview

Adults rarely present saying they are turning yellow; jaundice is often noticed by someone else or on examination.

Assessment aims to determine whether jaundice is pre-hepatic, hepatocellular, or cholestatic/obstructive, while urgently identifying pancreatic malignancy, hepatic failure, sepsis, and cholangitis.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Gallstones in the common bile duct
- Viral hepatitis, including EBV, hepatitis A, B, or C
- Carcinoma of the head of pancreas
- Hepatic carcinoma or metastases
- Alcohol-related cirrhosis

### Occasional

- Alcoholic hepatitis
- Primary biliary cholangitis
- Medicine or toxin-induced jaundice, including chlorpromazine, isoniazid, anabolic steroids, methyldopa, or paracetamol overdose
- Haemolytic anaemia
- Venous congestion from heart failure or constrictive pericarditis
- Cholangitis and biliary stricture
- Pancreatitis

### Rare But Important

- Cholangiocarcinoma
- Leptospirosis
- Rotor, Dubin-Johnson, and Mirizzi syndromes
- Cholestasis or fatty liver of pregnancy
- Gilbert's syndrome
- Wilson's disease
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
- Haemochromatosis
- Amyloidosis

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Time course: sudden onset suggests hepatitis or obstruction; progressive painless jaundice suggests malignancy.
- Pale stools, dark urine, pruritus, and right upper quadrant pain.
- Fever or rigors, which raise concern for cholangitis.
- Weight loss and anorexia.
- Recent travel and contact with travellers.
- IV drug use and relevant sexual history.
- Recent medicines, supplements, toxins, alcohol intake, and paracetamol overdose risk.

### Examination

- Inspect sclera and skin.
- Assess right upper quadrant tenderness.
- Look for palpable gallbladder, which suggests malignant obstruction.
- Check for hepatomegaly or a hard knobbly liver.
- Assess mental state: restlessness, poor concentration, or drowsiness can suggest hepatic encephalopathy.

## Investigations

### Likely First-Line

- Urinalysis
- FBC
- Liver function tests
- Hepatitis serology

### Case-Dependent

- Ultrasound of liver, gallbladder, biliary tree, and pancreas.
- Antimitochondrial antibody if primary biliary cholangitis is suspected.

### Specialist Or Second-Line

- Amylase or lipase if pancreatitis is suspected.
- ERCP when obstruction requires diagnosis or therapy.
- Liver biopsy in selected cases.

## Interpretation Tips

- Urine bilirubin suggests conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia.
- Markedly raised ALP with bilirubin suggests cholestasis or obstruction.
- Markedly raised ALT or AST suggests hepatocellular injury.
- Macrocytosis may reflect alcohol use.
- Anaemia with reticulocytosis can suggest haemolysis.

## Red Flags

- Painless progressive jaundice, especially with weight loss.
- Enlarged hard knobbly liver.
- Restlessness, poor concentration, or drowsiness.
- Fever with right upper quadrant pain and jaundice.
- Suspected paracetamol overdose or acute liver failure.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Always consider iatrogenic causes.
- Jaundice may imply impaired hepatic function, so use caution with prescribing and avoid hepatotoxic medicines where possible.
- Ask specifically about paracetamol dose, timing, alcohol, OTC products, and recent medicine starts.

## Related Conditions

### Cholestatic Pruritus

Cholestasis commonly causes severe generalised itching, sometimes before visible jaundice. Pruritus with pale stools and dark urine supports an obstructive or cholestatic pattern.

### Weight Loss

Painless progressive jaundice with weight loss suggests pancreatic or biliary malignancy and requires urgent referral rather than watchful waiting.

### Cholangitis

Fever or rigors with right upper quadrant pain and jaundice is a sepsis-risk pattern requiring urgent assessment, antibiotics, and source control.

### Viral Hepatitis

Viral hepatitis is a common hepatocellular cause of jaundice. Exposure history and hepatitis serology guide diagnosis.

### Alcohol-Related Liver Disease

Alcohol-related hepatitis or cirrhosis can cause jaundice and decompensation. Jaundice in cirrhosis may signal worsening synthetic function.

### Hepatic Encephalopathy

Confusion, drowsiness, or behavioural change with jaundice is an emergency escalation trigger.

### Hepatobiliary Malignancy

Hepatic, biliary, or pancreatic malignancy can cause jaundice through obstruction or liver failure. Hepatomegaly, hard liver, systemic symptoms, or progressive painless jaundice needs urgent imaging and referral.
