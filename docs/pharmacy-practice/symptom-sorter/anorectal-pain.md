# Anorectal Pain

## Overview

Anorectal pain is often severe and distressing. Sphincter spasm can lead to constipation, which then worsens pain.

Examination may be difficult, but careful visual inspection can identify many common causes even when DRE is not tolerated.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Anal fissure
- Thrombosed haemorrhoid or perianal haematoma
- Perianal abscess
- Proctalgia fugax
- Anorectal malignancy

### Occasional

- Levator ani syndrome
- Crohn's disease
- Coccydynia
- Descending perineum syndrome
- Prostatitis
- Ovarian cyst or tumour
- Solitary rectal ulcer syndrome

### Rare But Important

- Anal tuberculosis
- Cauda equina lesion
- Endometriosis
- Trauma
- Presacral tumour

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Whether pain began during defaecation.
- Constant pain versus fleeting severe episodes.
- Rectal bleeding or discharge.
- Fever or systemic upset.
- Constipation or stool avoidance.
- Bowel habit change, weight loss, recurrent perianal disease, diabetes symptoms, immunocompromise, pelvic symptoms, or urinary symptoms.

### Examination

- Inspect for fissure, thrombosed haemorrhoid, haematoma, abscess, and suspicious ulcerating lesions.
- DRE can be very painful; proceed only when tolerated.
- Some abscesses have minimal external swelling early.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Fissure | Haematoma | Abscess | Proctalgia fugax | Malignancy |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Began while defaecating | Yes | No | No | Possible | No |
| Visible swelling | No | Yes | Possible | No | Possible |
| Intermittent pain | Possible | No | No | Yes | No |
| Rectal bleeding | Yes | Possible | Possible | No | Yes |
| DRE excruciating | Yes | Yes | Yes | Possible | Possible |

## Investigations

- None if the diagnosis is clear and benign.
- FBC, ESR, CRP, faecal calprotectin, or proctoscopy when IBD, infection, or malignancy is suspected.
- Urinalysis if prostatitis or urinary pathology is possible.
- Pelvic ultrasound when pelvic mass is found.

## Red Flags

- Fever or systemic upset.
- Severe pain with minimal external findings.
- Weight loss or bowel habit change.
- Persistent rectal bleeding not consistent with fissure.
- Florid or recurrent perianal disease.
- Intractable constant pain with normal examination.

## Management Notes

- Treat pain but also address constipation.
- Fleeting severe pain with normal exam suggests proctalgia fugax.
- Urgent surgical assessment is needed for abscess, deep infection, strangulation, or malignancy concern.

## Related Conditions

Anal fissure, thrombosed haemorrhoid, perianal abscess, proctitis, Crohn's disease, malignancy, and constipation are key linked pathways.
