# Episodic Loss Of Consciousness

## Overview

Terminology is often imprecise: patients may say syncope, faint, blackout, fit, collapse, or seizure. Episodic loss of consciousness can occur at any age and is more common in older adults.

The key triage aims are to distinguish syncope, seizure, metabolic or toxic causes, and cardiac syncope. An eyewitness account is often the most useful history.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Vasovagal syncope
- Paroxysmal arrhythmia, including Stokes-Adams attacks, sinus bradycardia, and SVT
- Epilepsy
- Hypoglycaemia
- Orthostatic hypotension

### Occasional

- Structural cardiac lesion, such as aortic stenosis, hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy, or pulmonary stenosis
- Micturition syncope or cough syncope
- Sleep apnoea
- Valsalva-induced syncope
- Functional seizures

### Rare But Important

- Narcolepsy
- Carotid sinus syncope
- Hyperventilation
- Subclavian steal syndrome

## Clinical Assessment

- Clarify posture, exertion, triggers, micturition, cough, Valsalva, and whether there was any warning.
- Ask about prodrome: light-headedness, nausea, sweating, flushing, palpitations, breathlessness, chest pain, headache, or focal neurological symptoms.
- Get an eyewitness description of pallor, flushing, abnormal movements, tongue biting, incontinence, eyes closed throughout, and injury.
- Ask about recovery: rapid recovery suggests syncope; prolonged confusion suggests seizure or metabolic cause.
- Check family history of sudden cardiac death, known heart disease, murmur, diabetes, and medicines.
- Examine vitals including postural blood pressure, cardiovascular signs, and a neurological screen.

| Sweating | Yes | No | No | Yes | Possible |

## Investigations

- FBC and ECG are common first-line checks, especially in older adults.
- Capillary glucose is useful during or immediately after an episode.
- Ambulatory ECG monitoring is used when intermittent arrhythmia is suspected.
- EEG and CT or MRI brain are specialist investigations when epilepsy or structural neurological disease is suspected.
- Echocardiography is used when a structural cardiac lesion is possible.
- Tilt-table testing may help in unexplained syncope or vasovagal susceptibility.

## Red Flags

- Syncope during exertion.
- Family history of sudden cardiac death.
- Abnormal ECG or known heart disease.
- Suspected Stokes-Adams attack: patient appears lifeless, then becomes markedly flushed on recovery.
- New aortic stenosis murmur.
- Syncope triggered by neck pressure or head movement, suggesting carotid sinus syncope.
- Chest pain, hypoxia, haemodynamic instability, severe headache, or persistent focal neurology.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Review antihypertensives, diuretics, vasodilators, QT-prolonging medicines, and glucose-lowering medicines.
- Orthostatic hypotension is often multifactorial in older adults.
- Document driving and safety-sensitive work implications and ensure escalation when cardiac or seizure causes are possible.

## Related Conditions

- Epilepsy and seizures
- Palpitations and arrhythmia-related syncope
- Chest pain and acute shortness of breath
- Dizziness and orthostatic hypotension
- Hypoglycaemia
- Stroke and TIA
- Falls risk
