# Diarrhoea

## Overview

Diarrhoea is the passage of abnormally liquid and frequent stools. Patients may use the term diarrhoea to mean frequent stools that are still formed, so clarify what they mean.

Diarrhoea is usually considered chronic if it lasts more than 2 weeks.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Acute infective gastroenteritis
- Antibiotics and other medicine side effects
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Diverticulitis
- Overflow constipation, especially in older adults

### Occasional

- Lactose intolerance
- Chronic infection, including amoebiasis, giardiasis, and hookworm
- Bowel neoplasia
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Excess alcohol
- Toddler diarrhoea
- Coeliac disease

### Rare But Important

- Appendicitis
- Laxative misuse
- Thyrotoxicosis
- Malabsorption
- Allergy
- Ovarian cancer

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Clarify whether this is truly watery stool or increased frequency.
- Ask about nocturnal diarrhoea; IBS rarely causes nocturnal diarrhoea.
- Ask about blood or mucus.
- Duration: acute, persistent over 1 week, or chronic over 2 weeks.
- Pattern: steady improvement versus non-improving or worsening.
- Fever, vomiting, constant abdominal pain, weight loss, thirst, dizziness, or reduced urine.
- Foreign travel, food-handling occupation, and sick contacts.
- Antibiotic exposure, laxative use, ACE inhibitor use, and dehydration or AKI risk.
- In older adults, consider overflow diarrhoea and confirm with rectal examination when appropriate.

### Examination

- Hydration status, especially in infants and older adults.
- Abdominal tenderness.
- Constant severe pain needs in-person assessment to exclude acute surgical pathology.
- Rectal examination may be needed in older adults to assess faecal loading and overflow.

## Investigations

### Likely If Persistent

- Stool specimen
- FBC
- ESR and CRP
- Thyroid function tests
- Coeliac serology
- Faecal calprotectin

### Case-Dependent

- Urinalysis; high specific gravity may suggest dehydration.
- Liver function tests.
- Proctoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, colonoscopy, or specialist bowel evaluation.
- CA-125 in women age 50 or older if ovarian cancer needs exclusion.

### Interpretation Pearls

- Acute diarrhoea lasting more than a week usually needs one stool sample for common infections.
- Salmonella requires follow-up to show clearance.
- Chronic diarrhoea may need three daily stool samples for ova, cysts, and parasites.
- FBC may show anaemia in malignancy or coeliac disease, raised inflammatory markers in IBD or infection, and raised white cell count in IBD or infection.
- Faecal calprotectin helps rule out IBD.

## Red Flags

- Weight loss with chronic diarrhoea
- Dehydration in infants or older adults
- Constant, non-colicky abdominal pain
- New IBS diagnosis in middle-aged or older adults
- Suspected overflow diarrhoea in an older adult
- Acute diarrhoea in older adults at AKI risk, especially while taking ACE inhibitors

## Top Tips

- Clarify what the patient means by diarrhoea.
- Gastroenteritis should steadily improve after a few days, although symptoms can persist up to 10 days.
- Giardiasis may be difficult to isolate from stool specimens; consider the clinical picture after travel with persistent fatty diarrhoea, anorexia, nausea, and bloating.
- Ask about travel and occupation because both affect differential diagnosis and management.
- IBS rarely causes nocturnal diarrhoea.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Review antibiotics, metformin, magnesium products, SSRIs, laxatives, and recent medicine changes.
- In dehydration risk, review ACE inhibitors, diuretics, NSAIDs, SGLT2 inhibitors, and other medicines that may need temporary holding according to local sick-day guidance.
- Avoid antimotility agents when invasive infection, C. difficile, severe colitis, or significant systemic illness is suspected.

## Related Conditions

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease

IBD can present with chronic or recurrent diarrhoea, urgency, abdominal pain, blood, and mucus.

Bloody diarrhoea, nocturnal diarrhoea, weight loss, fever, systemic toxicity, or raised inflammatory markers should trigger faecal calprotectin and specialist pathway rather than symptomatic treatment alone.

### Colorectal Cancer

Colorectal neoplasia can cause chronic diarrhoea, alternating bowel habit, bleeding, anaemia, and weight loss.

New bowel habit change in older adults, iron deficiency anaemia, rectal bleeding, or weight loss requires investigation rather than an IBS label.

### Infectious And Travellers' Diarrhoea

Acute watery diarrhoea is usually infectious. Persistent diarrhoea after travel shifts the differential toward protozoa and post-infectious syndromes.

Severe illness, fever, blood, dehydration, or diarrhoea lasting 14 days or more after travel requires targeted evaluation.

### Medicine-Induced Diarrhoea

Antibiotics, metformin, magnesium, SSRIs, and other medicines frequently cause diarrhoea.

Persisting diarrhoea with systemic features should not be attributed to side effects without excluding infection, C. difficile, or IBD.

### C. Difficile Infection

C. difficile is a key cause of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea and can be severe in older adults or people with comorbidity.

New diarrhoea after antibiotics or healthcare exposure warrants prompt testing.

### Dehydration And AKI Risk

Diarrhoea can cause fluid and electrolyte loss and precipitate prerenal AKI.

Older adults, infants, and people on ACE inhibitors or diuretics are higher risk. Oral rehydration and appropriate sick-day medicine holds can reduce harm.

### Constipation With Overflow

Faecal impaction can present as frequent loose stool and be mistaken for diarrhoea.

In older adults, rectal examination is an important decision point.

### Vomiting And Gastroenteritis

Viral gastroenteritis commonly causes both vomiting and diarrhoea. Main harms are dehydration and electrolyte disturbance.

Persistent vomiting, severe pain, blood-stained emesis, or inability to keep fluids down warrants escalation.

### Acute Abdominal Pain And Surgical Mimics

Appendicitis, diverticulitis, and intra-abdominal sepsis can present with diarrhoea.

Marked tenderness, guarding, constant severe pain, peritonism, or systemic toxicity requires urgent assessment and imaging.
