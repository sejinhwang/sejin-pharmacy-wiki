# Abnormal Nails

## Overview

Abnormal nails are more often presented for cosmetic reasons, but nail changes may also reveal significant systemic, dermatological, vascular, or malignant disease.

The commonest diagnostic tension is psoriasis versus fungal infection. Nail clippings are often needed before lengthy antifungal treatment.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Psoriasis
- Fungal nail infection
- Trauma to the nail bed
- Nail biting and hangnail trauma
- Onychogryphosis

### Occasional

- Beau's lines after severe illness
- Hand eczema
- Longitudinal ridging
- Chronic paronychia
- Clubbing
- Autoimmune disease, including alopecia areata and lichen planus
- Koilonychia from iron deficiency or Plummer-Vinson syndrome

### Rare But Important

- Subungual melanoma
- Leuconychia from liver disease or diabetes
- Lamellar nail dystrophy
- Yellow nail syndrome
- Exfoliative dermatitis with nail shedding
- Nail dystrophy from poor circulation, including Raynaud disease
- Epidermolysis bullosa

## Clinical Assessment

- Examine beyond the nails: look for psoriasis plaques, tinea corporis, eczema, alopecia, vascular disease, and systemic clues.
- Ask about trauma, biting, occupation, wet work, footwear, diabetes, circulation, lung symptoms, systemic illness, and previous severe illness 2 to 3 months earlier.
- Assess symmetry, pitting, thickening, friability, onycholysis, pigment, nail fold involvement, and periungual inflammation.

## Investigations

- Nail clippings for mycology help distinguish psoriatic nail dystrophy from onychomycosis.
- Urinalysis is worthwhile if nails are unusually white and systemic disease is possible.
- FBC may confirm iron deficiency in koilonychia.
- Liver function tests are selected for leuconychia or liver disease concern.
- Chest X-ray is considered when clubbing or yellow nails occur with chest symptoms.

## Red Flags

- Possible subungual melanoma: nail destruction, pigment extending onto the nail fold, or longitudinal pigmented band.
- Clubbing with signs of major pulmonary or cardiac disease.
- Crumbly white nails should not automatically be treated as fungus without confirmation when treatment is long or risky.
- Severely bitten nails may reflect significant anxiety or compulsive behaviour.

## Top Tips

- Patients often worry about vitamin or calcium deficiency; these are rarely the explanation for nail dystrophy.
- Beau's lines appear months after the precipitating illness, so look back in the history.
- Fungal infection is often asymmetrical; psoriasis is more often symmetrical and may show pitting.

## Related Conditions

- Psoriasis
- Onychomycosis
- Contact dermatitis and hand eczema
- Anaemia and fatigue
- Diabetes mellitus and infection risk
- Itching and secondary skin infection
