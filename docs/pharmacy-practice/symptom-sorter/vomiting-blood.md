# Vomiting Blood

## Overview

Vomiting blood, or haematemesis, ranges from minor streaking to life-threatening upper GI haemorrhage. Treat it as urgent and be prepared for rapid deterioration.

Blood retained in the stomach may appear dark and granular, often described as coffee-ground vomit.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Peptic ulcer disease or acute gastritis
- Mallory-Weiss tear
- Oesophageal varices, usually from cirrhosis
- Oesophageal or gastric malignancy
- GORD

### Occasional

- Swallowed blood, such as epistaxis
- Haemoptysis mistaken for vomiting
- Foreign body or mediastinal tumour eroding the oesophagus or aorta
- Haemobilia
- Ingested poisons, including corrosive acid, alkali, or arsenic
- Blood dyscrasias such as thrombocytopenia, leukaemia, haemophilia, or aplastic anaemia

### Rare But Important

- Oesophageal rupture after severe vomiting or trauma
- Factitious disorder
- Gallstone perforation of duodenum
- Scurvy
- Polyarteritis nodosa or SLE

## Clinical Assessment

### Immediate Priorities

- Distinguish haematemesis from haemoptysis.
- Ask the patient to keep the vomitus if safe.
- Assess colour and volume: bright red versus coffee-ground.
- Ask about ongoing bleeding, dizziness, syncope, and melaena.
- Ask about NSAIDs, antiplatelets, anticoagulants, steroids, alcohol, cirrhosis, prior ulcer, or prior GI bleed.

### Examination

- Vital signs; tachycardia may be the earliest sign of shock.
- Postural blood pressure if safe.
- Cool peripheries, delayed capillary refill, and hypotension.
- Abdominal tenderness or guarding.
- Stigmata of chronic liver disease.
- Malignancy clues, such as left supraclavicular node enlargement.

## Investigations

Most investigations are performed acutely in hospital.

### Likely First-Line

- FBC, noting haemoglobin may be normal early after acute bleeding
- Urea and electrolytes
- Liver function tests including gamma-GT if alcohol or liver disease is suspected
- Coagulation profile, especially if anticoagulants are possible
- Group and screen or crossmatch

### Definitive Or Specialist

- Upper GI endoscopy
- Biopsy of suspicious lesions
- H. pylori testing if peptic ulceration is suspected

### Case-Dependent

- Erect chest or abdominal X-ray if perforation is suspected.

## Red Flags

- Shock or haemodynamic instability.
- Tachycardia, hypotension, or syncope.
- Ongoing haematemesis or large-volume bleed.
- Coffee-ground vomit or melaena.
- Suspected varices or known cirrhosis.
- Severe chest or abdominal pain.
- Frailty, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, or significant comorbidity.
- Anticoagulant use or major bleeding risk.

## Top Tips

- Patients may confuse coughing up blood with vomiting blood.
- Drug history matters: NSAIDs, steroids, anticoagulants, and antiplatelets can precipitate or worsen bleeding.
- Admit most acute cases unless the patient is perfectly well and the cause is clearly minor.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Hold antithrombotics during uncontrolled bleeding according to local pathway.
- Identify NSAID, aspirin, steroid, anticoagulant, and antiplatelet exposure early.
- Consider reversal only in appropriate major or life-threatening bleeding pathways.
- A clear restart plan is needed after haemostasis, balancing thrombosis and rebleeding risk.

## Related Conditions

### Upper GI Bleeding

Haematemesis is an upper GI bleeding presentation requiring urgent risk stratification and endoscopy planning.

Management depends on distinguishing non-variceal from variceal bleeding and recognising shock physiology.

### Peptic Ulcer Disease

PUD and erosive disease are common causes of non-variceal haematemesis. NSAID or aspirin exposure, prior ulcer, and H. pylori status are key decision points.

### Variceal Bleeding

Oesophageal or gastric varices are high-mortality causes of haematemesis, usually from cirrhosis.

Suspected variceal bleed changes immediate treatment, including vasoactive therapy and antibiotic prophylaxis according to local pathway.

### Mallory-Weiss Tear

A mucosal tear after forceful retching can cause haematemesis. Persistent bleeding or instability still requires urgent endoscopic evaluation.

### Oesophageal Rupture

Severe chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema, sepsis physiology, or rapid deterioration after vomiting are red flags requiring immediate emergency assessment.

### Aortic Catastrophe

Rare aorto-oesophageal or aortoenteric bleeding can cause massive upper GI bleeding. Sentinel bleeds, shock, back pain, or known aneurysm history require immediate surgical escalation.

### Haemoptysis

Correct source identification changes the pathway. Ask about coughing, frothy blood, nausea, and retching.

### Vomiting

Vomiting can trigger Mallory-Weiss tears and coexist with ulcer disease or gastritis. Ongoing vomiting with haematemesis usually requires admission and IV management.
