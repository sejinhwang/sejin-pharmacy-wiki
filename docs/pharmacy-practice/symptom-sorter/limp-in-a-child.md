# Limp In A Child

## Overview

A limp in a child is an infrequent but potentially serious presentation. Assessment can be challenging in an uncooperative toddler, so a methodical approach and planned follow-up are important unless the diagnosis is obvious.

The priorities are to identify septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, trauma, referred hip pain, and non-musculoskeletal causes such as appendicitis.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Trauma, including foreign body in the foot
- Irritable hip or transient synovitis
- Acute viral infection with arthralgia
- Oligoarticular juvenile idiopathic arthritis
- Slipped capital femoral epiphysis, typically over age 10

### Occasional

- Perthes disease, typically age 4 to 10
- Septic arthritis
- Idiopathic scoliosis
- Developmental dysplasia or dislocation of the hip
- Acute lower abdominal pain, especially appendicitis
- Leg-length discrepancy
- Neurological causes, including cerebral palsy

### Rare But Important

- Acute osteomyelitis
- Rheumatic fever
- Autoimmune disorders such as SLE or dermatomyositis
- Rickets
- Bone malignancy
- Duchenne muscular dystrophy

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about sudden versus gradual onset, refusal to weight-bear, pain location, trauma, fever, rigors, malaise, recent infection, morning stiffness, multiple joints, and age-specific clues.
- Remember hip disease may present as knee pain.
- Observe gait and ability to weight-bear.
- Examine from feet upward, including soles and between toes for foreign body.
- Assess ankle, knee, hip range of movement, focal bony tenderness, joint effusion, systemic illness, and abdomen when the cause is unclear.

## Investigations

- FBC, ESR or CRP, and X-ray are common first-line investigations when pathology is possible.
- Hip or pelvis imaging is guided by symptoms.
- Autoimmune screen is selected when connective tissue disease is possible.
- Calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase, creatine kinase, ASO titre, blood cultures, ultrasound, and MRI are specialist or case-dependent tests.

## Red Flags

- Fever with limp or refusal to weight-bear: urgent specialist opinion or admission to exclude septic arthritis and osteomyelitis.
- Marked restriction of movement or dramatic bony tenderness.
- Pubertal or adolescent child, often overweight, with groin pain and limp: suspect slipped capital femoral epiphysis and refer urgently.
- Abdominal pain or systemic illness presenting as limp.
- Inconsistent injury history or safeguarding concern.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Do not delay urgent assessment if infection is suspected and the child is systemically unwell.
- Review regular antipyretics that may mask fever or pain severity.
- Ensure analgesic and antipyretic dosing is weight-based.
