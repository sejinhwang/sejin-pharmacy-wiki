# Acute Confusion

## Overview

Acute confusion is delirium until proven otherwise. It is common in older adults and often presents via a concerned relative or caregiver.

The priority is to confirm that the change is acute, identify reversible causes, and decide whether home safety is realistic. Admission is common and often appropriate.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Hypoxia from respiratory or cardiac disease
- Systemic infection
- Cerebrovascular accident or TIA
- Hypoglycaemia
- Diabetic ketoacidosis

### Occasional

- Alcohol withdrawal or intoxication
- Cerebral infection
- Electrolyte imbalance or uraemia
- Iatrogenic causes, including digoxin, diuretics, steroids, and opioids
- Myxoedema
- Drug misuse

### Rare But Important

- Wernicke's encephalopathy
- Cerebral tumour
- Hypo- or hyperparathyroidism
- Cushing's disease
- Postictal state
- Carbon monoxide poisoning

## Clinical Assessment

### Immediate Priorities

- Establish acuity with collateral history.
- Clarify baseline cognition and function.
- Use an ABC approach: airway, breathing, circulation, oxygen saturation, temperature, blood pressure, and heart rate.
- Check capillary glucose early.
- Review medicines, including medicines found at home if the patient cannot give a reliable history.

### Focused History And Examination

- Screen for infection: urinary symptoms, cough, rigors, fever, or atypical older-adult presentation.
- Look for neurological clues: focal weakness, speech change, seizure, or postictal state.
- Assess hydration and metabolic clues: poor intake, dehydration, polyuria, polydipsia, vomiting, and tachypnoea.
- Ask whether other household members are unwell, which raises concern for carbon monoxide exposure.

## Investigations

Acute confusion has many causes. Many patients require admission and hospital-based investigations.

### Likely First-Line Checks

- Urinalysis
- Capillary blood glucose
- Pulse oximetry

### Case-Dependent Tests

- FBC
- Urea and electrolytes
- Chest X-ray
- ECG
- Cardiac enzymes if silent MI is suspected
- Thyroid function tests
- Liver function tests

### Specialist Or Hospital-Based Tests

- Calcium if parathyroid disorder is possible
- Digoxin level if toxicity is possible
- CT head to assess bleed, infarct, or space-occupying lesion when clinically indicated

## Red Flags

- Hypoxia or central cyanosis
- Hypoglycaemia, especially in a treated diabetic patient
- Focal neurological deficit, seizure, or postictal state
- Fever or sepsis concern, remembering older adults may not mount fever or tachycardia
- Suspected meningitis or encephalitis
- Suspected carbon monoxide exposure
- Unsafe home situation where supervision, hydration, medicines, or follow-up cannot be assured

## Top Tips

- The key step is confirming that confusion is truly acute rather than chronic cognitive decline.
- A drug history is essential.
- If history is limited, inspect what medicines are actually available at home.
- Examination often gives more useful clues than the patient history.
- Be cautious about home management unless the cause and safety plan are clear.

## Related Conditions

### Delirium

Acute confusion is delirium until proven otherwise. Delirium is a syndrome with multiple precipitating causes, including infection, hypoxia, medicines, and metabolic derangement.

A structured delirium approach prevents anchoring on a single explanation such as UTI or dementia worsening.

### Stroke And TIA

Stroke can present with delirium, falls, or reduced attention rather than classic FAST signs, especially posterior circulation or non-dominant hemisphere stroke.

New focal neurology, seizure, sudden onset, or uncertainty about last known well should trigger urgent stroke-pathway assessment.

### Sepsis And Septic Shock

Sepsis commonly presents with delirium in older adults, sometimes before fever or localising symptoms.

Hypotension, tachypnoea, hypoxia, rapidly worsening confusion, or lactate elevation should lower the threshold for urgent escalation.

### Hypoglycaemia

Neuroglycopenia causes confusion, abnormal behaviour, seizures, and reduced consciousness.

Bedside glucose is mandatory in acute confusion. If low, treat immediately and then review precipitating factors such as missed meals, insulin or sulfonylurea dosing, renal impairment, and alcohol.

### Hyperglycaemic Emergencies

DKA and HHS can cause dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, and altered mental state.

Polyuria, polydipsia, vomiting, tachypnoea, hypotension, or ketotic breath should prompt urgent protocol-based care.

### Medicine Toxicity And Polypharmacy

Opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, digoxin, sedatives, and recent medicine changes can precipitate delirium.

Consider renal impairment, interactions, withdrawal states, and the actual medicines being taken at home.

### Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Carbon monoxide exposure can cause headache, dizziness, and confusion. Multiple household members may be affected.

Winter or heating exposure, shared symptoms, or unexplained hypoxia requires urgent removal from exposure and oxygen treatment.

### Falls And Functional Decline

Delirium may present as falls, reduced intake, agitation, or being off legs.

A fall in a frail or anticoagulated patient with new confusion increases concern for occult intracranial bleeding and should lower the threshold for urgent imaging.
