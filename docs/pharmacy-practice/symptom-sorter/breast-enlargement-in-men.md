# Breast Enlargement In Men

## Overview

Breast enlargement in men is often presented late because of embarrassment. First confirm true gynaecomastia: palpable glandular tissue behind the areola, usually bilateral.

True gynaecomastia can be physiological, medicine-related, endocrine, systemic, or malignant.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Puberty
- Medicines such as spironolactone, cimetidine, digoxin, cyproterone, finasteride, or cannabis
- Chronic liver disease, especially alcohol-related
- Lung carcinoma
- Hyperthyroidism

### Occasional

- Hypothyroidism
- Hyperprolactinaemia
- Chronic renal failure or haemodialysis
- Testicular carcinoma
- Adrenal carcinoma
- Cryptorchidism or hypogonadism

### Rare But Important

- Klinefelter syndrome
- Acromegaly
- Renal cell carcinoma
- Male breast carcinoma

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Onset, progression, laterality, tenderness, nipple discharge, and systemic symptoms.
- Respiratory symptoms, headache, or visual disturbance.
- Medicines, supplements, alcohol, anabolic steroids, cannabis, and other substances.
- Reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, infertility, testicular symptoms, or history of undescended testis.

### Examination

- Confirm glandular tissue behind the areola.
- Check for hard irregular mass, skin or nipple change, discharge, and axillary nodes.
- Examine testes for mass or asymmetry.
- Look for chronic liver disease, thyroid signs, and clubbing.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Puberty | Medicines | Liver disease | Lung cancer | Hyperthyroidism |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Often unilateral | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| Cough | No | No | No | Yes | No |
| Weight loss | No | No | Possible | Yes | Yes |
| Generally unwell | No | Possible | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Other physical signs | No | Possible | Yes | Yes | Possible |

## Investigations

- FBC, U&E, LFTs, and TFTs unless clearly physiological pubertal gynaecomastia.
- Testosterone, chest X-ray, prolactin, LH, and FSH when indicated.
- Testicular ultrasound and tumour markers if testicular cancer is suspected.
- Breast imaging or biopsy if malignancy is possible.

## Red Flags

- Unilateral hard or fixed mass.
- Skin or nipple changes.
- Axillary nodes.
- No palpable glandular tissue behind the areola.
- Testicular mass or asymmetry.
- Headache with visual disturbance.
- Clubbing in a smoker.
- Rapid progression, weight loss, or systemic illness.

## Management Notes

- Many male breast swellings are not true gynaecomastia.
- A medication timeline is often the highest-yield intervention.
- Adult unilateral lesions or suspicious breast findings need urgent referral.

## Related Conditions

Male breast cancer, testicular tumour, chronic liver disease, medicine-induced gynaecomastia, thyroid disease, and endocrine causes should be considered.
