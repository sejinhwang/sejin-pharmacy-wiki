# Blisters, Vesicles And Bullae

## Overview

Vesicles are small fluid-filled lesions, and bullae are larger fluid-filled lesions. First decide whether the eruption is localised or generalised, dermatomal or non-dermatomal, mucosal or cutaneous only, and whether the patient is systemically unwell.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Trauma, friction, or burns
- Insect bites
- Herpes simplex
- Herpes zoster
- Hand, foot and mouth disease
- Chickenpox
- Acute eczema, including pompholyx

### Occasional

- Bullous impetigo
- Drug eruption
- Erythema multiforme
- Pemphigoid or pemphigus
- Dermatitis herpetiformis
- Oedema bullae

### Rare But Important

- Stevens-Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis
- Porphyria cutanea tarda
- Epidermolysis bullosa
- Allergic vasculitis
- Pemphigoid gestationis

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about onset, pain, itch, distribution, mucosal involvement, fever, malaise, pregnancy, immunosuppression, new medicines, contact exposures, and chickenpox exposure.
- Examine vitals, grouped vesicles, dermatomal pattern, tense versus flaccid bullae, skin tenderness, epidermal detachment, ocular signs, oral ulcers, and secondary infection.

## Investigations

- Usually none when clear and patient well.
- HSV/VZV PCR when diagnosis is uncertain or severe.
- Bacterial swab for impetigo or secondary infection.
- Biopsy for autoimmune blistering or unclear diagnosis.
- Patch testing if allergic contact dermatitis is suspected.

## Red Flags

- Toxic appearance, hypotension, extensive skin pain, or rapid progression.
- Mucosal involvement, especially with fever or medicine exposure.
- Immunosuppressed patient with HSV/VZV.
- Pregnancy with chickenpox exposure or infection.
- Any eye involvement with vesicular facial rash.

## Pharmacist Notes

- Review recent medicines, including OTC and herbal products.
- Treat suspected SJS/TEN or SCAR as an emergency and document possible culprits.
- Consider renal function for antiviral dosing.
- Assess analgesia needs in zoster.
