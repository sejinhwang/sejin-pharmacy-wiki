# Swollen Ankles

## Overview

Ankle oedema is common in older adults and can be associated with reduced mobility and falls risk. The first step is to classify the oedema.

Bilateral pitting oedema suggests systemic, medicine-related, or dependent causes. Unilateral or markedly asymmetric swelling suggests local pathology such as DVT, cellulitis, ruptured Baker's cyst, or venous disease. Non-pitting swelling suggests lymphoedema.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Congestive cardiac failure
- Medicine-induced oedema, especially calcium-channel blockers and NSAIDs
- Dependent or gravitational oedema from venous insufficiency or reduced mobility
- Obesity
- Pelvic mass, including pregnancy

### Occasional

- Cirrhosis
- Premenstrual syndrome
- Anaemia
- Acute or chronic renal disease
- Nephrotic syndrome
- Protein-losing enteropathy

### Rare But Important

- Malnutrition
- Inferior vena cava thrombosis
- Filariasis
- Milroy disease
- Hookworm
- Angioedema

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about sudden versus gradual onset, persistent versus fluctuating swelling, symmetry, and whether swelling is better in the morning.
- Ask about dyspnoea, orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, chest pain, palpitations, foamy urine, reduced urine output, jaundice, abdominal distension, pain, erythema, warmth, calf pain, and calf swelling.
- Review calcium-channel blockers, NSAIDs, corticosteroids, recent immobility, surgery, long travel, pregnancy, and postpartum state.
- Examine vitals, JVP, basal crackles, sacral oedema, abdomen, hepatomegaly, ascites, pelvic or abdominal mass, pitting versus non-pitting oedema, venous skin changes, erythema, warmth, and calf asymmetry.

| Shortness of breath on exertion | Yes | No | No | Possible | Possible |
| Altered breath sounds | Yes | Possible | No | No | No |
| Less swollen in the morning | Possible | Possible | Yes | Possible | Possible |
| Oedema-genic medicine | Possible | Yes | No | No | No |

## Investigations

- Urinalysis, FBC, U&E, and liver function tests are common first-line checks.
- Thyroid function tests are selected when clinically relevant.
- Chest X-ray, ECG, BNP, echocardiography, renal work-up, pelvic ultrasound, bowel investigations, and venous duplex ultrasound are chosen according to the suspected cause.

## Red Flags

- Marked swelling of recent and sudden onset.
- Younger adult with ankle oedema, especially if renal disease is possible.
- Unilateral ankle or leg swelling.
- Breathlessness, hypoxia, chest pain, or suspected heart failure decompensation.
- No obvious cause in an older adult after initial review, especially if abdominal or pelvic pathology is possible.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Calcium-channel blockers are a classic cause of ankle oedema.
- NSAIDs can cause fluid retention and worsen heart failure or renal disease.
- Confirm timing around medicine initiation or dose increase before attributing swelling to medicines.
- Older adults often have multifactorial oedema: immobility, venous disease, heart failure, renal disease, and medicines may overlap.
