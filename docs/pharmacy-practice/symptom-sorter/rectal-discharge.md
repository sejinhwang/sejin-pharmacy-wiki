# Rectal Discharge

## Overview

Rectal discharge may present as itching, dampness, mucus, pus, or blood-stained material. Most causes are benign, but careful history and examination are needed because IBD, STI-related disease, fistula, and malignancy can present this way.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Haemorrhoids
- Anal fissure
- Rectal prolapse
- Proctitis
- Perianal warts

### Occasional

- Rectal carcinoma
- IBS
- Anal or perianal fistula
- Villous adenoma
- Poor anal hygiene
- Solitary rectal ulcer syndrome

### Rare But Important

- Anal tuberculosis
- Anal carcinoma
- Sexually transmitted infections
- HIV/AIDS

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Character: mucus, purulent, blood-stained, odour, volume, frequency, underwear soiling, or seen only on wiping.
- Associated pain, itching, bleeding, tenesmus, diarrhoea, abdominal pain, prolapse, fever, or weight loss.
- Recent antibiotics, immunocompromise, known IBD, and sexual exposure when relevant.
- In women, confirm whether the source might be vaginal rather than rectal.

### Examination

- Inspect for warts, fissure, haemorrhoids, prolapse, and ulcerating lesions.
- Perform DRE if tolerated.
- Use proctoscopy when available and appropriate.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Haemorrhoids | Fissure | Prolapse | Proctitis | Warts |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Purulent discharge | No | Possible | No | Possible | No |
| Mucus | Yes | Possible | Yes | Yes | No |
| Blood-stained | Possible | Yes | Possible | Possible | Possible |
| Painful DRE | Possible | Yes | No | Yes | Possible |
| Associated diarrhoea | No | No | Possible | Yes | No |

## Investigations

- Swab if purulent discharge and local inflammation are present.
- FBC, ESR, CRP, and U&E when systemic or inflammatory disease is possible.
- Proctoscopy or sigmoidoscopy when rectal pathology, inflammation, or malignancy is possible.
- Biopsy of suspicious lesions through the appropriate pathway.

## Red Flags

- Rectal discharge in an older adult.
- Diarrhoea with abdominal pain.
- Ulcerating perianal lesion.
- Fever, weight loss, or significant bleeding.
- Suspected fistula or abscess.
- Persistent symptoms despite initial management.

## Management Notes

- Use a sympathetic approach so examination can be completed.
- Treat the cause rather than only the skin irritation.
- If the source is unclear in women, consider vaginal examination and STI testing.

## Related Conditions

Haemorrhoids, fissure, proctitis, Crohn's disease, fistula, abscess, malignancy, rectal bleeding, vaginal discharge, and anal itching are the major overlaps.
