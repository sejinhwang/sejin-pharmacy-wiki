# Abdominal Swelling

## Overview

Abdominal swelling may mean a general increase in abdominal girth, abdominal distension, or a discrete abdominal or pelvic mass.

The first task is to distinguish intermittent or variable swelling, which is more often functional or benign, from progressive swelling, which carries higher risk of serious pathology.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Pregnancy
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Constipation
- Fibroid uterus
- Enlarged bladder from urinary retention

### Occasional

- Ascites
- Intestinal obstruction
- Ovarian mass, including cyst or malignancy
- Carcinoma of stomach or colon
- Hepatomegaly

### Rare But Important

- Splenomegaly
- Pancreatic carcinoma
- Abdominal aortic aneurysm
- Massive para-aortic lymphadenopathy
- Hydronephrosis, renal cysts, or renal malignancy

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Time course: intermittent, variable, or progressive.
- Pain, especially acute onset pain.
- Weight loss.
- Change in bowel habit.
- Nausea or vomiting.
- Urinary symptoms such as poor stream or retention.
- Gynaecological symptoms such as amenorrhoea, menorrhagia, or pelvic pain.
- Constipating medicines, including opioids, antimuscarinics, iron, and calcium-channel blockers.
- Anticholinergic burden and urinary retention risk.

### Examination

- Some reported swellings are not palpable or represent normal anatomy.
- Obesity can make examination difficult and may mimic ascites.
- If examination is uncertain, arrange ultrasound.
- Tympany on percussion does not exclude a solid mass, because retroperitoneal masses can displace bowel anteriorly.

## Investigations

### Likely First-Line

- Pregnancy test
- Abdominal or pelvic ultrasound

### Case-Dependent

- Urinalysis, including haematuria
- FBC
- Urea and electrolytes
- Liver function tests
- CA-125 in women, especially age 50 or older, to support ovarian cancer risk assessment
- Plain abdominal X-ray if constipation or obstruction is suspected

### Specialist Or Second-Line

- Endoscopy or colonic evaluation
- Paracentesis in ascites
- CT for mass characterisation, obstruction, or malignancy staging

## Red Flags

- Weight loss with abdominal swelling
- Acute onset swelling with abdominal pain
- Concern for obstruction
- Uncertain examination, especially obesity versus ascites
- Progressive persistent bloating in women age 50 or older

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Consider constipation from opioids, iron, anticholinergics, and verapamil.
- Consider urinary retention from antimuscarinics, opioids, and alpha-agonists.
- If obstruction is suspected, avoid laxatives until assessed.

## Related Conditions

### Constipation

Faecal loading and gas trapping can cause bloating or distension. Opioid- and anticholinergic-associated constipation is common.

Worsening distension with vomiting, severe pain, or obstipation should prompt urgent assessment for obstruction rather than laxative escalation.

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome

IBS can cause bloating and variable distension. A diurnal pattern and fluctuation support IBS.

New symptoms after age 50, nocturnal symptoms, progressive swelling, rectal bleeding, or weight loss should trigger investigation rather than an IBS label.

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease Or Toxic Megacolon

Severe colitis may present with abdominal distension, pain, and systemic upset. Stricturing Crohn's disease can cause obstructive symptoms.

Fever, marked distension, reduced stool output, and systemic illness require urgent hospital assessment.

### Bowel Obstruction Or Ileus

Mechanical obstruction or ileus causes progressive distension from gas and fluid accumulation.

Persistent vomiting, severe or colicky pain, obstipation, peritonism, or dehydration require urgent imaging and surgical assessment.

### Ascites

Free intraperitoneal fluid causes abdominal distension and early satiety and may precipitate breathlessness.

Ascites can signal decompensated liver disease, malignancy, heart failure, or pancreatitis. New abdominal pain, fever, or encephalopathy in a patient with ascites raises concern for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.

### Ovarian Malignancy

Ovarian malignancy often presents non-specifically with persistent abdominal distension or bloating and may cause malignant ascites.

Persistent progressive bloating, early satiety, pelvic or abdominal pain, or urinary frequency in women age 50 or older should prompt ovarian cancer work-up.

### Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm

AAA can present as a pulsatile abdominal mass. Severe abdominal or back pain, collapse, syncope, or shock are emergency features.

### Urinary Retention

A distended bladder can mimic a lower abdominal mass. Acute urinary retention requires urgent decompression.

Retention with back pain, saddle anaesthesia, or leg weakness raises concern for cauda equina or spinal cord compression.

### Pregnancy

Pregnancy is a common cause of increasing abdominal girth. Always exclude pregnancy in people of childbearing potential.

Pain with vaginal bleeding or syncope requires urgent assessment for ectopic pregnancy.
