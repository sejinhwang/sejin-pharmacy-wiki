# Abnormal Movements

## Overview

Abnormal movements are an infrequent presentation. The task is to distinguish benign tic, fasciculation, myokymia, or restless legs from seizure, progressive neurological disease, and medicine-induced movement disorders.

Medicine-induced dystonia, tardive dyskinesia, akathisia, and acute hemiballismus are particularly important not to miss.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Restless legs syndrome
- Myokymia
- Medicine-induced choreoathetosis, dystonia, tardive dyskinesia, or akathisia
- Tourette syndrome
- Focal seizures

### Occasional

- Anxiety or nervous tic
- Fasciculation, benign or from motor neurone disease
- Simple childhood tics
- Dystonias, including writer's cramp, blepharospasm, and torticollis
- Periodic limb movements

### Rare But Important

- Myoclonus
- Sydenham chorea
- Huntington disease
- Wilson disease
- Hemiballismus after stroke
- Functional movement disorder

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask the patient or witness to describe the movement: urge, tic, jerk, tremor, writhing, posture, restlessness, or repeated stereotyped episode.
- Clarify whether it is worse at rest, improves with movement, occurs during sleep, or appears in episodes.
- Ask about weakness, sensory symptoms, speech change, loss of awareness, personality change, family history, and rapid progression.
- Review medicines carefully, including levodopa, tricyclic antidepressants, metoclopramide, prochlorperazine, antipsychotics, stimulants, and recent dose changes.
- Examine cranial nerves, tone, reflexes, power, coordination, gait, oromandibular and tongue movements, akathisia, and focal neurological signs.

## Investigations

- FBC, U&E, ferritin, B12, folate, thyroid function, HbA1c, and calcium are selected based on the presentation.
- CT or MRI is used when focal neurology, acute onset, structural disease, or stroke is possible.
- EEG is used when seizures are suspected.
- EMG or nerve conduction studies are used for fasciculations or neuromuscular concern.
- Huntington, Wilson disease, and myoclonus work-up are specialist-led.

## Red Flags

- Acute dystonia affecting airway, swallowing, eyes, or neck.
- Complex childhood tics with functional impairment or diagnostic uncertainty.
- Personality change with abnormal movements suggesting Huntington disease.
- Focal neurological signs or acute hemiballismus suggesting stroke.
- Progressive weakness, rapid deterioration, or widespread fasciculation.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Take a careful medicine history with dates, dose changes, missed doses, and recent additions.
- Antipsychotics, metoclopramide, and prochlorperazine are common culprits for acute dystonia and tardive syndromes.
- For restless legs, check ferritin and review medicines that can worsen symptoms.
- Document timing and doses clearly before escalation, because causality often depends on sequence.

## Related Conditions

- Medicine-induced movement disorders
- Epilepsy and focal seizures
- Tremor and Parkinsonism
- Anxiety, tics, and functional symptoms
- Sleep disturbance and restless legs syndrome
- Metabolic causes of abnormal movements
