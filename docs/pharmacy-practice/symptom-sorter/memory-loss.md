# Memory Loss

## Overview

Memory loss is distressing for patients and carers. The first distinction is acute memory impairment, which may represent delirium or acute confusion, versus subacute or chronic memory loss over weeks to years.

Collateral history is often essential because people with dementia may be unaware of, minimise, or deny functional problems.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Anxiety or stress
- Depressive illness
- Dementia, including Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, and dementia with an underlying reversible cause
- Head injury
- Cerebrovascular event

### Occasional

- Chronic alcohol excess and thiamine deficiency
- Subarachnoid haemorrhage
- Thiamine deficiency from malabsorption, gastric carcinoma, or hyperemesis gravidarum
- Transient global amnesia
- Fugue state or psychogenic amnesia
- Tumour of the third ventricle or hypothalamus

### Rare But Important

- Intractable epilepsy
- Carbon monoxide poisoning
- Herpes simplex encephalitis
- Malingering or personality disorder

## Clinical Assessment

- Establish when the patient was last normal and whether the onset was sudden, stepwise, gradual, or fluctuating.
- Clarify the main deficit: recent events, word-finding, getting lost, executive dysfunction, behaviour change, or personality change.
- Assess functional impact: medicines, finances, driving, cooking, appliances, work, and safety at home.
- Screen for delirium: inattention, fluctuation, sleep-wake reversal, hallucinations, infection, dehydration, hypoxia, and medicine toxicity.
- Screen mood, anxiety, alcohol use, sedatives, anticholinergics, opioids, and other substances.
- Examine observations, hydration, nutrition, orientation, attention, recall, focal neurology, gait, and coordination.

## Investigations

- FBC, thyroid function, liver function including gamma-GT, and calcium are common first-line checks.
- Vitamin B12 and folate are selected when deficiency is possible.
- Syphilis serology is used in selected cases.
- CT or MRI brain is used when stroke, tumour, rapidly progressive disease, head injury, or other structural disease is possible.

## Red Flags

- Sudden memory loss with focal neurological deficit.
- Memory loss after significant head injury.
- Rapid progression over 3 to 6 months or less.
- Fever, meningism, seizures, or concern for encephalitis.
- Suspected carbon monoxide exposure, especially if others in the household are unwell.
- Delirium features or inability to ensure safety at home.

## Top Tips

- Self-presenting memory complaints are often depression or anxiety-related poor concentration.
- Explain poor concentration versus failing memory to reduce anxiety about dementia.
- Depression and dementia can coexist.
- A medicine and substance review is often a high-yield reversible intervention.
