# Cough In Adults

## Overview

Cough is a common presenting complaint and is often due to viral upper respiratory tract infection. Persistent cough, however, can signal more serious disease.

The priority is to clarify duration, identify common reversible causes, and use a low threshold for chest X-ray when red flags or risk factors are present.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Upper respiratory tract infection
- Lower respiratory tract infection
- Asthma
- COPD
- ACE inhibitor-associated cough

### Occasional

- Smoking, including passive exposure
- Lung tumour, primary or secondary
- Rhinitis or post-nasal drip
- Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease
- Left ventricular failure
- Bronchiectasis
- Aspiration, such as after stroke

### Rare But Important

- Tuberculosis
- Medicine adverse effects, such as methotrexate-related lung toxicity
- Pulmonary fibrosis or fibrosing alveolitis
- Extrinsic allergic alveolitis
- Psychogenic cough
- Laryngeal carcinoma
- Inhaled foreign body
- Diaphragmatic irritation, such as subphrenic abscess

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Duration: acute under 3 weeks, subacute 3 to 8 weeks, chronic over 8 weeks.
- Cough type: dry, productive, sputum volume and colour, or haemoptysis.
- Associated symptoms: breathlessness, wheeze, fever, chest pain, weight loss, night sweats, hoarseness, or voice change.
- Pattern: worse at night, worse on exertion, worse after meals or lying down, throat clearing, or post-nasal drip.
- Risk factors: smoking, immunosuppression, methotrexate, recent travel, TB exposure, aspiration risk, stroke, and dysphagia.
- Medicines: ACE inhibitor use, noting that onset can be delayed by months.

### Examination

- Temperature, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation if unwell
- Respiratory examination for wheeze, crackles, or focal findings
- Cardiovascular examination for signs of heart failure
- ENT assessment for rhinitis or post-nasal drip

## Investigations

### Often Not Needed

- No investigations are usually needed for uncomplicated acute viral URTI without red flags.

### Case-Dependent Tests

- FBC: anaemia in malignancy or chronic disease, raised white cell count in infection, eosinophilia in allergic disease.
- ESR or CRP: may rise with infection, inflammation, or neoplasia.
- Chest X-ray: consider early when risk factors or persistent cough are present.
- Spirometry: asthma, COPD, or restrictive patterns.
- Serial peak flow: supports asthma diagnosis.
- Sputum testing: TB investigation when relevant, or occasional antibiotic guidance in LRTI or COPD exacerbation.
- BNP or echocardiogram if left ventricular failure is suspected.
- Serum precipitins if extrinsic allergic alveolitis is suspected.
- CT chest or bronchoscopy if chest X-ray is abnormal or suspicion remains high.

## Red Flags

- Haemoptysis
- Weight loss, night sweats, or persistent fever
- Persistent cough with voice change, especially in smokers
- Significant breathlessness or hypoxia
- Immunosuppression, including methotrexate use, with cough
- TB risk with persistent cough
- Persistent cough in a smoker or older adult

## Top Tips

- Cough after a simple URTI can last up to 3 weeks.
- In persistent cough, trigger history is often more helpful than auscultation.
- ACE inhibitor cough can start months after initiation.
- ACE inhibitor cough often improves within 1 to 4 weeks after stopping, but can take up to 3 months to fully resolve.
- Persistent cough with normal chest X-ray and no chest signs should prompt consideration of asthma, GORD, and rhinitis.

## Related Conditions

### Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease

Acid reflux is a common cause of chronic dry cough. Cough that is worse at night or lying flat, throat clearing, or heartburn supports a reflux trial after red flags are excluded.

### Asthma

Asthma can present with cough as the dominant symptom. Nocturnal cough, wheeze, trigger-related symptoms, or peak-flow variability supports an asthma pathway and inhaled therapy trial.

### COPD And Chronic Bronchitis

COPD causes chronic cough with sputum and progressive exertional dyspnoea. Smoking history and persistent productive cough are decision points for spirometry and COPD optimisation.

### Rhinitis And Post-Nasal Drip

Nasal congestion, throat clearing, and post-nasal drip commonly drive chronic cough. A trial of intranasal steroid or antihistamine may be more appropriate than antibiotics when the chest examination is normal.

### ACE Inhibitor Cough

ACE inhibitors can cause persistent dry cough. Medication review is essential, and stopping or substituting the ACE inhibitor usually improves cough within weeks.

### Chronic Shortness Of Breath

Dyspnoea helps gauge underlying lung or cardiac disease burden. Progressive exertional breathlessness with cough should prompt spirometry, chest X-ray, and consideration of COPD, interstitial lung disease, or heart failure.

### Haemoptysis

Coughing up blood is a red flag in any cough. Even small-volume haemoptysis, especially in smokers or older adults, warrants urgent assessment and a low threshold for imaging.

### Pneumonia Or Lower Respiratory Tract Infection

Acute cough with fever, pleuritic pain, and focal chest signs suggests pneumonia. Hypoxia, tachypnoea, confusion, or significant comorbidity should prompt escalation.

### Tuberculosis Risk

Persistent cough with night sweats, weight loss, travel, or exposure risk changes the investigation pathway and raises infection-control urgency.
