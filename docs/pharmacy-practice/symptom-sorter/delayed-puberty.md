# Delayed Puberty

## Overview

Delayed puberty is delayed development of secondary sexual characteristics. In girls, it may present as no menstruation by age 16 or lack of pubertal development by age 14. In boys, it is typically defined as no pubertal development by age 15.

Constitutional delay is common in boys, but delayed puberty in girls is more often pathological and usually warrants investigation.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Constitutional delay
- Hyperthyroidism
- Turner syndrome
- Anorexia nervosa
- Hypothalamic GnRH deficiency, including Noonan syndrome or Kallmann syndrome

### Occasional

- Hypothalamo-pituitary space-occupying lesion
- Chronic disease, including diabetes, renal failure, cystic fibrosis, or coeliac disease
- Hyperprolactinaemia
- Adrenal disease, including congenital adrenal hyperplasia or Cushing disease
- Medicines or treatments, including thyroxine, chemotherapy, androgens, anabolic steroids, and radiotherapy
- Growth hormone deficiency

### Rare But Important

- Ovarian dysgenesis or autoimmune ovarian disease
- Autoimmune hypothyroidism
- Pure gonadal dysgenesis
- Maldescent of testes
- Trauma, infection, or granulomas of the hypothalamus or pituitary

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about breast development, pubic and axillary hair, growth spurt, menarche, menstrual pattern, testicular enlargement, and voice change.
- Assess growth and nutrition: height, weight, centiles, weight loss, restrictive eating, and excessive exercise.
- Ask about chronic illness symptoms, polyuria, polydipsia, chronic diarrhoea, abdominal symptoms, fatigue, and reduced exercise tolerance.
- Ask about headache, visual disturbance, anosmia, family history, glucocorticoids, chemotherapy, and anabolic steroids.
- Examine height, weight, growth trajectory, Tanner stage, dysmorphic features, short stature, Turner features, and signs of chronic disease.

## Investigations

- Most cases needing investigation require paediatrics or endocrinology referral.
- Primary care tests may include urinalysis, FBC, U&E, and thyroid function tests.
- Secondary care testing may include pelvic ultrasound, chromosomal analysis, gonadotrophins, sex steroids, prolactin, and MRI for pituitary or hypothalamic disease.

## Red Flags

- Do not diagnose constitutional delay in a boy with very small penis or anosmia.
- Delayed puberty in girls is commonly pathological and usually needs investigation.
- Short stature, malaise, and symptoms or signs of hypothyroidism may suggest hypothalamo-pituitary disease.
- Headache, visual symptoms, poor growth, weight loss, or chronic systemic symptoms increase urgency.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Chemotherapy and radiotherapy can impair pubertal development.
- Anabolic steroids and androgens can disrupt pubertal progression.
- Poorly controlled diabetes, coeliac disease, eating disorder behaviours, and chronic disease can delay puberty.
- Maintain safeguarding awareness when restrictive eating or bullying is suspected.
