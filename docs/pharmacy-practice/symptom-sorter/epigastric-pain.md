# Epigastric Pain

## Overview

Epigastric pain is common in primary care and urgent care and is often described as indigestion.

First clarify what the patient means: pain, burning, fullness, or nausea. Then establish whether the presentation is acute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic, and identify red flags that need urgent investigation.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Functional dyspepsia
- GORD or gastritis
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Gallstones or biliary colic
- Duodenal ulcer or duodenitis

### Occasional

- Medicine-related irritation, including NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, and some antibiotics
- Acute or chronic pancreatitis
- Abdominal wall or musculoskeletal pain
- Peritonitis, such as perforated duodenal ulcer
- Gastric ulcer
- Oesophageal spasm
- Aerophagy

### Rare But Important

- Myocardial infarction, especially inferior MI
- Pneumonia, especially lower lobe
- Stomach cancer
- Pancreatic cancer
- Ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm
- Gastrointestinal obstruction
- Referred pain from spine
- Epigastric hernia

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Time course: acute, chronic, acute-on-chronic, progressive, or intermittent.
- Character: burning, gnawing hunger pain, post-prandial pain, or severe constant pain radiating to the back.
- Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, early satiety, anorexia, dysphagia, odynophagia, melaena, haematemesis, weight loss, or fever.
- Triggers and modifiers: meals, antacid response, nocturnal symptoms, and alcohol intake.
- Medicines: NSAIDs, aspirin, bisphosphonates, oral iron, potassium salts, and corticosteroids, especially if combined with NSAIDs.

### Examination

- General appearance and observations, including fever.
- Epigastric tenderness.
- Guarding or rebound tenderness.
- Right upper quadrant tenderness.
- Mass.
- Cardiopulmonary assessment when older age, diabetes, cardiovascular risk, or atypical symptoms make cardiac or pulmonary causes plausible.

## Investigations

### Likely First-Line

- FBC
- Helicobacter pylori testing according to local pathway

### Case-Dependent

- Liver function tests
- Ultrasound if biliary disease is suspected
- Upper GI endoscopy according to age, red flags, and local guideline pathway

### Specialist Or Second-Line

- Amylase or lipase if pancreatitis is suspected
- ECG and cardiac enzymes if cardiac cause is possible
- Chest X-ray if pneumonia is possible
- CT or MRI if mass or complications are suspected
- Oesophageal manometry or pH studies in selected specialist cases

## Red Flags

- New dyspepsia or significant symptom change in patients age 55 or older
- Unexplained weight loss
- Dysphagia
- Gastrointestinal bleeding
- Persistent vomiting
- Abdominal mass
- Anaemia
- Systemic upset or severe worsening pain
- Clinical concern for malignancy despite previous normal investigations

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Medicines history is often the quickest fix.
- NSAIDs, aspirin, bisphosphonates, iron, potassium salts, and corticosteroids can cause or worsen symptoms.
- If NSAID-related dyspepsia is suspected, review the indication, deprescribe where appropriate, and consider gastroprotection if ongoing NSAID use is required.
- A normal endoscopy does not rule out oesophageal disease such as GORD or spasm.

## Related Conditions

### GORD And Gastritis

Reflux and gastritis commonly cause burning epigastric discomfort, regurgitation, and fluctuating symptoms.

Persistent symptoms despite optimised PPI use, dysphagia, weight loss, anaemia, GI bleeding, or persistent vomiting require investigation rather than indefinite escalation.

### Peptic Ulcer Disease And H. Pylori

Duodenal or gastric ulceration causes gnawing epigastric pain and nausea. H. pylori testing and NSAID or aspirin exposure review are decision points that prevent recurrence and bleeding.

Severe sudden pain with guarding or rebound suggests perforation and requires emergency care.

### Upper GI Bleeding

Ulcers and erosions can bleed and present with haematemesis or melaena.

Any haematemesis, melaena, syncope, or haemodynamic instability requires urgent assessment. Anticoagulants, antiplatelets, and NSAIDs amplify bleeding risk.

### Acute Pancreatitis

Pancreatitis classically causes severe constant epigastric pain radiating to the back with vomiting.

Jaundice, rigors, cholestatic liver tests, shock, SIRS, ARDS, or AKI require urgent escalation.

### Gallstones And Acute Cholecystitis

Biliary colic and cholecystitis can present with epigastric or right upper quadrant pain, often post-prandial, with nausea or vomiting.

Pain lasting more than 4 to 6 hours, fever, jaundice, or rigors suggests complicated biliary disease.

### Acute Coronary Syndrome Mimic

Inferior MI or ACS can present as epigastric discomfort with nausea, vomiting, or diaphoresis.

New epigastric pain in older adults or people with diabetes or cardiovascular risk warrants ECG and troponin pathway consideration.

### Aortic Catastrophe

Acute aortic syndrome or ruptured AAA can present with sudden severe epigastric, abdominal, or back pain and shock.

Abrupt maximal pain, collapse, syncope, pulse deficits, or blood pressure differential are emergency features.

### Upper GI Malignancy

Gastric or pancreatic cancer may present with persistent epigastric pain, early satiety, anorexia, and weight loss.

Progressive symptoms, anaemia, persistent vomiting, or palpable mass require urgent investigation.
