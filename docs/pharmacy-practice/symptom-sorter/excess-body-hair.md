# Excess Body Hair

## Overview

Excess body hair means excess terminal hair growth in women in male-distribution sites such as the chin, cheeks, upper lip, lower abdomen, and thighs. It often presents as a cosmetic concern.

Ethnic background matters when interpreting hair growth. Long-standing mild hirsutism is often benign; sudden severe hirsutism needs careful endocrine assessment.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Constitutional or physiological hirsutism
- Polycystic ovary syndrome
- Anorexia nervosa with lanugo-type hair
- Menopause-related relative androgen excess
- Iatrogenic causes, including phenytoin, minoxidil, danazol, and glucocorticoids

### Occasional

- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia
- Anabolic steroid use
- Ovarian tumours, including arrhenoblastoma, hilus cell tumour, and luteoma
- Adrenal carcinoma or adenoma
- Congenital or juvenile hypothyroidism

### Rare But Important

- Cushing disease or acromegaly
- Porphyria cutanea tarda
- Hypertrichosis lanuginosa
- Cornelia de Lange syndrome

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about onset, speed of progression, distribution, menstrual irregularity, amenorrhoea, acne, scalp hair loss, voice deepening, clitoromegaly, weight change, eating behaviours, exercise, and menopause symptoms.
- Review medicines, supplements, bodybuilding products, and possible anabolic steroid exposure.
- Ask about headache and visual field symptoms suggesting pituitary disease.
- Examine for terminal versus vellus hair, acne, androgenic alopecia, virilisation, obesity, striae, Cushingoid features, thyroid signs, and signs of eating disorder.

## Investigations

- Mild, long-standing cases may not need investigation.
- Serum testosterone is often the most useful first endocrine test. Mild elevation suggests PCOS; marked elevation suggests a tumour.
- Pelvic ultrasound may show polycystic ovaries or ovarian tumour.
- FBC and U&E may be useful when anorexia, anaemia, electrolyte disturbance, or adrenal disease is possible.
- Thyroid function tests are selected when thyroid disease is possible.
- FSH, LH, prolactin, adrenal testing, pituitary or adrenal imaging, and urinary porphyrins are specialist or case-dependent tests.

## Red Flags

- Sudden and severe hirsutism.
- Amenorrhoea, new scalp balding, virilisation, or patient generally unwell.
- Recent significant headache or visual field defect suggesting pituitary disease.
- Suspected anabolic steroid use or severe eating disorder.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Review minoxidil, phenytoin, danazol, glucocorticoids, anabolic steroids, and non-prescribed hormone products.
- Take the cosmetic burden seriously and be ready to discuss bleaching, depilatory creams, electrolysis, and medical referral.
- Screen for pregnancy risk before medicine changes when menstrual irregularity is present.
