# Acute Abdominal Pain in Adults

## Overview

Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain can represent a true acute abdomen and is a time-critical emergency.

In primary care or ambulatory triage, the aim is usually safe disposition rather than an exact diagnosis: urgent referral or admission, short-interval review, or carefully safety-netted home management.

Scope note: this page focuses on upper and mid-abdominal pain. Lower abdominal pain is handled with acute pelvic pain, and epigastric-type pain is covered in more detail under epigastric pain.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Peptic ulcer disease
- Biliary colic
- Appendicitis
- Gastroenteritis
- Renal colic

### Occasional

- Cholecystitis, especially constant pain with fever after biliary colic
- Diverticulitis
- Acute or subacute bowel obstruction from adhesions, carcinoma, strangulated hernia, or volvulus
- Pyelonephritis
- Muscular wall pain
- Pancreatitis

### Rare But Important

- Perforation, such as duodenal ulcer or carcinoma, causing peritonitis
- Hepatitis
- Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis
- Ischaemic bowel
- Dissecting or leaking aneurysm
- DKA and other medical causes, including myocardial infarction, pneumonia, and sickle cell crisis

## Clinical Assessment

### Triage Priorities

- Use ABCDE and vital signs early.
- Look for peritonism and shock.
- Separate colicky pain from constant pain with tenderness.
- Colicky pain may be suitable for home management only in selected patients with a clear plan and safety-netting.
- Constant pain with tenderness is more likely to require hospital assessment.

### Examination

- Pulse rate and temperature
- Abdominal tenderness, guarding, and percussion tenderness
- Bowel sounds
- Rectal examination when indicated
- Hernial orifices, because hernia-related obstruction is easy to miss

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Peptic ulcer | Renal colic | Biliary colic | Appendicitis | Gastroenteritis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Colicky pain | No | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| Localised pain | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | No |
| Abdominal tenderness | Yes | No | Possible | Yes | Possible |
| Fever | No | No | No | Yes | Possible |
| Diarrhoea | No | No | No | Possible | Yes |

## Investigations

### In Primary Care

- Urinalysis is often the most useful immediate test.
- Haematuria can support renal colic.
- Urinary infection can suggest UTI or pyelonephritis.
- Glycosuria or ketones should prompt consideration of DKA.

### Usually Hospital-Based

- FBC for raised white cell count
- Urea and electrolytes, especially with diarrhoea or vomiting
- Amylase or lipase for pancreatitis, noting that amylase may also rise in ischaemic bowel
- Liver function tests and bilirubin for biliary obstruction or hepatitis
- Helicobacter pylori testing when peptic ulcer disease is suspected
- Imaging according to suspected cause: erect chest or abdominal X-ray for perforation, supine abdominal X-ray for obstruction, ultrasound for gallstones, and CT KUB for ureteric stones in many modern pathways

## Red Flags

- Constant or worsening pain with tenderness
- Peritonism, guarding, rigidity, or shock
- Gastroenteritis label with evolving constant pain, especially possible appendicitis
- Older adult with severe pain and irregular pulse, which raises concern for mesenteric infarction
- Suspected obstruction, especially with hernia, vomiting, distension, or reduced stool and flatus

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Ask about NSAIDs and anticoagulants because they increase peptic ulcer and bleeding risk.
- Opioids and anticholinergics can contribute to constipation or ileus.
- SGLT2 inhibitors can contribute to euglycaemic DKA in the right clinical context.
- Do not let symptomatic treatment delay urgent referral when pain is constant, worsening, or accompanied by systemic features.

## Related Conditions

### Appendicitis

Appendicitis is a common surgical cause of acute abdominal pain. Classically, pain migrates from periumbilical to right iliac fossa and may be associated with anorexia, nausea, and vomiting.

Missed appendicitis is high harm. Peritonism, sepsis physiology, persistent vomiting or dehydration, deterioration despite a gastroenteritis framing, or possible pregnancy should prompt urgent assessment.

### Acute Pancreatitis

Severe constant epigastric pain radiating to the back, often with nausea and vomiting, is a classic acute abdomen phenotype.

Severity can escalate rapidly with SIRS, shock, ARDS, or AKI. Jaundice, rigors, or cholestatic liver tests suggest biliary obstruction or cholangitis and require urgent escalation.

### Peptic Ulcer Disease And Perforation

Ulceration can cause epigastric pain. Perforation causes sudden severe pain with peritonism.

A rigid abdomen, rebound or guarding, and shock features warrant immediate hospital pathway. NSAID exposure and anticoagulation increase ulcer and bleeding risk.

### Bowel Obstruction Or Ileus

Obstruction or ileus causes progressive distension, colicky pain, vomiting, and reduced stool or flatus.

Obstipation, persistent vomiting, severe pain, peritonism, or dehydration require urgent imaging and surgical assessment. Hernial orifices must be checked.

### Sepsis And Septic Shock

Peritonitis, perforation, appendicitis, cholangitis, and complicated pancreatitis can progress to systemic infection and shock.

Hypotension, altered mental state, tachypnoea, oliguria, or lactate elevation should trigger immediate sepsis pathway rather than watchful waiting.

### Acute Coronary Syndrome Mimic

ACS can present atypically with epigastric discomfort, nausea, vomiting, and diaphoresis, particularly in older adults and people with diabetes.

Do not assume a gastric cause until cardiac risk is considered. ECG and troponin pathway may be time-critical.

### Acute Aortic Syndrome Or Leaking Aneurysm

Dissection or leaking aneurysm may present with sudden severe abdominal or back pain and shock.

Abrupt maximal pain, pulse or blood pressure differential, syncope, collapse, neurological features, or anticoagulation context should prompt urgent imaging and senior escalation.

### DKA And Hyperglycaemic Emergencies

DKA commonly presents with abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, dehydration, and tachypnoea. SGLT2 inhibitors can precipitate euglycaemic DKA.

Check capillary glucose and ketones early in unwell patients, especially with diabetes, SGLT2 inhibitor use, or pregnancy.

### Vomiting And Dehydration

Vomiting can accompany acute abdominal pathology and can drive dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, and AKI.

A pain-first-then-vomiting sequence raises surgical concern. Bilious or blood-stained vomit, severe headache or neck stiffness, severe abdominal pain or guarding, or shock features require urgent assessment.

### Diarrhoea And Surgical Mimics

Diarrhoea often points to gastroenteritis but can occur with pelvic appendicitis or inflammatory bowel disease.

Significant tenderness, persistent constant pain, blood in stool, high fever, severe dehydration, or systemic toxicity should not be dismissed as simple diarrhoea.
