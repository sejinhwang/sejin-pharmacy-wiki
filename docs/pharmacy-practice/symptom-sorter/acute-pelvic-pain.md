# Acute Pelvic Pain

## Overview

Acute pelvic pain is common and often benign, but several causes are time-critical. The priorities are to exclude pregnancy and ectopic pregnancy, identify sepsis or PID, and consider ovarian torsion, ruptured cyst, appendicitis, and strangulated hernia.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Pelvic inflammatory disease
- Urinary tract infection
- Miscarriage
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Ovarian cyst torsion or rupture

### Occasional

- Pelvic abscess from appendix or PID
- Endometriosis
- Pelvic congestion
- Prostatitis in men
- Functional or psychosexual pain

### Rare But Important

- Misplaced or perforating intrauterine contraceptive device
- Referred pain
- Proctitis
- Ovarian or cervical cancer
- Fibroid degeneration
- Strangulated femoral or inguinal hernia

## Clinical Assessment

- Decide first whether pregnancy is possible, whether the patient is unstable, and whether sepsis or peritonism is present.
- Ask about onset, site, radiation, severity, bleeding, discharge, urinary symptoms, bowel symptoms, vomiting, fever, LMP, contraception, IUCD, recent pregnancy or abortion, and sexual risk.
- Examine observations, abdomen, peritonism, guarding, masses, and groin hernias.
- Pelvic examination and cervical motion tenderness assessment are performed where appropriate and within clinician scope.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | PID | UTI | Miscarriage | Ectopic Pregnancy | Ovarian Cyst |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Vaginal bleeding | Possible | No | Yes | Possible or absent | No |
| Purulent discharge | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| Fever | Possible | Possible | Possible | Possible | Possible |
| Pelvic mass | No | No | Possible | Possible | Possible |
| Uterine or cervical motion tenderness | Yes | No | Possible | Possible | Possible |

## Investigations

- Pregnancy test.
- Urinalysis and urine culture when UTI is possible.
- Swabs for STI and vaginal infection assessment where indicated.
- FBC, CRP, and ESR when infection or inflammation is suspected.
- Pelvic ultrasound.
- CT abdomen or pelvis when non-gynaecological causes are possible.
- Laparoscopy may be needed for ectopic pregnancy, torsion, or unclear severe pain.

## Red Flags

- Severe unilateral pelvic pain around six weeks after LMP: suspect ectopic pregnancy.
- Syncope, shoulder-tip pain, hypotension, or pallor: possible ruptured ectopic pregnancy.
- Fever, rigors, worsening pain, or sepsis features.
- Peritonism or persistent vomiting.
- Suspected ovarian torsion.
- Painful groin hernia.
- PID not improving within 48 to 72 hours of appropriate treatment.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Think pregnancy first, even when the presenting complaint sounds urinary or gastrointestinal.
- PID requires testing, treatment, partner considerations, and escalation if severe.
- Vaginal contamination can confuse UTI assessment.
- Provide analgesia safety-netting rather than masking worsening pain without follow-up.
- Recent IUCD insertion, missing strings, or new severe pain raises concern for perforation or malposition.
