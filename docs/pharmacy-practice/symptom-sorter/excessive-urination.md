# Excessive Urination

## Overview

Excessive urination, or polyuria, is urine output above 3 L/day in adults. It must be distinguished from urinary frequency, where small amounts are passed often, and nocturia.

The core task is to distinguish osmotic diuresis, usually glucose-driven, from water diuresis due to diabetes insipidus or primary polydipsia.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Diabetes mellitus
- Diuretic therapy, including loop diuretics, thiazides, and SGLT2 inhibitors
- Primary polydipsia
- Excess caffeine or alcohol

### Occasional

- Chronic renal failure
- Hypercalcaemia
- Hypokalaemia
- Post-obstructive diuresis

### Rare But Important

- Cranial diabetes insipidus
- Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, including lithium-associated
- Addison's disease
- Sickle cell anaemia

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Clarify large-volume polyuria versus frequent small voids.
- Ask about thirst, nocturia, sudden versus gradual onset, weight loss, fatigue, and visual symptoms.
- Review diuretics, lithium, SGLT2 inhibitors, vitamin D, caffeine, alcohol, and fluid intake.

### Examination

- Hydration status.
- Abdominal examination for bladder distension.
- Neurological and visual field clues when pituitary disease is possible.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Diabetes mellitus | Diabetes insipidus | Primary polydipsia | Hypercalcaemia | Frequency/LUTS |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Urine volume | High | Very high | High | High | Normal |
| Thirst | Yes | Intense | Variable | Yes | No |
| Urine glucose | Yes | No | No | No | No |
| Urine osmolality | High | Very low | Low | Low | Normal |
| Nocturia | Yes | Yes | Variable | Yes | Yes |

## Investigations

### First-Line

- Urinalysis for glucose and specific gravity.
- Fasting glucose or HbA1c.
- U&E, creatinine, potassium, calcium, and renal function.

### Specialist Or Secondary Care

- Paired serum and urine osmolality.
- Water deprivation test.
- MRI pituitary if cranial diabetes insipidus is suspected.

## Red Flags

- Polyuria with thirst and weight loss, especially type 1 diabetes concern.
- Sudden polyuria with headache or visual loss.
- Hypercalcaemia concern.
- Severe dehydration.

## Top Tips

- Patients often confuse frequency with polyuria.
- A fluid balance chart or bladder diary is the most useful practical differentiator.
- SGLT2 inhibitors cause glycosuria and polyuria as a therapeutic effect, but DKA symptoms remain important.
- In diabetes insipidus, nocturia is expected; in primary polydipsia, nocturia may be absent or variable.

## Related Conditions

Diabetes mellitus, thirst and dry mouth, nocturia, urinary frequency, incontinence, renal disease, hypercalcaemia, and pituitary disease are the main linked pathways.
