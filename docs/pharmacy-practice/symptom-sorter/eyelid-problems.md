# Eyelid Problems

## Overview

Eyelid problems are often distressing and cosmetic, but they should not be treated as a quick minor complaint. The differential includes infection, lid malposition, allergic swelling, systemic oedema, neurological ptosis, and malignancy.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Stye
- Blepharitis
- Meibomian cyst
- Xanthelasma
- Blocked tear duct

### Occasional

- Periorbital oedema from orbital cellulitis, herpes zoster, angioedema, nephrotic syndrome, or insect bite
- Ectropion
- Entropion or trichiasis
- Eyelid eczema
- Ptosis from congenital causes, third nerve palsy, Horner syndrome, myasthenia gravis, senile change, or myotonic dystrophy
- Myokymia or blepharospasm

### Rare But Important

- Malignant eyelid growth, especially basal cell carcinoma
- Benign eyelid growth
- Dacrocystitis
- Alopecia affecting lashes
- Molluscum contagiosum
- Eyelash lice

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about pain, duration, recurrent episodes, visible swelling, watering, itch, discharge, crusting, vision change, diplopia, fever, rash, and contact lens use.
- Inspect lid margins, lashes, conjunctiva, lacrimal area, skin lesions, and lid position.
- Check for lash loss, lid ulceration, pearly rolled edge, distortion of lid margin, or recurrent unilateral blepharitis.
- In ptosis, ask about variation through the day, diplopia, headache, pupil symptoms, diabetes, trauma, and neurological symptoms.

## Investigations

- Lipid profile is appropriate when xanthelasma is identified and cholesterol status is unknown.
- Urinalysis for proteinuria, FBC, and liver function or protein status may be needed for systemic oedema.
- Ptosis may require specialist testing, imaging, chest X-ray, or myasthenia assessment depending on the pattern.
- Biopsy or urgent ophthalmology referral is needed when malignancy is possible.

## Red Flags

- Orbital cellulitis: fever, proptosis, painful or restricted eye movements, reduced vision, or systemic illness.
- Bilateral ptosis that worsens through the day: consider myasthenia gravis.
- New unilateral ptosis requires investigation, from diabetes to malignancy and aneurysmal causes.
- Eyelash loss can be a poor prognostic sign in alopecia and may also indicate eyelid tumour.
- Entropion and trichiasis are easily missed in older adults and may damage the cornea.
- Unilateral lash loss or recurrent unilateral blepharitis can indicate tumour.

## Top Tips

- Myokymia is usually harmless but can be very distressing.
- Check whether cholesterol has already been measured when xanthelasma is found.
- Do not ignore a "stye" that does not resolve or repeatedly recurs in the same location.
