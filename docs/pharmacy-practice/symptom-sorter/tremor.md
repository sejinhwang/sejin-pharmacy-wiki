# Tremor

## Overview

Tremor is a rhythmic involuntary movement of a body part. The main clinical patterns are rest tremor, postural tremor, and intention tremor.

Patients often worry about Parkinson's disease, so the assessment should be calm, specific, and direct about the pattern.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Anxiety
- Thyrotoxicosis
- Withdrawal from alcohol, benzodiazepines, or opioids
- Essential tremor
- Parkinson's disease

### Occasional

- Medicine adverse effects, including antipsychotics, phenothiazines, antiemetics, and beta-agonists
- Carbon dioxide retention in COPD
- Multiple sclerosis
- Cerebellar pathology, including tumour, stroke, abscess, or acoustic neuroma

### Rare But Important

- Fulminant hepatic failure with asterixis
- Wilson disease
- Tertiary syphilis
- Functional tremor
- Meningoencephalitis

## Clinical Assessment

- Identify whether tremor is worst at rest, with posture, or during voluntary movement.
- Ask about acute onset, gradual onset, stress, caffeine, alcohol response, withdrawal risk, and medicine changes.
- Ask about palpitations, weight loss, heat intolerance, falls, slowness, stiffness, unsteadiness, dysarthria, diplopia, sleepiness, headache, and confusion.
- Review antipsychotics, metoclopramide, beta-agonists, alcohol use, and benzodiazepine use.
- Observe tremor at rest, with arms outstretched, and during action.
- Examine heart rate, bradykinesia, rigidity, gait, nystagmus, dysarthria, limb ataxia, and respiratory status if CO2 retention is possible.

## Investigations

- Thyroid function tests are a low-threshold first-line test.
- FBC can show macrocytosis suggesting chronic alcohol use.
- Liver function tests help assess alcohol-related liver disease or hepatic failure.
- MRI brain, lumbar puncture, visual evoked potentials, blood gases, caeruloplasmin or copper testing, and syphilis serology are specialist or case-dependent tests.

## Red Flags

- Intention tremor with nystagmus, dysarthria, ataxia, or other cerebellar signs.
- Parkinsonism with frequent falls, mask-like facies, micrographia, bradykinesia, festinant gait, or difficulty rising from a chair.
- Suspected alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal.
- Altered mental state, fever, meningism, or acute focal neurology.
- Asterixis with systemic illness or liver failure concern.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Review beta-agonists, antipsychotics, antiemetics, stimulants, lithium, valproate, and recent withdrawal from sedatives or alcohol.
- Withdrawal states can be life-threatening and should not be managed as a benign tremor.
- If tremor affects function, a targeted medicines review may be more useful than repeated broad investigations.
