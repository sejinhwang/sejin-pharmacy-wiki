# Erythema

## Overview

Erythema is persistent skin redness from superficial blood vessel dilation. Distinguish it from transient flushing and from non-blanching purpura.

First decide whether the erythema is localised or generalised, tender or itchy, blanching or non-blanching, and whether the patient is systemically unwell.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Cellulitis or erysipelas
- Gout around a hot joint
- Burns or sunburn
- Drug or infection exanthem
- Rosacea

### Occasional

- Palmar erythema from pregnancy, liver disease, or thyrotoxicosis
- Phototoxic reaction
- Venous stasis or immobility erythema
- Erythema multiforme
- SLE
- Erythema ab igne

### Rare But Important

- Fixed drug eruption
- Livedo reticularis
- Acute HIV seroconversion rash
- Erythema nodosum
- Erythema migrans from Lyme disease

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about onset, progression, pain, itch, fever, sore throat, coryza, joint symptoms, tick exposure, travel, sexual risk, pregnancy, and medicines.
- Review new medicines or dose increases in the last 1 to 8 weeks.
- Examine vitals, blanching, warmth, tenderness, swelling, lymphangitis, joint involvement, vesicles, bullae, target lesions, reticular patterns, and nodules.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Cellulitis | Gout | Burns | Drug/infection exanthem | Rosacea |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fever/systemic upset | Often | Possible | No | Possible | No |
| Pustules | No | No | No | No | Possible |
| Periarticular | Possible | Yes | Possible | Possible | No |
| Blisters | Possible | No | Possible | Possible | No |
| Widespread | No | No | Possible | Yes | No |

## Investigations

- Serum urate when gout is possible.
- FBC, ESR, CRP, LFTs, or TFTs according to context.
- Autoimmune or infection testing only when clinically indicated.

## Red Flags

- Fever, rigors, hypotension, or rapidly progressive rash.
- Non-blanching rash with systemic symptoms.
- Mucosal involvement or blistering.
- Suspected severe drug reaction.
- Erythema migrans with neurological or cardiac symptoms.

## Pharmacist Notes

- Common rash culprits include antibiotics, NSAIDs, anticonvulsants, and allopurinol.
- Ask about photosensitisers when sun exposure is relevant.
- Document suspected medicine reactions and avoid re-challenge when serious reaction is possible.
