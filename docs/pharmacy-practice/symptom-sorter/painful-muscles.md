# Painful Muscles

## Overview

Painful muscles, or myalgia, has many causes. The history should distinguish muscle pain from joint pain, pain from weakness, and benign overuse from systemic or medicine-related disease.

Weakness, dark urine, systemic symptoms, or proximal pain in an older adult changes urgency.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Overuse or strain injury
- Acute viral illness
- Depression, anxiety, and stress-related somatisation
- Polymyalgia rheumatica
- Statin-associated muscle symptoms

### Occasional

- Vitamin D deficiency
- Referred pain from joint pathology
- Fibromyalgia or nociplastic pain
- Chronic fatigue syndrome
- Connective tissue disease
- Peripheral arterial disease with intermittent claudication
- Neuropathy
- Hypothyroidism
- Medicines or substances, including statins, fibrates, chemotherapy, lithium, and withdrawal states

### Rare But Important

- Polymyositis or dermatomyositis
- Underlying malignancy
- Guillain-Barre syndrome
- Rhabdomyolysis

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about sudden versus gradual onset, intermittent versus persistent symptoms, localised versus generalised pain, proximal versus distal distribution, cramp-like, aching, or burning quality.
- Screen for weakness: difficulty climbing stairs, rising from a chair, or lifting arms.
- Ask about fever, systemic upset, weight loss, headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms, dark urine, recent infection, new exercise, heavy exertion, trauma, heat illness, and dehydration.
- Review statins, dose changes, interacting medicines such as macrolides or azoles, fibrates, alcohol, recreational substances, CKD, and liver disease.
- Examine temperature, pulse, blood pressure, focal tenderness, diffuse tenderness, joint swelling, warmth, range of motion, proximal power, reflexes, and sensation.

| Morning stiffness | Possible | Possible | No | Yes | No |
| Vague or generalised symptoms | No | Possible | Yes | No | Possible |

## Investigations

- FBC and ESR or CRP are common first-line tests when inflammatory disease, infection, malignancy, or PMR is possible.
- Urinalysis is useful when rhabdomyolysis is suspected.
- Thyroid function, liver function, glucose or HbA1c, creatine kinase, vitamin D, and autoimmune tests are selected according to the pattern.
- Electromyography, muscle biopsy, and imaging are specialist or case-dependent tests.

## Red Flags

- Muscle pain with significant or progressive weakness.
- Dark urine with systemic symptoms, suggesting rhabdomyolysis.
- Older adult with proximal aching, morning stiffness, systemic symptoms, or symptoms of giant cell arteritis.
- Persistent symptoms with abnormal tests, inflammatory myopathy, or malignancy concern.
- Progressive weakness or ascending symptoms suggesting Guillain-Barre syndrome.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- If symptoms relate temporally to a statin, consider statin-associated muscle symptoms and review interacting medicines.
- Avoid high-risk combinations such as statin plus gemfibrozil when possible.
- Escalate urgently if there is severe weakness, markedly raised CK if known, or dark urine.
- PMR and giant cell arteritis should be clinician-led; always screen for headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, and visual symptoms.
