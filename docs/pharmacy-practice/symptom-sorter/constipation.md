# Constipation

## Overview

Constipation is infrequent or difficult evacuation of faeces. Normal bowel frequency varies widely, so clarify whether the patient means reduced frequency, hard stools, straining, difficulty evacuating, or incomplete emptying.

Most cases have several contributing factors, and serious causes are uncommon, but new or changing constipation in an older adult deserves careful red-flag screening.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Diet and lifestyle, including inadequate fibre or ignoring the urge to defecate
- Inactivity, especially in older adults
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Painful perianal conditions, including fissure, haemorrhoids, abscess, or florid warts
- Drug-related constipation, including opioids, iron, and aluminium hydroxide

### Occasional

- Poor fluid intake
- Acquired megacolon from chronic laxative abuse, neurological disease, or scleroderma
- Diverticulosis with or without stricture
- Hypothyroidism
- Rectal or colonic carcinoma

### Rare But Important

- Extracolonic pelvic mass
- Acute bowel obstruction
- Hypercalcaemia
- Crohn's disease with stricture
- In children: stool holding behaviour or Hirschsprung's disease

## Clinical Assessment

### History

- Clarify stool frequency, stool form, straining, incomplete emptying, and manual manoeuvres.
- Ask whether symptoms are short-lived or long-standing.
- Ask about any change from baseline bowel habit.
- Screen for abdominal pain, bloating, nausea, vomiting, rectal bleeding, mucus, overflow diarrhoea, and weight loss.
- Review fibre, fluid intake, activity level, and ignoring the urge to defecate.
- Review prescription, OTC, and supplement use, especially opioids, iron, aluminium antacids, antimuscarinics, verapamil, and ondansetron.
- In children, ask about stool withholding, abdominal distension, and delayed passage of meconium if relevant.

### Examination

- Look for general clues such as hypothyroid features or cachexia.
- Assess abdominal distension and tenderness.
- Visible peristalsis and audible borborygmi are not simple constipation; consider obstruction.
- Inspect the perianal area when appropriate.
- Rectal examination may identify faecal loading, marked pain from fissure or perianal disease, or an empty rectum in a child with possible Hirschsprung's disease.

## Investigations

### Straightforward Cases

- No tests are usually needed when history and examination support uncomplicated constipation.

### When Significant Pathology Is Suspected

- FBC
- Endoscopic evaluation, such as sigmoidoscopy or colonoscopy
- Contrast imaging as indicated

### Case-Dependent

- Urinalysis; high specific gravity may suggest poor fluid intake.
- Thyroid function tests.
- Serum calcium.
- Abdominal X-ray if megacolon or obstruction is suspected.
- Ultrasound or CT if a pelvic mass is suspected.
- Biopsy for suspicious lesions or suspected Hirschsprung's disease.

## Red Flags

- Older adult with constipation plus weight loss, rectal bleeding, mucus, diarrhoea, or marked bowel habit change.
- Abdominal pain attributed to constipation when there is visible peristalsis, borborygmi, significant vomiting, or obstruction concern.
- Child with chronic constipation, persistently swollen abdomen, and empty rectum.
- New constipation in someone with known diverticular disease or IBS; do not assume the old diagnosis explains the new pattern.

## Top Tips

- Clarify what the patient means by constipation.
- A longer history is less likely to have one reversible cause.
- Check the full medicines history, including OTC products.
- First impression matters: hypothyroidism, malignancy, dehydration, or frailty may be visible before the details are complete.

## Pharmacist And Medicines Optimisation Notes

- Prevent opioid-induced constipation when regular opioids are started.
- Consider constipating medicines before escalating laxatives indefinitely.
- Avoid oral laxative escalation when obstruction is suspected.
- Constipation can worsen delirium, urinary retention, pain, and falls risk in older adults.

## Related Conditions

### IBS-C

Constipation-predominant IBS causes constipation, abdominal pain, and bloating. Alarm features must be excluded before using an IBS label.

### Bowel Obstruction Or Ileus

True obstruction can present as constipation with distension. Obstipation, vomiting, visible peristalsis, severe pain, peritonism, or systemic toxicity requires urgent imaging and surgical assessment.

### Colorectal Cancer

New or progressive constipation may reflect colonic lesion or stricture, especially in older adults. Weight loss, iron deficiency anaemia, persistent bowel habit change, or rectal bleeding requires urgent evaluation.

### Opioid-Induced Constipation

Opioids commonly cause constipation. Start a bowel regimen when regular opioids are initiated and reassess if constipation persists.

### Perianal Disease

Painful fissures, haemorrhoids, abscesses, or warts can cause stool withholding and worsening constipation. Severe pain, fever, or rapidly progressive swelling needs urgent assessment.

### Abdominal Swelling

Faecal loading and gas trapping can cause bloating and distension. Persistent distension after bowel clearance should prompt reassessment.

### Urinary Retention

Rectal loading can worsen urinary symptoms and retention. Treating constipation may improve urinary symptoms, but persistent retention needs urologic or neurologic evaluation.

### Endocrine And Metabolic Causes

Hypothyroidism and hypercalcaemia can cause constipation. Consider TSH and calcium when constipation is persistent, unexplained, or associated with systemic features.
