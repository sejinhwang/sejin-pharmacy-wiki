# Absent Periods

## Overview

Absent periods are anxiety-provoking. The first missed period suggests pregnancy until proven otherwise, while prolonged amenorrhoea raises endocrine, functional, medication-related, and structural possibilities.

The priorities are to exclude pregnancy, distinguish functional hypothalamic causes from endocrine or structural disease, and identify pituitary, eating disorder, or systemic red flags.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Pregnancy
- Functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea from rapid weight loss, stress, or exercise
- Perimenopause, menopause, or premature ovarian insufficiency
- Polycystic ovary syndrome
- Medicines, including dopamine antagonists, valproate, antiepileptics, and cytotoxics

### Occasional

- Thyroid disease
- Eating disorder
- Severe systemic illness
- Contraception-related amenorrhoea

### Rare But Important

- Adrenal disorders
- Sheehan syndrome
- Pituitary tumour or prolactinoma
- Ovarian tumours
- Structural or chromosomal causes of primary amenorrhoea
- Pituitary failure

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about last menstrual period, pregnancy risk, contraception adherence, unprotected intercourse, emergency contraception, and recent pregnancy or miscarriage.
- Clarify duration of amenorrhoea and whether cycles were previously regular.
- Ask about weight loss, exercise, stress, low mood, eating patterns, vasomotor symptoms, acne, hirsutism, galactorrhoea, libido, headache, visual change, and thyroid symptoms.
- Review medicines that raise prolactin, affect ovulation, or are teratogenic.
- Examine weight, BMI, signs of undernutrition, acne, hirsutism, thyroid enlargement, and breast signs if galactorrhoea is present.

## Investigations

- Pregnancy test is the essential first test.
- FBC and U&E are selected when systemic illness, low weight, or broader illness is possible.
- Thyroid function tests.
- Prolactin.
- FSH and LH.
- Testosterone when PCOS or androgen excess is suspected.
- Pelvic ultrasound for PCOS, ovarian pathology, or structural concern.
- Pituitary imaging is specialist-led when prolactinoma or pituitary disease is suspected.

## Red Flags

- Do not accept "no chance of pregnancy" without testing when pregnancy is clinically possible.
- Eating disorders may be hidden despite reassuring explanations.
- Early morning headache with visual disturbance suggests pituitary pathology and needs urgent assessment.
- Before attributing amenorrhoea to weight loss, exclude thyrotoxicosis and systemic disease.
- Premature ovarian insufficiency should be confirmed and managed rather than dismissed as early menopause.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Exclude pregnancy before advising on teratogenic medicine changes, especially valproate or other high-risk medicines.
- Review prolactin-raising medicines such as antipsychotics, metoclopramide, and domperidone.
- Contraception-related amenorrhoea can be benign, but pregnancy testing is still needed when indicated.

## Related Conditions

- Pregnancy with bleeding or pain
- PCOS
- Thyroid disease
- Pituitary disease and hyperprolactinaemia
- Weight loss, systemic illness, and eating disorders
- Infertility
