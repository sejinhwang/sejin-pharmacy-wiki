# Abnormal Gait In Adults

## Overview

Abnormal gait may be noticed by the patient, family, or clinician. The presenting complaint may be unsteadiness, pain, weakness, dragging a foot, or being unable to walk as far as usual.

The first job is to separate a painful limp from ataxia, parkinsonian gait, vascular claudication, and time-critical neurological disease.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Trauma affecting the back or leg
- Osteoarthritis or another painful joint disorder
- Vestibular ataxia
- Parkinson's disease
- Intermittent claudication

### Occasional

- Foot drop
- Multiple sclerosis
- L5 or S1 radiculopathy
- Cauda equina syndrome
- Myasthenia gravis

### Rare But Important

- Tabes dorsalis
- Myotonic dystrophy
- Motor neurone disease
- Cerebellar ataxia
- Functional gait disorder

## Clinical Assessment

- Establish the time course: sudden, episodic, progressive, or longstanding.
- Ask about pain, especially hip, knee, ankle, back, and calf pain on exertion.
- Ask about weakness, foot drop, paraesthesia, numbness, optic neuritis, fatigable weakness, vertigo, falls, and loss of balance.
- Screen for cauda equina symptoms: saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, urinary incontinence, faecal incontinence, and bilateral leg symptoms.
- Observe gait directly if possible, including turning, tandem gait if safe, and walking a short corridor.
- Examine the nervous system, spine, painful joints, and peripheral pulses.

## Ready Reckoner

| Feature | Trauma | Osteoarthritis | Vestibular Ataxia | Parkinsonism | Claudication |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sudden onset | Yes | Possible | Yes | No | Possible |
| Painful unilateral limp | Yes | Yes | No | No | Yes |
| Worse on exercise | Yes | Yes | No | No | Yes |
| Shuffling gait | No | Possible | No | Yes | No |
| Staggering gait | No | No | Yes | No | No |

## Investigations

- No investigation is needed when a benign cause is clear and risk is low.
- X-ray may be useful after trauma or when osteoarthritis is suspected.
- FBC, ESR, CRP, rheumatoid factor, and uric acid are selected according to the joint pattern.
- CT or MRI is used when central neurological disease, radiculopathy, spinal cord compression, or cauda equina is possible.
- Lumbar puncture and syphilis serology are specialist investigations for selected cases.
- Vascular imaging is used when claudication or arterial disease is suspected.

## Red Flags

- Persistent ataxia after presumed vestibular neuritis.
- Back pain with bilateral leg symptoms, saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, or urinary incontinence.
- Ataxia with previous neurological episodes, optic neuritis, or other features suggesting multiple sclerosis.
- New or progressive focal neurological signs.
- Rapidly worsening gait, falls, or inability to walk safely.

## Pharmacist Practice Notes

- Review sedatives, hypnotics, anticholinergic burden, opioids, alcohol, antihypertensives, and medicines causing postural hypotension.
- Check for extrapyramidal effects from antipsychotics, metoclopramide, prochlorperazine, and related medicines.
- A falls-risk medicine review is often clinically useful even when the gait cause is not purely drug-related.
