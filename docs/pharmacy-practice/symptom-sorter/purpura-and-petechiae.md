# Purpura And Petechiae

## Overview

Petechiae are pinpoint non-blanching red or purple macules. Purpura are larger non-blanching lesions, and ecchymoses are larger bruise-like patches.

Confirm non-blanching, assess whether the patient is systemically unwell, and identify medicine, haematological, infective, and vasculitic causes.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Trauma
- Senile purpura
- Liver disease
- Raised intravascular pressure from coughing, vomiting, or straining
- Anticoagulants, antiplatelets, or systemic steroids

### Occasional

- Vasculitis
- Thrombocytopenia
- Bone marrow disease
- Renal failure
- Infective endocarditis

### Rare But Important

- Meningococcal septicaemia
- DIC
- Inherited bleeding disorders
- Severe vitamin C or K deficiency
- Paraproteinaemia

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about onset, distribution, bleeding symptoms, fever, headache, meningism, weight loss, night sweats, infection, travel, trauma, and medicines.
- Examine vitals, distribution, palpable purpura, lymph nodes, hepatosplenomegaly, murmur, and sepsis signs.
- In children, consider non-accidental injury when the story or distribution is concerning.

## Investigations

- FBC, ESR/CRP, and INR if on warfarin.
- LFTs, U&E, coagulation screen, plasma electrophoresis, or autoimmune testing according to context.
- Skin biopsy or bone marrow work-up through specialist pathways when indicated.

## Red Flags

- Fever plus non-blanching rash.
- Rapid progression, hypotension, meningism, or altered mental state.
- Pale unwell patient with purpura.
- Significant inherited bleeding disorder concern.
- Child safeguarding concern.

## Pharmacist Notes

- Review anticoagulants, antiplatelets, steroids, chemotherapy, and interacting medicines.
- Escalate major bleeding or high-risk features promptly.
- Avoid IM injections if serious bleeding disorder is suspected.
