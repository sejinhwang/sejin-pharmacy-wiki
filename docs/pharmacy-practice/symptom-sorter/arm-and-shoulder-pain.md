# Arm And Shoulder Pain

## Overview

Arm and shoulder pain may be local musculoskeletal pain, cervical radiculopathy, nerve entrapment, or referred pain. Angina can present as arm pain without prominent chest pain.

This page focuses on arm-specific and referred pain rather than generalized arthritis, polymyalgia, or peripheral neuropathy.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Muscle strain or sprain
- Epicondylitis
- Subacromial bursitis or adhesive capsulitis
- Cervical spondylosis
- Angina

### Occasional

- Bicipital tendinitis
- Acromioclavicular or sternoclavicular joint pain
- Calcific tendinitis
- De Quervain tenosynovitis
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Cervical rib or thoracic outlet syndrome
- Brachial or ulnar neuralgia, including post-herpetic neuralgia
- Cervical or thoracic disc disease

### Rare But Important

- GORD mimicking chest or arm discomfort
- Local bone, spine, cord, or lung malignancy
- Subclavian aneurysm
- Multiple sclerosis
- Syphilitic aortitis
- Thoracic outlet vascular compromise

## Clinical Assessment

- Localise the pain: shoulder, upper arm, elbow, forearm, wrist, hand, or diffuse.
- Ask whether movement of the arm, neck, or hand reproduces pain.
- Ask about exertional symptoms, breathlessness, sweating, nausea, chest pressure, and cardiovascular risk.
- Ask about paraesthesia, numbness, weakness, neck pain, rash, trauma, fever, weight loss, and smoking history.
- Examine active and passive shoulder movement, resisted movements, local tenderness, neck movement, neurological function, pulses, and chest signs when indicated.

## Investigations

- No tests are needed for many clear mechanical presentations.
- FBC, ESR, and CRP are selected when inflammatory disease, infection, or malignancy is possible.
- Thyroid function may be relevant when carpal tunnel syndrome or myxoedema is suspected.
- ECG and cardiac work-up are required if angina or acute coronary syndrome is possible.
- Nerve conduction studies help confirm entrapment neuropathy.
- Chest X-ray is used when an apical lung tumour is possible.
- MRI, CT, lumbar puncture, or syphilis serology are specialist tests in selected neurological or rare causes.

## Red Flags

- Arm pain that may represent angina, especially if exertional or associated with autonomic symptoms.
- Persistent paraesthesia with weakness.
- Severe arm or shoulder pain in a smoker, especially with neurological signs, raising concern for Pancoast tumour.
- Severe persistent pain with unrestricted movement and no clear diagnosis.
- Trauma with deformity, neurovascular symptoms, or suspected fracture.

## Top Tips

- Patients often worry about angina or stroke; a clear explanation of the pattern helps.
- Subacromial bursitis, adhesive capsulitis, and epicondylitis may last longer than patients expect.
- Localising the pain accurately is usually more useful than ordering broad tests early.
