# Numbness And Paraesthesiae

## Overview

Numbness and paraesthesiae include tingling, pins and needles, altered sensation, and abnormal hot or cold sensation.

The priorities are to exclude stroke or TIA, spinal cord compression, and progressive neurological disease, then localise the pattern to peripheral nerve, root, cord, brain, or systemic neuropathy.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Anxiety or hyperventilation
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Sciatica
- Diabetic neuropathy
- Cervical spondylosis

### Occasional

- Multiple sclerosis or dorsal myelitis
- Polyneuropathy from alcohol, B12 or folate deficiency, medicines, metabolic disease, connective tissue disease, malignancy, Guillain-Barre syndrome, or leprosy
- Stroke or TIA
- Trauma or compression
- Migraine with focal symptoms

### Rare But Important

- Spinal cord tumour
- Syringomyelia
- Sensory cortex trauma, tumour, or epilepsy
- Functional neurological disorder
- Vascular disease, including IHD or peripheral vascular disease

## Clinical Assessment

- Map the distribution: single peripheral nerve, nerve root, one limb, whole side of body, stocking-glove, both legs, sensory level, or saddle area.
- Establish time course: sudden, episodic, progressive, nocturnal, exertional, or positional.
- Ask about pain, weakness, visual symptoms, vertigo, speech change, sphincter disturbance, gait change, and falls.
- Ask about anxiety, panic, hyperventilation, diabetes, alcohol, diet, bariatric surgery, B12 or folate risk, vascular risk, and medicines.
- Review medicines associated with neuropathy or deficiency, including neurotoxic medicines and long-term metformin or PPI use.
- Examine power, reflexes, sensation, coordination, gait, upper motor neurone signs, sensory level, and saddle sensation when relevant.

## Investigations

- Urinalysis and glucose or HbA1c for diabetes assessment.
- FBC, LFTs, U&E, calcium, B12, folate, and thyroid function tests for systemic causes.
- Nerve conduction studies for entrapment or peripheral neuropathy.
- MRI or CT of brain or spine when central or compressive disease is suspected.
- Lumbar puncture, carotid or vascular testing, and autoimmune investigations are specialist tests in selected cases.

## Red Flags

- Sudden neurological symptoms suggesting TIA or stroke.
- Progressive bilateral leg symptoms with sphincter disturbance or saddle anaesthesia.
- Constant progressive paraesthesia with objective neurological signs.
- Varying neurological distributions with visual symptoms, ataxia, or other features suggesting multiple sclerosis.
- Rapidly worsening weakness or ascending symptoms.

## Top Tips

- Intermittent perioral tingling strongly suggests hyperventilation, but only after dangerous causes fit poorly.
- Thenar wasting suggests clinically significant carpal tunnel syndrome.
- Migraine aura with focal symptoms in a patient using combined hormonal contraception needs medical review.
