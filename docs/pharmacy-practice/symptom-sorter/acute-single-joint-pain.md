# Acute Single Joint Pain

## Overview

Acute pain in one joint is common. Trauma is the most likely explanation, but gout, pseudogout, haemarthrosis, inflammatory arthritis, and septic arthritis must stay in view.

The knee is a common site. In older adults, an osteoarthritis flare is also common.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Common

- Osteoarthritis flare
- Traumatic synovitis
- Gout or pseudogout
- Chondromalacia or anterior knee pain
- Traumatic haemarthrosis

### Occasional

- Fracture
- Reactive arthritis
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Psoriatic arthritis
- Patellar tendinitis or Osgood-Schlatter disease

### Rare But Important

- Septic arthritis
- Haemophilia
- Tropical infection
- Malignancy
- Avascular necrosis
- Recurrent subluxation

## Clinical Assessment

- Ask about trauma, twisting injury, direct blow, and ability to weight-bear.
- Clarify speed of swelling. Rapid swelling after injury suggests haemarthrosis and significant internal damage.
- Ask about fever, rigors, malaise, skin infection, immunosuppression, prosthetic joint, and injection drug use.
- Ask about previous attacks, alcohol, dehydration, diuretics, renal disease, psoriasis, urethritis, diarrhoea, eye symptoms, and multiple joint symptoms.
- Examine for heat, redness, effusion, deformity, bony tenderness, range of movement, and neurovascular compromise.

## Investigations

- Many minor traumatic or mechanical presentations need no immediate investigation.
- FBC, ESR, and CRP help assess inflammatory or infective possibilities.
- Serum uric acid is more useful after the acute attack than during it.
- X-ray is used when fracture, osteoarthritis, avascular necrosis, or malignancy is possible.
- Joint aspiration is needed when pus, blood, or crystals must be identified.
- Rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP are selected when rheumatoid arthritis is suspected.
- Clotting studies are used if haemophilia or bleeding tendency is possible.
- Arthroscopy may be needed after traumatic haemarthrosis.

## Red Flags

- Hot, red, intensely painful joint with marked movement restriction or systemic illness: exclude septic arthritis urgently.
- Rapid haemarthrosis after trauma: suspect significant internal injury and refer promptly.
- Septic arthritis may be easy to miss in a patient with rheumatoid arthritis.
- Young man with non-traumatic knee monoarthritis may have reactive arthritis.
- Neurovascular compromise, open injury, or inability to bear weight after trauma needs urgent assessment.

## Top Tips

- Gout is often exquisitely painful and may cause slight fever.
- Septic arthritis can look like gout but often has more marked movement restriction and systemic illness.
- Autoimmune blood tests can mislead if the clinical pattern does not fit.
- Look carefully for psoriasis, urethritis, conjunctivitis, or recent gastrointestinal infection.
