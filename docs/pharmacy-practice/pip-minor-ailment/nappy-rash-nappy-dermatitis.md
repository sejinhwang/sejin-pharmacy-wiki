# Nappy Rash Nappy Dermatitis

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Nappy rash, or nappy dermatitis, is an irritant dermatitis in the nappy area. It is commonly related to prolonged contact with urine/stool, friction, moisture, diarrhoea, occlusion, and skin barrier disruption. Secondary candidal infection or bacterial infection may complicate the presentation.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- rash location and distribution: convex surfaces, skin folds, perianal area, genital area, thighs, or buttocks
- erythema, papules, erosions, satellite lesions, scaling, maceration, weeping, crusting, or ulceration
- duration and progression
- pain, itch, bleeding, discharge, or distress with nappy changes
- recent diarrhoea, antibiotic use, new wipes/creams/nappies, or diet change
- nappy change frequency and barrier product use
- fever, systemic symptoms, oral thrush, or other rash sites

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and general wellbeing
- feeding, hydration, urine output, and stool pattern
- prematurity, immunocompromised status, or significant comorbidity
- recurrent or severe rash history
- current medicines and allergy status
- previous topical products tried and response
- carer ability to implement skin-care measures

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- infant/child appears systemically unwell, febrile, lethargic, dehydrated, or feeding poorly
- extensive erosions, ulceration, bleeding, severe pain, or rapidly worsening rash
- suspected bacterial infection: spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, purulence, honey-coloured crusting, or fever
- suspected candidal infection that is severe, recurrent, or not improving with appropriate measures
- rash extending beyond nappy area or diagnostic uncertainty
- recurrent, persistent, or treatment-refractory rash
- immunocompromised child or significant comorbidity
- safeguarding concern or inadequate ability to maintain basic skin care

Escalate urgently if severe systemic illness, dehydration, spreading cellulitis, or severe skin breakdown is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Skin-fold involvement | Fold involvement and satellite lesions may suggest Candida. |
| Convex surface erythema | Common in irritant nappy dermatitis. |
| Erosions/ulceration | Indicates more severe dermatitis and may require referral. |
| Fever/systemic symptoms | Suggests infection or another diagnosis. |
| Diarrhoea/antibiotics | Increases irritant and Candida risk. |
| Oral thrush | May support candidal involvement. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- irritant nappy dermatitis
- candidal nappy dermatitis
- bacterial infection or impetigo
- atopic dermatitis
- seborrhoeic dermatitis
- psoriasis
- allergic contact dermatitis
- scabies
- nutritional deficiency or other rare causes if severe/persistent
- non-accidental injury/safeguarding concerns if pattern is concerning

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- frequent nappy changes
- gentle cleansing with water or fragrance-free products
- allow nappy-free time where practical and safe
- pat dry rather than rubbing
- apply barrier product consistently
- avoid fragranced wipes, harsh soaps, and irritant products
- ensure nappies fit appropriately and are not overly tight
- manage diarrhoea/loose stool triggers where clinically relevant

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment/product selection should follow local policy, product monograph, child age, severity, suspected Candida or bacterial infection, skin integrity, allergy history, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild irritant nappy rash | Barrier care, frequent changes, and irritant avoidance. |
| Rash with satellite lesions/fold involvement | Consider candidal pathway according to local policy. |
| Broken skin, bleeding, severe pain, or spreading infection signs | Refer for medical assessment. |
| Persistent or recurrent rash | Refer for clinician review and diagnosis confirmation. |
| Systemically unwell child | Urgent assessment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel carers on:

- frequent nappy changes and gentle cleansing
- consistent barrier product use
- avoiding irritant/fragranced products
- nappy-free time where feasible
- not applying multiple medicated products without review
- recognising signs of Candida or bacterial infection
- seeking review if rash is severe, persistent, recurrent, or associated with systemic symptoms

## Safety-Netting

Advise medical assessment if:

- fever, lethargy, poor feeding, dehydration, or systemic illness develops
- rash becomes very painful, ulcerated, bleeding, or rapidly worse
- pus, spreading redness, warmth, swelling, or honey-coloured crusting occurs
- rash does not improve with appropriate skin-care measures
- rash recurs frequently or extends beyond the nappy area
- the child is immunocompromised or has significant comorbidity

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Nappy area rash: distribution [convex surfaces/folds/perianal], morphology [erythema/satellite lesions/erosion], duration [x], severity [x]. Red flags screened: fever/systemic illness, severe skin breakdown, bacterial infection, dehydration, immunocompromised status [summary].

**Impression:** Likely nappy dermatitis / candidal involvement considered / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Nappy care, barrier protection, and irritant avoidance counselling provided. Product advice, if any, selected according to local policy and child-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
