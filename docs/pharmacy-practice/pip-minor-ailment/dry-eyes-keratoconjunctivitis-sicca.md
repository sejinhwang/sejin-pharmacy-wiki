# Dry Eyes Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Dry eye disease, or keratoconjunctivitis sicca, is a multifactorial ocular surface condition associated with tear film instability, ocular discomfort, visual fluctuation, and possible ocular surface inflammation.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- dryness, grittiness, burning, stinging, foreign-body sensation, or irritation
- watery eyes or reflex tearing
- fluctuating vision, especially with reading/screen use
- redness, discharge, pain, photophobia, or visual change
- duration, recurrence, and triggers
- screen use, low humidity, wind exposure, contact lens use, or occupational triggers
- previous eye products tried and response

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- contact lens use
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- autoimmune disease, Sjogren's syndrome, thyroid eye disease, rosacea, or blepharitis history
- ocular surgery, laser eye procedure, glaucoma, or chronic eye disease
- medicines associated with dryness, e.g. anticholinergics, antihistamines, antidepressants, isotretinoin, diuretics, or beta-blockers
- current eye drops and preservative exposure
- allergy status and previous adverse reactions

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer urgently for medical or eye assessment if any of the following are present:

- eye pain rather than mild irritation
- photophobia
- reduced or changed vision
- unilateral severe red eye
- contact lens wearer with red eye or pain
- suspected foreign body, trauma, or chemical exposure
- purulent discharge or suspected infection
- corneal opacity, hazy cornea, or abnormal pupil if observed
- symptoms after recent eye surgery or procedure
- persistent or worsening symptoms despite appropriate measures

Escalate to A&E or urgent eye care if vision change, severe pain, trauma, chemical exposure, or contact-lens-associated keratitis is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Visual change | Any reduced vision is a referral trigger. |
| Pain and photophobia | Suggests keratitis, uveitis, trauma, glaucoma, or other serious pathology. |
| Contact lens use | Lowers referral threshold. |
| Bilateral chronic symptoms | More consistent with dry eye, especially with screen/environmental triggers. |
| Discharge | Purulent discharge suggests infection rather than simple dry eye. |
| Medicine history | Many systemic medicines can worsen ocular dryness. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- dry eye disease
- allergic conjunctivitis
- blepharitis or meibomian gland dysfunction
- viral or bacterial conjunctivitis
- contact lens-related keratitis
- corneal abrasion or foreign body
- uveitis
- acute glaucoma
- medication-related ocular dryness

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- reduce environmental triggers such as wind, smoke, or low humidity
- take screen breaks and blink consciously during prolonged screen use
- maintain eyelid hygiene if blepharitis features are present
- avoid rubbing the eyes
- review contact lens wear if symptoms are contact lens-related
- consider humidification or workplace/environment adjustments where practical

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, contact lens status, preservative sensitivity, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, ocular history, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild intermittent dry eye symptoms | Consider lubricant eye drop pathway where appropriate. |
| Frequent symptoms or preservative sensitivity | Consider preservative-free options where appropriate. |
| Blepharitis features | Include eyelid hygiene and consider clinician review if persistent. |
| Contact lens-related symptoms | Stop lens wear and refer if pain, redness, or visual change occurs. |
| Pain, photophobia, or vision change | Urgent referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- correct administration technique for eye drops or ointments
- avoiding contamination of bottle/dropper tips
- spacing different eye products where multiple products are used
- contact lens compatibility of selected products
- preservative sensitivity where symptoms persist
- environmental and screen-use measures
- red flags requiring urgent assessment

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical attention if:

- eye pain develops
- photophobia occurs
- vision changes or worsens
- redness becomes severe or unilateral
- contact lens wearer develops red/painful eye
- discharge becomes purulent
- symptoms worsen or do not improve despite appropriate measures

**Impression:** Likely dry eye disease / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Environmental, eye-care, and product-use counselling provided. Product advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
