# Cold Sores

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Cold sores are recurrent herpes simplex virus lesions, most commonly affecting the lips or perioral skin. They often begin with prodromal tingling, burning, itching, or discomfort followed by vesicles, crusting, and healing.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- lesion location: lip, perioral skin, nasal area, oral mucosa, eye-adjacent, or elsewhere
- stage: prodrome, vesicles, ulceration, crusting, or healing
- onset and duration
- pain, burning, tingling, itching, or swelling
- recurrence pattern and previous episodes
- triggers such as sun exposure, stress, fever, illness, trauma, or dental procedures
- systemic symptoms such as fever or malaise
- impact on eating, drinking, work, or social functioning

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- immunocompromised status
- atopic dermatitis or extensive skin disease
- ocular symptoms or lesions near the eye
- current medicines and allergies
- previous treatment response
- frequency and severity of recurrences
- possible first episode versus recurrent episode

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- eye pain, visual symptoms, photophobia, or lesions close to the eye
- widespread, severe, or rapidly progressive lesions
- immunocompromised status
- severe systemic symptoms or dehydration risk
- suspected primary herpetic gingivostomatitis, particularly if severe
- lesions not typical of cold sores or diagnostic uncertainty
- frequent, severe, or treatment-refractory recurrences
- lesions in infants or high-risk patients
- secondary bacterial infection: spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, pus, or worsening pain

Escalate urgently if ocular herpes or severe disseminated infection is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Lesion site | Eye-adjacent lesions need urgent caution. |
| Lesion stage | Early prodrome/vesicles may influence management timing. |
| Extent | Widespread lesions suggest higher-risk disease or alternative diagnosis. |
| Systemic symptoms | Fever, malaise, or poor intake may indicate more severe infection. |
| Immune status | Immunocompromised patients have lower referral threshold. |
| Signs of bacterial infection | Warmth, purulence, spreading redness, or increasing pain requires referral. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- angular cheilitis
- aphthous ulcer
- impetigo
- contact dermatitis
- shingles involving trigeminal distribution
- hand-foot-mouth disease
- oral candidiasis or other oral lesions
- traumatic ulcer
- drug-related mucocutaneous reaction if atypical or severe

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- avoid touching or picking lesions
- wash hands after contact or topical product application
- avoid kissing or sharing cups, cutlery, lip balm, razors, or towels during active lesions
- avoid oral contact with infants, immunocompromised people, or anyone at high risk while lesions are active
- use sun protection on lips if sun exposure is a trigger
- keep lesions clean and dry

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, renal function, immune status, symptom stage, previous response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Typical recurrent cold sore, early symptoms | Consider local protocol options where appropriate and time-sensitive. |
| Late crusted/healing lesion | Symptomatic care may be the main focus. |
| Frequent or severe recurrences | Refer for clinician review and prevention strategy discussion. |
| Eye-adjacent or ocular symptoms | Urgent referral. |
| Immunocompromised patient | Refer for medical assessment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- treatment is most useful when started early according to product/local guidance
- avoid contact spread while lesions are active
- hand hygiene and avoiding shared personal items
- not touching the eyes after touching lesions
- sun protection if sunlight is a trigger
- expected lesion course and when to seek reassessment
- avoiding unnecessary or prolonged topical products that irritate lesions

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- eye symptoms occur
- lesions spread, become severe, or do not heal as expected
- fever, dehydration, or systemic illness develops
- signs of bacterial infection occur
- recurrences become frequent or unusually severe
- the patient is immunocompromised, pregnant with concern, or otherwise high risk

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Lesions consistent with recurrent cold sores: site [x], stage [prodrome/vesicles/crusted], duration [x], recurrence history [x]. Red flags screened: [none / present], including ocular symptoms and immune status.

**Impression:** Likely recurrent herpes labialis / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Transmission prevention and trigger advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
