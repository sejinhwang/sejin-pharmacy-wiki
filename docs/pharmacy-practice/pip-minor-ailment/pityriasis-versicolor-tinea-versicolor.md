# Pityriasis Versicolor Tinea Versicolor

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Pityriasis versicolor, also called tinea versicolor, is a superficial Malassezia-associated skin condition causing hypo- or hyperpigmented patches with fine scale. It commonly affects the trunk, shoulders, neck, and upper arms.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- distribution: trunk, shoulders, upper arms, neck, or face
- colour change: hypopigmented, hyperpigmented, pink, tan, or brown patches
- fine scale, itch, or mild irritation
- duration, recurrence, and seasonal pattern
- sun exposure making pigment contrast more noticeable
- prior antifungal use and response
- sweating, humid environment, oily skin, or recurrence history

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- immunocompromised status
- extensive or recurrent disease
- current medicines and allergy status
- diagnostic uncertainty or concern about cosmetic impact/QoL

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- diagnostic uncertainty or atypical distribution
- widespread, recurrent, or treatment-refractory lesions
- significant inflammation, pain, blistering, ulceration, or secondary infection
- immunocompromised status with worsening rash
- rash associated with systemic symptoms
- concern for vitiligo, psoriasis, eczema, pityriasis rosea, drug eruption, or other diagnosis
- lesions involving high-risk areas or severe cosmetic/psychological distress requiring clinician review

Escalate urgently if severe systemic illness, rapidly progressive rash, or serious drug eruption is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Fine scale | Supports pityriasis versicolor when paired with typical distribution. |
| Pigment change | Hypo- or hyperpigmentation may persist after organism clearance. |
| Distribution | Trunk/shoulder/neck pattern is typical. |
| Recurrence | Common and should be addressed in counselling. |
| Inflammation/pain | Atypical; consider alternative diagnosis or referral. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- pityriasis versicolor
- vitiligo
- pityriasis alba
- pityriasis rosea
- psoriasis
- eczema or dermatitis
- tinea corporis
- post-inflammatory pigment change
- drug eruption if widespread or systemic features are present

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- reassure that pigment changes may take weeks to months to normalise after successful treatment
- manage sweating and occlusion where practical
- avoid sharing towels/clothing if other fungal infection is suspected
- recurrence can occur, particularly in warm/humid conditions
- avoid unnecessary topical corticosteroid use unless directed by a clinician

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, lesion extent, immune status, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Typical mild/localised disease | Consider local topical antifungal pathway where appropriate. |
| Widespread or recurrent disease | Consider clinician review or local recurrent disease pathway. |
| Diagnostic uncertainty | Refer or reassess before treatment. |
| Inflammatory, painful, or systemic features | Refer for medical assessment. |
| Pigment persists after scale resolves | Reassure and monitor; pigment recovery can lag behind treatment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- recurrence risk
- pigment changes may persist after treatment
- completing any selected treatment according to local/product instructions
- avoiding overuse of irritating products
- seeking reassessment if rash spreads, becomes painful/inflamed, or fails to improve
- sun exposure may make colour differences more noticeable

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- rash becomes painful, inflamed, blistered, or rapidly progressive
- systemic symptoms develop
- lesions are widespread, recurrent, or treatment-refractory
- pigment loss is sharply demarcated or progressive and diagnosis is uncertain
- the patient is immunocompromised and symptoms worsen
