# Nasal Congestion Acute Sinusitis

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Nasal congestion and acute sinusitis symptoms commonly occur with viral URTI, allergic rhinitis, or acute rhinosinusitis. Most acute presentations are self-limiting, but assessment must identify red flags, bacterial complication features, and patients requiring referral.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- nasal obstruction or congestion
- rhinorrhoea: clear, purulent, unilateral, or bloody
- facial pain or pressure
- headache
- fever or systemic symptoms
- post-nasal drip, cough, sore throat, or ear symptoms
- reduced smell or taste
- symptom duration and trajectory
- unilateral versus bilateral symptoms
- previous episodes or recurrent sinusitis

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- asthma, allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps, or chronic sinus disease
- immunocompromised status
- recent dental infection/procedure or facial trauma
- current medicines, including topical nasal decongestants and OTC cold products
- duration of topical nasal decongestant use, because prolonged use can cause rebound congestion
- allergy status and previous adverse reactions

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- severe or worsening facial pain, especially unilateral
- periorbital swelling, eye pain, visual disturbance, diplopia, or painful eye movement
- severe headache, neck stiffness, confusion, or neurological symptoms
- high or persistent fever with systemic illness
- immunocompromised status with worsening symptoms
- recurrent epistaxis or bloody unilateral discharge
- unilateral obstruction or suspected foreign body, polyp, or structural lesion
- symptoms persisting or worsening beyond expected self-limiting course
- suspected dental source or facial cellulitis

Escalate to A&E if orbital, neurological, or severe systemic features are present.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Temp | Fever or systemic illness assessment. |
| Facial tenderness/pressure | May support sinus involvement; severe or unilateral pain requires caution. |
| Nasal discharge | Clear discharge often viral/allergic; unilateral bloody/purulent discharge needs review. |
| Eye findings | Periorbital swelling, visual change, or painful eye movement are urgent red flags. |
| Duration/trajectory | Initial viral symptoms often improve gradually; worsening after initial improvement may suggest complication. |
| Decongestant use | Prolonged topical use may cause rhinitis medicamentosa. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- viral URTI
- allergic rhinitis
- acute rhinosinusitis
- chronic rhinosinusitis or nasal polyps
- rhinitis medicamentosa from topical nasal decongestant overuse
- dental infection
- migraine or other headache syndrome
- foreign body or structural nasal obstruction, especially if unilateral

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- hydration and rest
- saline nasal spray or irrigation
- humidification or steam-like comfort measures with burn-risk caution
- avoid smoke/vaping exposure and respiratory irritants
- avoid prolonged topical nasal decongestant use
- manage allergic triggers if allergic rhinitis features are present

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age restrictions, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, co-morbidities, renal/hepatic function, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Viral congestion without red flags | Supportive care and symptom monitoring. |
| Allergic pattern | Consider allergic rhinitis pathway and trigger management. |
| Significant congestion | Consider short-term symptomatic options where appropriate and safe. |
| Topical decongestant overuse | Consider rebound congestion and plan discontinuation/supportive care. |
| Severe, recurrent, unilateral, or complicated symptoms | Refer for clinician review. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- typical self-limiting course of viral nasal symptoms
- saline irrigation technique and device hygiene if used
- avoiding duplicate ingredients across combination cold products
- avoiding prolonged topical nasal decongestant use due to rebound congestion risk
- red flags requiring urgent medical assessment
- reassessment if symptoms worsen, persist, or become unilateral/severe

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- symptoms worsen after initial improvement
- severe unilateral facial pain or swelling develops
- eye pain, visual symptoms, periorbital swelling, or painful eye movement occurs
- severe headache, confusion, neck stiffness, or neurological symptoms occur
- fever persists or systemic illness develops
- symptoms persist beyond expected duration or recur frequently
