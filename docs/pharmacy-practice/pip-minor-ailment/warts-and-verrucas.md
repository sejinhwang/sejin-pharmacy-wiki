# Warts And Verrucas

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Warts and verrucas are benign epidermal lesions caused by human papillomavirus. Verrucas are plantar warts that occur on the soles of the feet and may be painful with pressure or walking.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- location, number, size, and duration of lesions
- pain, tenderness, bleeding, itching, or functional impact
- plantar pressure symptoms or walking difficulty
- appearance: rough hyperkeratotic surface, interrupted skin lines, thrombosed capillary dots, or flat lesions
- previous treatments tried and response
- spread to other sites or contacts with similar lesions
- occupational/sport exposure, communal showers, or barefoot activities

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, neuropathy, or history of foot ulceration
- immunocompromised status
- anticoagulant use or bleeding risk where relevant
- allergy status and current medicines
- ability to self-care safely and inspect treated area
- diagnostic uncertainty or atypical pigmented lesion

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical or podiatry assessment if any of the following are present:

- diabetes, neuropathy, peripheral vascular disease, or prior foot ulceration with foot lesion
- immunocompromised status with extensive or refractory lesions
- lesion is bleeding, ulcerated, rapidly changing, atypically pigmented, or diagnostically uncertain
- severe pain, functional impairment, or inability to weight bear
- facial, genital, periungual, or high-risk anatomical site according to local pathway
- signs of infection: increasing pain, warmth, swelling, discharge, or spreading erythema
- recurrent or treatment-refractory lesions requiring clinician assessment

Escalate urgently if diabetic foot infection, acute ischaemia, severe infection, or suspicious malignant lesion is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Interrupted skin lines | Supports wart/verruca rather than callus. |
| Thrombosed capillary dots | Typical but not always present. |
| Pain with lateral compression | May support verruca; pressure pain may overlap with corn/callus. |
| Foot risk status | Diabetes, neuropathy, and vascular disease change management. |
| Atypical pigmentation/bleeding | Requires referral and diagnostic caution. |
| Skin integrity | Broken skin or infection signs require referral. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- common wart
- plantar verruca
- corn or callus
- foreign body reaction
- molluscum contagiosum
- seborrhoeic keratosis
- melanoma or other suspicious lesion if atypical/pigmented/changing
- pressure lesion or diabetic foot ulcer

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- many warts resolve spontaneously over time
- avoid picking, biting, or scratching lesions
- avoid sharing towels, footwear, or nail tools
- cover verrucas in communal areas where appropriate
- wear footwear in communal showers/changing areas
- reduce pressure over painful plantar lesions
- high-risk feet should be managed by appropriate clinicians or foot-care professionals

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment/product selection should follow local policy, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, lesion site, skin integrity, diabetes/vascular/neuropathy status, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, immune status, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Low-risk patient with typical hand/foot wart | Conservative management or local self-care pathway may be appropriate. |
| Painful plantar verruca | Pressure offloading and local pathway where safe. |
| Diabetes, neuropathy, vascular disease, or impaired self-care | Refer; avoid self-treatment that may damage skin. |
| Atypical, bleeding, pigmented, or rapidly changing lesion | Refer for assessment before treatment. |
| Genital/facial/periungual high-risk site | Refer or follow specific local pathway. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- expected slow resolution and recurrence possibility
- avoiding aggressive filing or cutting, especially on feet
- not using medicated wart products on high-risk feet unless directed by a clinician
- hand hygiene and avoiding spread
- protecting surrounding skin if using any selected topical product according to local guidance
- seeking reassessment if pain, bleeding, infection, or diagnostic uncertainty occurs

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical or podiatry assessment if:

- lesion changes rapidly, bleeds, ulcerates, or becomes atypically pigmented
- severe pain or walking difficulty occurs
- infection signs develop
- there is diabetes, neuropathy, vascular disease, immunocompromised status, or prior foot ulceration
- lesion persists, spreads, or diagnosis is uncertain

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Lesion consistent with possible wart/verruca: location [x], size/number [x], duration [x], pain/function impact [x]. Red flags screened: diabetes/vascular/neuropathy risk, infection, atypical pigment/bleeding, high-risk site [summary].

**Impression:** Likely wart/verruca / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Natural history, spread prevention, and safe self-care advice provided. Product advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific risk factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
