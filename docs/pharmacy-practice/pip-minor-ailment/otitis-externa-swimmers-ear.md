# Otitis Externa Swimmers Ear

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Otitis externa, often called swimmer's ear, is inflammation of the external auditory canal. It may be infectious, irritant-related, eczematous, or associated with moisture, trauma, or ear canal instrumentation.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- ear pain, itch, fullness, discharge, reduced hearing, or tinnitus
- laterality: unilateral or bilateral
- duration and progression
- recent swimming, water exposure, earbud/hearing aid use, cotton bud use, scratching, or ear instrumentation
- fever, systemic symptoms, dizziness, vertigo, severe headache, facial weakness, or mastoid pain
- discharge character: watery, purulent, bloody, or foul-smelling
- previous episodes or chronic ear disease

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- diabetes or immunocompromised status
- eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis, or seborrhoeic disease affecting ear canal
- known tympanic membrane perforation, grommets/tubes, or ear surgery history
- hearing aid or earplug use
- current medicines and allergy status
- previous otic product use and response

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- severe ear pain, rapidly worsening symptoms, or pain out of proportion
- fever or systemic illness
- diabetes or immunocompromised status with suspected ear infection
- spreading cellulitis, pinna swelling, or periauricular erythema
- mastoid tenderness, swelling, or protruding ear
- facial weakness, vertigo, severe headache, or neurological symptoms
- suspected tympanic membrane perforation, grommet/tube, or significant ear surgery history
- blood discharge, foreign body, trauma, or sudden hearing loss
- recurrent, persistent, or treatment-refractory symptoms

Escalate urgently if malignant otitis externa, mastoiditis, facial nerve involvement, or severe systemic illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Tragal/pinna tenderness | Supports otitis externa but is not diagnostic alone. |
| Ear canal swelling/discharge | Can support diagnosis and indicate severity. |
| Hearing change | May occur with canal obstruction, but sudden/severe hearing loss needs referral. |
| Diabetes/immune status | Raises risk of severe infection and lowers referral threshold. |
| TM status | Perforation or grommet/tube affects product suitability and referral threshold. |
| Mastoid/facial/neurological signs | Urgent referral features. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- otitis externa
- acute otitis media
- foreign body
- cerumen impaction
- dermatitis/eczema of ear canal
- fungal otitis externa
- herpes zoster oticus
- mastoiditis
- temporomandibular joint pain or dental referred pain
- malignant otitis externa in high-risk patients

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- keep ear dry during acute symptoms
- avoid swimming until resolved according to local guidance
- avoid cotton buds, scratching, or ear canal trauma
- avoid inserting earbuds/earplugs if painful or worsening symptoms
- review hearing aid fit/hygiene if relevant
- seek assessment before using ear products if tympanic membrane perforation or grommet/tube is possible

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, tympanic membrane status, diabetes/immune status, severity, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, allergy history, previous response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild typical otitis externa without red flags | Consider local otitis externa pathway where appropriate. |
| Possible TM perforation, grommet/tube, or ear surgery history | Refer or follow specific local pathway. |
| Diabetes, immunocompromised status, or severe pain | Refer for medical assessment. |
| Fungal/recurrent/persistent symptoms | Refer for clinician review and possible microscopy/culture pathway. |
| Mastoid, facial nerve, vertigo, or systemic features | Urgent referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- keeping the ear dry and avoiding canal trauma
- not inserting cotton buds or objects into the ear canal
- correct use of any selected ear product according to local/product instructions
- warming ear drops in the hand before use if appropriate for comfort
- avoiding contamination of dropper tips
- expected reassessment timeframe and red flags
- seeking review if symptoms worsen or fail to improve

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- pain becomes severe or rapidly worsens
- fever or systemic illness develops
- swelling/redness spreads around the ear
- hearing worsens significantly or sudden hearing loss occurs
- dizziness, vertigo, facial weakness, severe headache, or mastoid pain occurs
- diabetes or immunocompromised status is present with worsening symptoms
- discharge is bloody, persistent, or foul-smelling
