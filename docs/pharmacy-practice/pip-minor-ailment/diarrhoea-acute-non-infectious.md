# Diarrhoea Acute Non-Infectious

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Acute diarrhoea is usually defined as loose or watery stools of recent onset. This page focuses on uncomplicated, likely non-infectious or self-limiting presentations where red flags have been excluded.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- duration of diarrhoea
- stool frequency and consistency
- watery versus bloody stool
- mucus or melaena
- abdominal pain, cramping, bloating, nausea, or vomiting
- fever or systemic symptoms
- hydration status and oral intake
- recent diet change, alcohol intake, or suspected food trigger
- recent travel, sick contacts, outbreaks, or high-risk food exposure
- recent antibiotic use or hospitalisation

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- renal disease, HF, IBD, diabetes, immunocompromised status, or significant comorbidity
- current medicines, including laxatives, metformin, magnesium, antibiotics, NSAIDs, colchicine, or other diarrhoea-associated medicines
- diuretic, ACE inhibitor, AII receptor blocker, SGLT2 inhibitor, lithium, or other medicines where dehydration may increase risk
- allergies and previous adverse reactions
- ability to maintain hydration and access care if worsening

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- blood in stool, melaena, or suspected GI bleeding
- severe or worsening abdominal pain
- persistent vomiting or inability to maintain oral fluids
- signs of dehydration, dizziness, syncope, oliguria, or marked weakness
- fever with systemic illness
- recent antibiotic exposure with significant diarrhoea
- immunocompromised status
- pregnancy with concerning symptoms
- older/frail patient with clinical deterioration
- diarrhoea persisting beyond expected self-limiting course
- recent travel with severe or persistent symptoms
- suspected outbreak or public health concern

Escalate to A&E if severe dehydration, shock, acute abdomen, significant GI bleeding, or severe systemic illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Temp | Fever may suggest infection or systemic illness. |
| HR and BP | Tachycardia or hypotension may suggest dehydration or sepsis. |
| Hydration status | Dry mucosa, reduced urine, dizziness, or poor intake increases risk. |
| Abdominal features | Severe focal pain, guarding, or distension requires referral. |
| Stool description | Blood, melaena, or persistent profuse watery diarrhoea requires caution. |
| Medicine review | Some medicines cause diarrhoea or become higher risk during dehydration. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- viral gastroenteritis
- food-related gastrointestinal upset
- medicine-induced diarrhoea
- antibiotic-associated diarrhoea
- traveller's diarrhoea
- inflammatory bowel disease flare
- irritable bowel syndrome
- lactose intolerance or other dietary intolerance
- GI bleeding or serious intra-abdominal pathology if red flags are present

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- maintain oral fluid intake
- use oral rehydration solution when dehydration risk is present
- eat as tolerated; avoid dehydration-promoting alcohol intake
- maintain hand hygiene and avoid preparing food for others while symptomatic where infectious cause is possible
- review high-risk medicines during significant dehydration according to local sick-day guidance

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age restrictions, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, co-morbidities, renal/hepatic function, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild self-limiting diarrhoea | Hydration and monitoring may be sufficient. |
| Dehydration risk | Consider oral rehydration approach and referral threshold. |
| Suspected medicine-induced diarrhoea | Review likely contributors and refer/communicate as appropriate. |
| Bloody stool, fever, severe pain, or recent antibiotic exposure | Refer rather than symptomatic-only management. |
| Persistent or recurrent diarrhoea | Refer for clinician review and investigation. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- hydration and signs of dehydration
- oral rehydration principles where relevant
- avoiding duplicate or inappropriate OTC products
- avoiding antimotility self-treatment when blood, fever, severe pain, or suspected invasive infection is present
- hygiene measures if infectious cause is possible
- seeking reassessment if symptoms persist, worsen, or red flags develop

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- blood or black stool occurs
- severe or worsening abdominal pain develops
- fever or systemic illness occurs
- vomiting prevents fluid intake
- reduced urination, dizziness, fainting, or severe weakness occurs
- diarrhoea persists beyond expected duration
- symptoms occur after antibiotics, travel, or high-risk exposure
- the patient is frail, pregnant, immunocompromised, or has significant comorbidity and is worsening

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Acute diarrhoea of [duration], stool frequency [x/day], associated symptoms [pain/vomiting/fever/blood absent or present]. Hydration status reviewed. Red flags screened: [none / present]. Medicines reviewed for diarrhoea contributors and dehydration-risk medicines.

**Impression:** Likely uncomplicated acute diarrhoea / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Hydration and oral rehydration advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
