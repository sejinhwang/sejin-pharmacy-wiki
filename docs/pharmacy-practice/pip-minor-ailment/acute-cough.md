# Acute Cough

Status: Standalone public draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Acute cough is cough lasting less than 3 weeks. It may be dry/non-productive or chesty/productive and is commonly associated with viral upper respiratory tract infection.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Complaint

Document:

- duration of cough
- dry/non-productive versus chesty/productive cough
- sputum amount, colour, and presence/absence of blood
- nocte symptoms or sleep disruption
- triggers such as exertion, cold air, allergens, lying flat, meals, smoke/vaping exposure, or occupational exposure
- associated coryzal symptoms, sore throat, sinus symptoms, hoarseness, fever, chest discomfort, wheeze, or SOB

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- asthma/COPD or other chronic respiratory disease
- HF, GORD, aspiration risk, immunocompromised status, or recent infection exposure
- smoking/vaping history
- current medicines, including OTC products and natural health products
- ACE inhibitor or other medicine that may contribute to cough
- allergies and previous adverse reactions

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for urgent medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- haemoptysis
- significant SOB, rapid breathing, or increased work of breathing
- cyanosis
- chest pain
- confusion or acute change in mental status
- persistent or high fever, rigors, or systemic toxicity
- hypoxia or clinically unstable observations
- suspected pneumonia or serious lower respiratory tract infection
- immunocompromised patient with worsening respiratory symptoms
- cough lasting more than 3 weeks
- unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or TB exposure risk
- persistent hoarseness or voice change, particularly in smokers or older adults

Escalate to A&E if the patient is acutely unwell.

## Focused Examination / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Parameter | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Temp | Fever or systemic infection screen. |
| RR | Tachypnoea may indicate lower respiratory tract involvement or clinical instability. |
| SpO2 | Hypoxia is a referral trigger. |
| HR and BP | General severity assessment. |
| Respiratory exam | Wheeze, crackles, focal findings, or increased work of breathing. |
| ENT findings | Coryzal symptoms, post-nasal drip, throat irritation, or sinus features. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- viral URTI
- acute bronchitis
- post-nasal drip / rhinitis
- asthma or COPD exacerbation
- GORD-related cough
- ACE inhibitor-associated cough
- pneumonia or other lower respiratory tract infection
- pertussis or other communicable infection where clinically relevant
- aspiration-related cough
- less common serious causes if red flags are present

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Care

Advise:

- maintain hydration
- rest and monitor symptom trajectory
- avoid smoke/vaping exposure and respiratory irritants
- consider humidification or warm fluids for comfort
- honey may be considered for cough relief if age-appropriate and no contraindication

### Medicines / Product Selection

Medicine selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age restrictions, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, co-morbidities, and scope of practice.

General public-facing principles:

- Avoid duplicating ingredients across combination cough/cold products.
- Avoid sedating products when sedation would increase risk, e.g. driving, falls risk, frailty, or concomitant CNS depressants.
- Avoid suppressing a productive cough unless clinically justified.
- Consider whether the cough is medication-related before recommending symptomatic treatment.
- Reassess if symptoms worsen or fail to improve.

Detailed product- or dose-specific prescribing tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- expected course: acute viral cough can persist for days to a few weeks
- red flags requiring urgent reassessment
- correct use of any selected OTC product
- maximum daily dose and avoidance of duplicate active ingredients
- avoiding alcohol or other sedating agents if using sedating cough/cold medicines
- avoiding smoking/vaping and respiratory irritants
- return precautions if cough persists, worsens, or new systemic symptoms develop

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- symptoms worsen
- cough persists beyond 3 weeks
- fever persists or systemic symptoms develop
- SOB, chest pain, haemoptysis, confusion, cyanosis, or severe weakness occurs
- asthma/COPD symptoms worsen or rescue inhaler use increases
- the patient becomes clinically unwell or is unable to maintain hydration

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Acute cough of [duration], [dry/productive], associated symptoms [summary]. Red flags screened: [none / present]. Relevant PMHx/medicines reviewed, including respiratory disease and medicine-induced cough risk.

**Impression:** Acute cough likely [viral URTI / alternative suspected]. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Non-pharmacological advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
