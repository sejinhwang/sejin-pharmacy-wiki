# Dermatitis Atopic Eczema

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Atopic dermatitis, or eczema, is a chronic relapsing inflammatory skin condition associated with pruritus, xerosis, erythema, excoriation, lichenification, and impaired skin barrier function.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- affected sites and distribution
- itch severity and sleep disturbance
- erythema, scaling, dryness, excoriation, fissuring, weeping, crusting, or lichenification
- duration, recurrence, and flare pattern
- triggers: soaps, detergents, fragrances, heat, sweating, wool, stress, allergens, occupational exposure, or infection
- previous moisturisers/topicals and response
- impact on QoL, school/work, and sleep

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- asthma, allergic rhinitis, food allergy, or other atopic disease
- immunocompromised status
- diabetes or skin infection risk
- current medicines and allergy status
- topical corticosteroid use history and concerns
- occupational wet-work or irritant exposure

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- suspected eczema herpeticum: painful rapidly worsening eczema, punched-out erosions, vesicles, fever, or systemic illness
- spreading infection, cellulitis, abscess, purulence, or honey-coloured crusting with systemic symptoms
- severe widespread flare or erythroderma
- significant sleep loss, pain, or functional impairment
- treatment-refractory or recurrent infected eczema
- diagnostic uncertainty, e.g. psoriasis, scabies, tinea, contact dermatitis, or drug eruption
- immunocompromised status with worsening rash
- eye involvement or severe facial involvement
- growth/feeding concerns in children or severe QoL impact

Escalate urgently if eczema herpeticum, severe skin infection, systemic illness, or erythroderma is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Distribution | Flexural or chronic relapsing pattern supports atopic dermatitis. |
| Excoriation/lichenification | Indicates chronic itch-scratch cycle. |
| Weeping/crusting | May indicate secondary infection. |
| Vesicles or punched-out lesions | Raises concern for eczema herpeticum. |
| Trigger exposure | Guides avoidance and prevention strategy. |
| Sleep/QoL impact | Helps gauge severity and need for referral. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- atopic dermatitis
- irritant or allergic contact dermatitis
- psoriasis
- tinea corporis
- scabies
- impetigo or infected eczema
- seborrhoeic dermatitis
- drug eruption
- cutaneous T-cell lymphoma in atypical persistent adult presentations

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- regular emollient use and skin barrier support
- avoid known irritants and fragranced products
- use gentle cleansers and avoid harsh soaps
- avoid overheating and manage sweating triggers
- keep nails short to reduce excoriation
- consider wet-work protection and gloves where relevant
- review bathing habits and moisturise soon after washing

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, age, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, site of involvement, severity, infection features, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild flare without infection | Emollient optimisation and local anti-inflammatory pathway where appropriate. |
| Facial, eyelid, genital, or flexural involvement | Use caution and follow site-specific local guidance. |
| Suspected infection | Refer or follow infection pathway according to severity/local policy. |
| Severe, widespread, or refractory disease | Refer for clinician review. |
| Eczema herpeticum concern | Urgent referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- applying emollients liberally and regularly
- avoiding irritants and known triggers
- correct order and timing of topical products according to local guidance
- avoiding underuse/overuse of topical anti-inflammatory medicines
- recognising signs of secondary infection
- seeking urgent care for painful rapidly worsening rash, vesicles, or systemic symptoms
- chronic relapsing nature and flare-prevention strategy

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- rash becomes painful, rapidly worse, weeping, crusted, or infected
- fever or systemic symptoms develop
- vesicles or punched-out erosions occur
- eye involvement develops
- sleep/function is significantly impaired
- symptoms fail to improve despite appropriate measures

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Eczema flare: sites [x], severity [mild/moderate/severe], itch/sleep impact [x], signs of infection [yes/no], triggers [x]. Red flags screened: eczema herpeticum, cellulitis/systemic illness, eye involvement, severe widespread disease [summary].

**Impression:** Likely atopic dermatitis flare / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Emollient and trigger-avoidance counselling provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
