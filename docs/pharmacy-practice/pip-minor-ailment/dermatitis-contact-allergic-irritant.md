# Dermatitis Contact Allergic Irritant

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Contact dermatitis is an inflammatory skin reaction caused by direct exposure to irritants or allergens. Irritant contact dermatitis is due to skin barrier damage; allergic contact dermatitis is a delayed hypersensitivity reaction.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- site and distribution of rash
- erythema, dryness, scaling, fissuring, vesicles, weeping, crusting, or lichenification
- itch, burning, stinging, pain, or tenderness
- timing relative to exposure
- occupational or household exposures: soaps, detergents, solvents, gloves, metals, cosmetics, plants, adhesives, topical products, or PPE
- previous episodes and recurrence pattern
- treatments tried and response

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- atopic dermatitis history or impaired skin barrier
- occupation, hobbies, wet-work, or repeated handwashing/sanitiser exposure
- immunocompromised status or diabetes
- current medicines and allergy status
- topical steroid or topical antibiotic exposure

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- severe, widespread, blistering, ulcerated, or rapidly progressive rash
- facial, eyelid, genital, or extensive hand involvement requiring clinician review
- suspected secondary infection: spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, pus, fever, or severe pain
- systemic symptoms or mucosal involvement
- occupational dermatitis needing formal assessment/workplace documentation
- diagnostic uncertainty, e.g. tinea, scabies, psoriasis, drug eruption, cellulitis, or shingles
- immunocompromised status with worsening rash
- persistent or recurrent disease despite avoidance and appropriate initial measures

Escalate urgently if severe allergic reaction, mucosal involvement, serious drug eruption, rapidly spreading infection, or systemic illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Exposure pattern | Sharp borders or exposure-site distribution supports contact dermatitis. |
| Vesicles/weeping | May suggest allergic contact dermatitis or acute eczema flare. |
| Fissures | Common in irritant hand dermatitis and can impair function. |
| Infection signs | Warmth, purulence, spreading erythema, fever require referral. |
| Occupational link | May require formal workplace or clinician assessment. |
| Mucosal/systemic features | Red flags for more serious diagnoses. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- irritant contact dermatitis
- allergic contact dermatitis
- atopic dermatitis
- psoriasis
- tinea infection
- scabies
- impetigo or cellulitis
- drug eruption
- photodermatitis
- shingles if painful dermatomal vesicles

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- identify and avoid suspected triggers where practical
- use bland emollients and barrier protection
- avoid fragranced products and harsh cleansers
- use gloves appropriately for wet-work or irritant exposure, avoiding prolonged occlusion where possible
- remove or avoid suspected allergen exposure, e.g. nickel, cosmetics, adhesives, plants, or topical products
- protect fissured skin and reduce repetitive irritation

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, age, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, site, severity, infection features, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild localised irritant dermatitis | Trigger avoidance, emollient/barrier care, and local pathway where appropriate. |
| Allergic pattern with identifiable exposure | Allergen avoidance and clinician review if persistent/recurrent. |
| Facial/eyelid/genital involvement | Use caution and follow site-specific local guidance or refer. |
| Suspected infection | Refer or follow infection pathway according to severity/local policy. |
| Occupational or refractory dermatitis | Refer for assessment and possible patch testing pathway. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- trigger identification and avoidance
- regular moisturising and barrier repair
- avoiding irritant soaps, fragrances, and repeated wet/dry cycles where possible
- glove use and hand protection strategies
- not using topical steroids or antimicrobials indefinitely without review
- recognising signs of secondary infection
- seeking review if rash persists despite avoidance measures

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- rash spreads, blisters, ulcerates, or becomes very painful
- fever or systemic symptoms develop
- swelling, pus, warmth, or spreading redness occurs
- facial/eye/genital involvement is significant
- symptoms persist despite trigger avoidance and appropriate measures
- occupational exposure is suspected and ongoing

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Dermatitis symptoms: site [x], morphology [erythema/scaling/fissures/vesicles], duration [x], suspected exposure [x]. Red flags screened: infection, systemic symptoms, mucosal/eye/genital involvement, occupational link, diagnostic uncertainty [summary].

**Impression:** Likely irritant/allergic contact dermatitis / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Trigger avoidance, emollient/barrier care, and skin-protection counselling provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
