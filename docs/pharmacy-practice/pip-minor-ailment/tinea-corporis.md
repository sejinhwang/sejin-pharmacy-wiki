# Tinea Corporis

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Tinea corporis, commonly called ringworm of the body, is a superficial dermatophyte infection affecting non-scalp, non-groin, non-foot skin. It often presents as annular, scaly plaques with central clearing and a more active border.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- lesion site, number, size, and distribution
- annular shape, scaling, central clearing, raised border, erythema, or itch
- duration and spread
- contact with pets, household members, gym mats, close-contact sport, or communal facilities
- previous topical corticosteroid use, which may mask or worsen fungal infection
- prior antifungal use and response
- recurrence history

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- immunocompromised status
- diabetes or peripheral vascular disease
- eczema, psoriasis, or dermatitis history
- current medicines and allergy status
- occupational/sport exposures
- signs of tinea at other sites, e.g. feet, groin, scalp, or nails

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- extensive, widespread, recurrent, or treatment-refractory disease
- immunocompromised status with worsening lesions
- suspected tinea capitis or significant nail involvement
- suspected secondary bacterial infection: increasing pain, warmth, swelling, purulence, or fever
- diagnostic uncertainty, especially psoriasis, eczema, pityriasis rosea, Lyme erythema migrans, or drug eruption
- facial, periocular, genital, or high-risk anatomical involvement according to local pathway
- severe inflammation, bullae, ulceration, or rapidly progressive rash

Escalate urgently if systemic illness, severe skin infection, or rapidly progressive rash is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Annular scaly plaque | Supports tinea corporis, especially with active border. |
| Central clearing | Common but not always present. |
| Distribution and extent | Widespread disease may require clinician review. |
| Steroid exposure | May cause tinea incognito and diagnostic uncertainty. |
| Signs of bacterial infection | Warmth, swelling, purulence, fever, or pain need referral. |
| Other fungal sites | Feet/nails/groin/scalp involvement may influence management and recurrence prevention. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- tinea corporis
- eczema or nummular dermatitis
- psoriasis
- pityriasis rosea
- contact dermatitis
- granuloma annulare
- erythema migrans where exposure risk fits
- drug eruption or other inflammatory rash
- tinea incognito after topical steroid exposure

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- keep affected area clean and dry
- avoid sharing towels, clothing, or sports gear
- wash hands after touching lesions or applying topical products
- avoid occlusive clothing where moisture/friction worsen symptoms
- consider pet/household source if recurrent or clustered cases
- avoid topical corticosteroid use unless directed by a clinician

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, lesion extent, immune status, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Localised typical tinea corporis | Consider local topical antifungal pathway where appropriate. |
| Extensive, recurrent, or refractory lesions | Refer for clinician review. |
| Steroid-modified or atypical rash | Refer or reassess diagnosis. |
| Scalp or nail involvement | Follow separate pathway or refer. |
| Suspected bacterial infection | Refer. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- completing selected treatment according to local/product instructions
- continuing hygiene and dryness measures
- avoiding scratching and preventing spread to other sites
- avoiding shared towels and clothing
- washing clothing/towels regularly
- reassessment if lesions spread, persist, recur, or become painful/infected

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- rash spreads rapidly or becomes extensive
- pain, warmth, swelling, pus, or fever develops
- rash does not improve after an appropriate treatment trial
- lesions involve scalp, nails, face/eye area, or high-risk sites
- diagnosis is uncertain or rash pattern changes
- the patient is immunocompromised or diabetic and symptoms worsen

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Rash consistent with possible tinea corporis: location [x], morphology [annular/scaly/central clearing], duration [x], extent [localised/widespread]. Red flags screened: secondary infection, scalp/nail involvement, immunocompromised status, diagnostic uncertainty [summary].

**Impression:** Likely tinea corporis / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Hygiene and transmission-reduction advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
