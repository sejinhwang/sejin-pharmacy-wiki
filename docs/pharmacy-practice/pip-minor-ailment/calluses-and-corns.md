# Calluses And Corns

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Calluses and corns are localised areas of hyperkeratotic skin caused by repeated pressure or friction. They commonly occur on the feet, toes, or hands and are often related to footwear, gait, occupational friction, or biomechanical pressure points.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- location and number of lesions
- pain, tenderness, pressure symptoms, or functional impairment
- duration and recurrence
- footwear fit, occupational pressure, sports, gait, or repetitive friction
- skin breakdown, bleeding, discharge, swelling, or erythema
- previous self-treatment, filing, pads, or keratolytic product use
- ability to inspect and care for feet safely

### Patient Context

Assess:

- diabetes
- peripheral vascular disease
- peripheral neuropathy or reduced sensation
- anticoagulant use or bleeding risk where relevant
- immunocompromised status
- frailty, poor vision, reduced mobility, or inability to self-care
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- current medicines and allergy status
- history of foot ulceration, amputation, or podiatry care

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical/podiatry assessment if any of the following are present:

- diabetes with foot lesion, pain, ulceration, or reduced sensation
- peripheral vascular disease or neuropathy
- ulceration, bleeding, discharge, infection, or spreading erythema
- severe pain, rapidly worsening lesion, or inability to weight bear
- immunocompromised status with skin breakdown
- uncertain diagnosis, e.g. wart, foreign body, melanoma, or other lesion
- recurrent or function-limiting lesions requiring biomechanical assessment
- patient unable to safely self-manage feet

Escalate urgently if diabetic foot infection, spreading cellulitis, acute ischaemia, or severe systemic illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Lesion site and pressure point | Helps identify footwear or biomechanical cause. |
| Skin integrity | Ulceration or fissuring requires referral. |
| Pain and weight-bearing | Severe pain or inability to weight bear needs assessment. |
| Diabetes/vascular/neuropathy status | Strongly lowers threshold for referral. |
| Signs of infection | Warmth, swelling, erythema, pus, or fever requires referral. |
| Differential features | Verruca, foreign body, or atypical pigmentation may alter pathway. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- callus
- corn
- plantar wart/verruca
- foreign body reaction
- pressure ulcer
- diabetic foot ulcer
- fungal infection with hyperkeratosis
- eczema/psoriasis
- malignancy or atypical pigmented lesion if appearance is unusual

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- reduce pressure and friction
- review footwear fit and avoid tight shoes
- use cushioning or protective pads if appropriate and safe
- keep skin moisturised while avoiding application between toes if maceration risk
- avoid cutting lesions with blades or sharp tools
- seek podiatry/foot-care support if recurrent or biomechanical
- high-risk feet should be managed by appropriate foot-care professionals

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment/product selection should follow local policy, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, diabetes/vascular/neuropathy status, skin integrity, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Low-risk patient with intact skin and typical callus/corn | Consider conservative pressure-relief and self-care measures. |
| Painful or recurrent pressure lesion | Consider podiatry/footwear/biomechanical review. |
| Diabetes, neuropathy, vascular disease, or impaired self-care | Refer; avoid self-treatment that may damage skin. |
| Broken skin, bleeding, discharge, or infection signs | Refer for medical assessment. |
| Diagnostic uncertainty | Refer for assessment before treatment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- pressure and friction reduction
- safe footwear and protective padding principles
- avoiding sharp removal or aggressive filing
- avoiding medicated corn/callus products in high-risk feet unless specifically advised by a clinician
- daily foot checks for people with diabetes or neuropathy
- seeking reassessment if pain, redness, discharge, ulceration, or bleeding occurs

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical or podiatry assessment if:

- pain worsens or walking becomes difficult
- skin breaks, bleeds, discharges, or becomes red/warm/swollen
- there is diabetes, neuropathy, vascular disease, immunocompromised status, or prior foot ulceration
- lesion recurs despite pressure reduction
- diagnosis is uncertain or lesion appearance changes

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Hyperkeratotic lesion consistent with callus/corn: location [x], pain/function impact [x], duration [x]. Red flags screened: diabetes, vascular disease, neuropathy, ulceration, infection, diagnostic uncertainty [summary].

**Impression:** Likely callus/corn related to pressure/friction / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Pressure-reduction and safe foot-care advice provided. Product advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific risk factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
