# Minor Aphthous Ulcers Mouth Ulcers

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Minor aphthous ulcers are common, painful, shallow oral ulcers that usually heal spontaneously. They typically appear on non-keratinised oral mucosa and are not associated with vesicles.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- number, size, and location of ulcers
- duration and recurrence pattern
- pain severity and impact on eating/drinking
- trauma, biting, dental appliance irritation, or recent dental work
- associated fever, systemic symptoms, rash, genital ulcers, eye symptoms, or GI symptoms
- oral hygiene products, new medicines, or food triggers
- previous episodes and treatments tried

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- immunocompromised status
- IBD, coeliac disease, Behcet disease, haematological disease, or nutritional deficiency risk
- current medicines and allergy status
- smoking status and alcohol use where relevant
- denture or orthodontic appliance use

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical or dental assessment if any of the following are present:

- ulcer lasting more than 2 to 3 weeks
- large, severe, recurrent, or multiple ulcers
- unexplained weight loss, fever, malaise, or systemic symptoms
- dysphagia, odynophagia, or inability to maintain oral intake
- genital ulcers, eye symptoms, rash, or GI symptoms suggesting systemic disease
- immunocompromised status or neutropaenia risk
- fixed, indurated, bleeding, or non-healing lesion
- suspected oral malignancy, leukoplakia, lichen planus, or drug reaction
- severe pain or dehydration risk

Escalate urgently if airway compromise, severe dehydration, severe systemic illness, or suspected serious mucocutaneous drug reaction is present.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Ulcer duration | Non-healing ulcers need referral. |
| Location | Aphthous ulcers usually occur on non-keratinised mucosa. |
| Vesicles/crusting | Suggests HSV or other viral cause rather than simple aphthous ulcer. |
| Systemic symptoms | May suggest IBD, infection, haematological disease, or systemic inflammatory disease. |
| Oral intake | Poor intake increases dehydration risk. |
| Fixed/indurated lesion | Raises concern for malignancy or other pathology. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- minor aphthous ulcer
- traumatic ulcer
- herpes simplex infection
- oral candidiasis
- hand-foot-mouth disease
- lichen planus
- leukoplakia or oral malignancy
- Behcet disease
- IBD- or coeliac-associated oral ulceration
- haematological or nutritional deficiency-related ulceration
- drug-related mucosal reaction

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- avoid trauma from sharp foods, biting, or dental appliances
- maintain oral hygiene with gentle products
- avoid triggers if clearly identified
- use bland rinses or comfort measures where appropriate
- maintain hydration and nutrition
- dental review if appliances or dental trauma contribute

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, immune status, oral intake, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Small typical minor aphthous ulcer without red flags | Symptomatic management and reassurance may be appropriate. |
| Pain affecting intake | Consider local analgesic/protective approach where appropriate. |
| Recurrent or multiple ulcers | Refer for clinician review and underlying-cause assessment. |
| Non-healing, fixed, or indurated lesion | Urgent referral. |
| Systemic symptoms or immunocompromised status | Refer. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- expected healing course for typical minor ulcers
- avoiding local trauma and irritating foods
- maintaining oral hygiene and hydration
- using selected products according to local/product instructions
- seeking review for recurrent, persistent, severe, or atypical ulcers
- avoiding unnecessary antimicrobial use unless clinically indicated

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical or dental attention if:

- ulcer persists beyond 2 to 3 weeks
- ulcers are recurrent, large, multiple, or severe
- fever, weight loss, GI symptoms, genital ulcers, eye symptoms, or rash develops
- eating/drinking becomes difficult
- lesion is fixed, hard, bleeding, or atypical
- the patient is immunocompromised or at risk of neutropaenia
