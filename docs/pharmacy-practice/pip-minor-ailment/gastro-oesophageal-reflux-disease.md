# Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) refers to reflux of gastric contents causing troublesome symptoms such as heartburn, acid regurgitation, sour taste, throat irritation, or reflux-associated cough.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- heartburn or retrosternal burning
- acid regurgitation or sour/bitter taste
- epigastric discomfort
- nausea, bloating, belching, or early satiety
- cough, hoarseness, throat clearing, or nocte symptoms
- relationship to meals, lying flat, bending, alcohol, caffeine, spicy/fatty foods, or large meals
- frequency, duration, and impact on sleep or daily activities

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- previous diagnosis of GORD, peptic ulcer disease, or upper GI pathology
- NSAID, ASA, anticoagulant, antiplatelet, bisphosphonate, corticosteroid, potassium, iron, or other GI-irritant medicine use
- smoking/vaping and alcohol use
- weight change and dietary triggers
- renal/hepatic impairment where product choice may be affected
- current medicines, including OTC antacids, alginates, H2 receptor antagonists, or proton pump inhibitors
- previous response to therapy and any rebound symptoms after stopping therapy

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- dysphagia or odynophagia
- haematemesis, melaena, or suspected GI bleeding
- unexplained weight loss
- persistent vomiting
- iron deficiency anaemia or symptoms suggestive of anaemia
- severe, persistent, or progressive abdominal pain
- new-onset dyspepsia/GORD symptoms in an older adult according to local referral criteria
- chest pain where cardiac cause has not been excluded
- recurrent symptoms despite appropriate initial therapy
- history of complicated ulcer disease, GI malignancy, or significant alarm features

Escalate to A&E if chest pain, haemodynamic instability, significant GI bleeding, or acute severe illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Pain location and character | Helps differentiate reflux, dyspepsia, biliary, cardiac, or other causes. |
| Relation to meals/position | Reflux often worsens after meals or lying flat. |
| Weight trend | Unintentional weight loss is a red flag. |
| Stool/vomit history | Melaena or haematemesis suggests GI bleeding. |
| Medicine history | NSAIDs, ASA, antithrombotics, bisphosphonates, iron, and potassium can contribute to GI symptoms or harm. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- functional dyspepsia
- peptic ulcer disease
- medication-related dyspepsia or oesophagitis
- cardiac chest pain
- biliary disease
- gastritis
- malignancy or other structural disease if alarm features are present
- pregnancy-associated reflux
- reflux-associated cough or laryngopharyngeal reflux

## Management Principles

### Lifestyle and Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where relevant:

- avoid large late meals
- avoid lying down soon after meals
- elevate head of bed for nocte symptoms if appropriate
- reduce trigger foods/drinks if clearly associated
- reduce alcohol intake and avoid smoking/vaping exposure
- weight management where appropriate
- review NSAID/ASA or other GI-irritant medicines if clinically relevant

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age restrictions, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, co-morbidities, renal/hepatic function, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Infrequent mild symptoms | Consider antacid or alginate-based symptomatic treatment where appropriate. |
| Frequent or troublesome reflux symptoms | Consider acid suppression strategy according to local policy. |
| Nocte symptoms | Review meal timing, positional measures, and appropriateness of therapy. |
| NSAID-associated symptoms | Review indication, gastroprotection need, and referral criteria. |
| Refractory or recurrent symptoms | Refer for clinician review and further investigation where appropriate. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- expected onset and appropriate duration of any selected therapy
- correct timing of any selected medicine according to product instructions
- avoiding duplicate acid-suppression therapy unless clinically directed
- avoiding unnecessary prolonged OTC use without review
- lifestyle triggers and meal timing
- red flags requiring urgent assessment
- need for review if symptoms recur rapidly after stopping therapy

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- symptoms worsen or persist despite appropriate measures
- dysphagia, odynophagia, vomiting, haematemesis, melaena, or unexplained weight loss occurs
- chest pain, SOB, sweating, radiation to arm/jaw, or collapse occurs
- recurrent symptoms require frequent or prolonged OTC treatment
- new symptoms arise in a higher-risk patient according to local referral criteria

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Reflux/dyspepsia symptoms: [heartburn/regurgitation/epigastric symptoms], duration [x], frequency [x], triggers [x]. Red flags screened: [none / present]. PMHx and medicines reviewed, including GI-irritant medicines and antithrombotic risk where relevant.

**Impression:** Symptoms consistent with uncomplicated GORD / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Lifestyle advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
