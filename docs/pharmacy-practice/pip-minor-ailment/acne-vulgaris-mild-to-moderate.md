# Acne Vulgaris Mild To Moderate

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Acne vulgaris is a chronic inflammatory condition of the pilosebaceous unit. Mild to moderate acne commonly presents with comedones, papules, pustules, and variable post-inflammatory erythema or hyperpigmentation.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- lesion type: open comedones, closed comedones, papules, pustules, nodules, cysts, or scarring
- distribution: face, chest, back, shoulders, or other sites
- severity and extent
- duration and previous flares
- pain, tenderness, drainage, or psychosocial impact
- post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation or scarring
- triggers: cosmetics, occlusion/masks, sweating, medicines, menstrual pattern, stress, or skin-care products
- previous treatments tried and response

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and pubertal status where relevant
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- signs of hyperandrogenism, e.g. hirsutism, irregular menses, or rapid onset severe acne
- current medicines, including corticosteroids, lithium, some antiepileptics, hormones, and supplements
- skin sensitivity, eczema, rosacea, or dermatitis history
- allergy status and previous adverse reactions
- adherence barriers and preference for topical versus other approaches

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- severe nodulocystic acne
- scarring, keloid risk, or significant post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation concern
- sudden onset severe acne
- systemic symptoms or suspected infection
- marked psychological distress, depression, self-harm concern, or severe social impairment
- signs of hyperandrogenism or endocrine disorder
- pregnancy where product choice requires clinician review under local policy
- failure of appropriate initial therapy or recurrent severe flares
- diagnostic uncertainty, e.g. rosacea, perioral dermatitis, folliculitis, drug eruption, or hidradenitis suppurativa

Escalate urgently if severe mental health risk or serious infection is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Lesion type | Comedonal versus inflammatory pattern guides management pathway. |
| Severity and distribution | Widespread, truncal, nodular, or scarring disease may require referral. |
| Scarring/pigment change | Early referral may reduce long-term morbidity. |
| Medication triggers | Some medicines can worsen acneiform eruptions. |
| Pregnancy/breastfeeding status | Changes product suitability and referral threshold. |
| Psychosocial impact | Acne can significantly affect QoL and mental health. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- acne vulgaris
- acne rosacea
- perioral dermatitis
- folliculitis
- drug-induced acneiform eruption
- contact dermatitis or cosmetic-related eruption
- hidradenitis suppurativa if lesions are nodular/recurrent in intertriginous areas

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- gentle cleansing once or twice daily
- avoid harsh scrubs, picking, or squeezing lesions
- use non-comedogenic moisturiser and sunscreen
- avoid occlusive cosmetics or oily hair/skin products if contributing
- set expectations: improvement is gradual and may take weeks
- address adherence, irritation prevention, and skin barrier support

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, skin sensitivity, severity, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mainly comedonal mild acne | Consider topical comedolytic approach where appropriate. |
| Mild inflammatory papules/pustules | Consider local topical anti-inflammatory/antimicrobial pathway where appropriate. |
| Moderate, truncal, scarring, or nodular disease | Consider clinician review. |
| Pregnancy or breastfeeding | Use pregnancy-/lactation-appropriate pathway and local guidance. |
| Significant irritation from treatment | Review application technique, frequency, moisturiser use, and need for reassessment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- expected time to improvement and importance of adherence
- applying topical products as directed and avoiding over-application
- managing dryness, peeling, or irritation with gradual introduction and moisturiser where appropriate
- photosensitivity precautions where relevant
- avoiding picking to reduce scarring and post-inflammatory changes
- avoiding duplicate irritating products unless clinically directed
- reassessment if worsening, scarring, severe distress, or poor response occurs

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- acne becomes painful, nodular, cystic, or scarring
- sudden severe acne develops
- significant mood impact, distress, or self-harm thoughts occur
- rash appears atypical or widespread
- signs of endocrine disorder are present
- symptoms fail to improve after an appropriate trial according to local guidance

**Impression:** Mild to moderate acne vulgaris / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Skin-care, adherence, and irritation counselling provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
