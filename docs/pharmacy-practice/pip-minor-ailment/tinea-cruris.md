# Tinea Cruris

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Tinea cruris, commonly called jock itch, is a superficial dermatophyte infection affecting the groin and adjacent upper thigh. It often presents with itchy, scaly, erythematous plaques with an active border and relative sparing of the scrotum.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- location: groin, inner thigh, perineal area, scrotal involvement, buttock, or lower abdomen
- itch, burning, pain, scaling, erythema, maceration, fissuring, or odour
- annular or advancing border
- duration, recurrence, and spread
- sweating, occlusive clothing, sport, friction, or hot/humid exposure
- tinea pedis or nail involvement
- previous topical steroid or antifungal use and response

### Patient Context

Assess:

- diabetes, obesity, immunocompromised status, or recurrent fungal infection history
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- current medicines and allergy status
- dermatitis, psoriasis, intertrigo, or candidiasis history
- hygiene, moisture, and clothing factors
- diagnostic uncertainty or genital/sexual health concerns requiring referral

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- severe pain, swelling, ulceration, blistering, or rapidly progressive rash
- fever or systemic symptoms
- suspected cellulitis, abscess, or bacterial superinfection
- immunocompromised status with worsening lesions
- extensive, recurrent, or treatment-refractory disease
- significant genital lesions, ulcers, discharge, or STI concern
- diagnostic uncertainty, especially psoriasis, candidiasis, intertrigo, eczema, erythrasma, or STI-related rash
- diabetes with skin breakdown or spreading infection

Escalate urgently if severe genital infection, rapidly spreading cellulitis, systemic illness, or necrotising infection concern is present.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Groin/thigh distribution | Supports tinea cruris when typical and scaly with active border. |
| Scrotal involvement | Prominent scrotal involvement may suggest candidiasis or other diagnosis. |
| Moisture/friction factors | Contribute to recurrence and persistence. |
| Tinea pedis/nail involvement | Feet or nails may act as a reservoir. |
| Steroid exposure | May mask features and worsen fungal infection. |
| Signs of bacterial infection | Pain, warmth, swelling, purulence, or fever requires referral. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- tinea cruris
- candidal intertrigo
- irritant or allergic contact dermatitis
- eczema
- psoriasis
- erythrasma
- scabies
- STI-related genital rash or ulcers
- bacterial intertrigo or cellulitis

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- keep groin area clean and dry
- change sweaty clothing promptly
- use loose, breathable clothing where possible
- avoid sharing towels or clothing
- manage concurrent tinea pedis or nail infection if present
- avoid topical corticosteroid use unless directed by a clinician
- reduce friction and moisture triggers where practical

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, lesion extent, immune status, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Localised typical tinea cruris | Consider local topical antifungal pathway where appropriate. |
| Recurrent disease with tinea pedis | Address foot reservoir and recurrence prevention. |
| Genital ulcers/discharge or STI concern | Refer to appropriate clinical pathway. |
| Extensive, painful, or refractory rash | Refer for clinician review. |
| Diabetes/immunocompromised with worsening lesions | Lower threshold for referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- completing treatment according to local/product instructions
- continuing moisture-control measures after symptoms improve
- treating associated tinea pedis if present according to local guidance
- avoiding scratching and sharing towels/clothing
- avoiding topical steroid self-treatment unless directed
- reassessment if rash worsens, recurs, or fails to improve

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- rash becomes painful, ulcerated, blistered, or rapidly progressive
- fever or systemic symptoms develop
- swelling, pus, spreading redness, or severe tenderness occurs
- genital ulcers, discharge, or STI concern is present
- symptoms persist despite appropriate treatment and hygiene measures
- the patient is immunocompromised or diabetic and symptoms worsen

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Groin rash consistent with possible tinea cruris: location [x], morphology [scaly/erythematous/active border], duration [x], recurrence [yes/no]. Red flags screened: bacterial infection, genital ulcers/discharge, STI concern, diabetes/immunocompromised status [summary].

**Impression:** Likely tinea cruris / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Moisture-control and hygiene advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
