# Folliculitis Shaving Rash Hot Tub Rash

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Folliculitis is inflammation of hair follicles. It may be infectious, irritant-related, occlusion-related, or associated with shaving, friction, sweating, hot tubs, or contaminated water exposure.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Features

Document:

- location and distribution of papules/pustules
- relationship to shaving, waxing, friction, tight clothing, sweating, or occlusion
- hot tub, pool, spa, or contaminated water exposure
- itch, tenderness, pain, drainage, swelling, or crusting
- duration and progression
- recurrence history
- fever or systemic symptoms
- previous self-treatment and response

### Patient Context

Assess:

- diabetes or immunocompromised status
- recurrent boils, abscesses, or MRSA history where relevant
- eczema, dermatitis, acne, or hidradenitis suppurativa history
- current medicines and allergy status
- occupational/sport exposures
- shaving technique and skin-care products

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- abscess, boil, fluctuance, or significant purulent drainage
- rapidly spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, or severe pain
- fever or systemic illness
- facial/periorbital involvement or high-risk anatomical site
- immunocompromised status or diabetes with worsening infection
- extensive, recurrent, or treatment-refractory disease
- suspected hidradenitis suppurativa, cellulitis, impetigo, or deeper infection
- severe pain out of proportion or necrotising infection concern

Escalate urgently if systemic illness, rapidly spreading cellulitis, periorbital infection, or necrotising infection concern is present.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Follicular papules/pustules | Supports folliculitis when centred on hair follicles. |
| Exposure history | Shaving, friction, occlusion, sweating, and hot tub exposure guide likely cause. |
| Abscess/fluctuance | Requires referral or procedural assessment. |
| Spread and tenderness | Suggests cellulitis or deeper infection. |
| Recurrent pattern | May suggest colonisation, shaving trigger, or alternate diagnosis. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- bacterial folliculitis
- pseudofolliculitis barbae / shaving rash
- hot-tub-associated folliculitis
- acne vulgaris
- impetigo
- contact dermatitis
- insect bites
- hidradenitis suppurativa
- scabies
- cellulitis or abscess

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- avoid shaving or friction over inflamed areas until improved
- use clean razors and avoid sharing shaving equipment
- shave with the grain and avoid close shaving if recurrent shaving rash
- wear loose breathable clothing where friction/occlusion contributes
- shower after sweating or hot tub/pool exposure
- avoid squeezing pustules or picking lesions
- maintain hand hygiene and launder towels/clothing regularly

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, severity, extent, infection signs, diabetes/immune status, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild localised folliculitis without red flags | Hygiene, trigger reduction, and local pathway where appropriate. |
| Shaving-related rash | Modify shaving technique and reduce irritation. |
| Hot-tub-associated rash without systemic symptoms | Supportive care and avoidance of exposure may be appropriate. |
| Abscess, cellulitis, systemic symptoms, or severe pain | Refer for medical assessment. |
| Recurrent or refractory disease | Refer for clinician review and recurrence prevention plan. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- hygiene and avoiding lesion manipulation
- reducing friction, occlusion, and shaving trauma
- not sharing razors or towels
- cleaning or avoiding suspected contaminated exposure sources
- seeking reassessment if lesions spread, become painful, or do not improve
- recognising abscess/cellulitis features

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- redness spreads, pain worsens, or swelling develops
- fever or systemic symptoms occur
- a boil/abscess develops
- lesions involve the face/eye area or high-risk sites
- the patient is diabetic or immunocompromised and symptoms worsen
- symptoms recur frequently or fail to improve as expected
