# Conjunctivitis

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva and may be viral, bacterial, allergic, irritant-related, or associated with another ocular condition. Public-facing assessment must prioritise identifying red flags that require urgent eye assessment.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- unilateral or bilateral eye involvement
- redness pattern and onset
- discharge: watery, mucoid, purulent, crusting, or absent
- itch, gritty sensation, burning, pain, or foreign-body sensation
- photophobia
- visual change or blurred vision
- eyelid swelling
- contact lens use
- recent trauma, chemical exposure, or foreign body risk
- associated URTI symptoms or allergic symptoms
- exposure to others with conjunctivitis
- duration and progression

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- contact lens use
- immunocompromised status
- history of ocular surgery, glaucoma, dry eye disease, or recurrent eye disease
- current eye medicines or recent ophthalmic treatment
- allergy status and previous adverse reactions
- occupational or environmental irritant exposure

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer urgently for medical or eye assessment if any of the following are present:

- eye pain rather than mild irritation
- photophobia
- reduced vision, visual disturbance, or new blurred vision
- contact lens use with red eye
- suspected foreign body, trauma, or chemical exposure
- severe unilateral red eye
- corneal opacity, hazy cornea, or abnormal pupil if observed
- marked eyelid swelling or periorbital cellulitis concern
- recurrent, persistent, or treatment-refractory symptoms
- neonatal eye symptoms
- immunocompromised patient with worsening eye symptoms

Escalate to A&E or urgent eye care if vision change, severe pain, trauma, chemical exposure, or contact-lens-associated keratitis is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Visual change | Any reduced vision is a red flag. |
| Pain and photophobia | Suggests keratitis, uveitis, glaucoma, trauma, or other serious pathology. |
| Contact lens use | Raises concern for keratitis and lowers referral threshold. |
| Discharge type | Watery may be viral/allergic; purulent may suggest bacterial involvement but is not diagnostic alone. |
| Itch | Prominent itch supports allergic conjunctivitis. |
| Eyelid/periorbital swelling | May indicate preseptal/orbital cellulitis or more severe disease. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- viral conjunctivitis
- bacterial conjunctivitis
- allergic conjunctivitis
- dry eye or blepharitis
- irritant conjunctivitis
- keratitis, especially with contact lens use
- uveitis
- acute angle-closure glaucoma
- corneal abrasion or foreign body
- preseptal or orbital cellulitis

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- hand hygiene and avoidance of touching/rubbing eyes
- avoid sharing towels, face cloths, eye cosmetics, or contact lens equipment
- clean crusting gently with clean water and cotton/gauze
- use cool compresses for comfort where suitable
- avoid contact lenses until assessed/resolved according to local guidance
- discard or replace contaminated eye cosmetics/contact lens items if relevant

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, contact lens status, symptom severity, ocular history, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild watery red eye with URTI symptoms and no red flags | Supportive care and hygiene advice may be sufficient. |
| Prominent itch/watery eyes with allergic symptoms | Consider allergic conjunctivitis pathway. |
| Purulent discharge without red flags | Consider local policy approach; reassess if not improving. |
| Contact lens wearer with red eye | Refer urgently rather than routine symptomatic management. |
| Pain, photophobia, or visual change | Urgent referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- hand hygiene and reducing spread
- avoiding contact lens use until appropriate
- avoiding eye rubbing
- avoiding sharing towels or cosmetics
- expected course and reassessment timeframe
- safe administration technique for any selected eye product
- avoiding contamination of dropper tips
- red flags requiring urgent assessment

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical attention if:

- eye pain develops
- light sensitivity occurs
- vision changes or worsens
- contact lens-related red eye occurs
- swelling around the eye worsens
- symptoms are unilateral and severe
- symptoms do not improve or worsen despite appropriate measures
- trauma, foreign body, or chemical exposure is suspected

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Red eye/conjunctivitis symptoms: laterality [unilateral/bilateral], discharge [watery/purulent/none], itch/pain/photophobia/vision change [summary], contact lens use [yes/no]. Red flags screened: [none / present]. Ocular history and allergies reviewed.

**Impression:** Likely [viral/bacterial/allergic/irritant] conjunctivitis / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Hygiene and non-pharmacological advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
