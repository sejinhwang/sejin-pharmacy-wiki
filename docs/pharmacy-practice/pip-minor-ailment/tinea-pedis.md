# Tinea Pedis

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Tinea pedis, commonly called athlete's foot, is a superficial dermatophyte infection of the foot. It commonly affects the interdigital spaces but may also involve the sole or lateral foot.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- itch, burning, stinging, or discomfort
- scaling, maceration, fissuring, peeling, erythema, or vesicles
- site: interdigital, plantar, moccasin-type, lateral foot, or widespread
- laterality: unilateral or bilateral
- duration and recurrence
- odour, discharge, pain, swelling, or secondary skin breakdown
- nail involvement suggesting possible onychomycosis
- exposure risks: communal showers, occlusive footwear, sweating, sports, or shared footwear/towels

### Patient Context

Assess:

- diabetes or peripheral vascular disease
- immunocompromised status
- peripheral neuropathy or reduced foot sensation
- recurrent cellulitis or skin breakdown
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- current medicines and allergy status
- previous antifungal use and response
- occupational/sport factors causing prolonged moisture exposure

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- diabetes with foot ulceration, spreading infection, or uncertain diagnosis
- significant pain, swelling, warmth, purulent discharge, or suspected cellulitis
- systemic symptoms or fever
- extensive, recurrent, or refractory infection
- immunocompromised patient with worsening skin infection
- suspected bacterial superinfection
- severe fissuring, ulceration, or maceration
- significant nail involvement requiring separate assessment
- diagnostic uncertainty, especially psoriasis, eczema, contact dermatitis, or pitted keratolysis

Escalate urgently if diabetic foot infection, rapidly spreading cellulitis, or systemic illness is suspected.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Location and morphology | Interdigital maceration/scaling supports tinea pedis; plantar diffuse scaling may suggest moccasin-type infection. |
| Skin integrity | Fissures, ulceration, or maceration increase bacterial infection risk. |
| Signs of bacterial infection | Warmth, swelling, purulence, marked pain, or spreading erythema requires referral. |
| Nail involvement | May indicate onychomycosis and influence management. |
| Diabetes/neuropathy status | Raises risk and lowers referral threshold. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- contact dermatitis
- eczema
- psoriasis
- pitted keratolysis
- bacterial intertrigo or secondary infection
- candidal intertrigo
- onychomycosis if nails are involved

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise:

- keep feet clean and dry
- dry carefully between toes after washing
- change socks regularly
- use breathable footwear where possible
- rotate footwear and allow shoes to dry
- avoid sharing towels, socks, or footwear
- wear footwear in communal shower/changing areas
- manage excessive moisture and sweating where relevant

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, co-morbidities, skin integrity, infection extent, prior response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Mild localised interdigital disease | Consider topical antifungal therapy where appropriate. |
| Moist/macerated interdigital disease | Emphasise drying measures and assess for secondary infection. |
| Extensive plantar/moccasin-type disease | Consider clinician review if widespread, recurrent, or refractory. |
| Nail involvement | Assess for onychomycosis pathway rather than treating as simple tinea pedis alone. |
| Diabetes, ulceration, or suspected cellulitis | Refer for medical assessment. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- completing the full recommended treatment course for the selected product
- continuing hygiene and drying measures even after symptoms improve
- avoiding scratching and preventing spread to other body sites
- washing hands after applying topical treatment
- avoiding sharing towels or footwear
- recurrence prevention through moisture control and footwear hygiene
- seeking reassessment if symptoms fail to improve or recur frequently

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- spreading redness, warmth, swelling, pus, severe pain, or fever develops
- skin breaks down, ulcers, or significant fissures appear
- diabetes, neuropathy, or immunocompromised status is present with worsening symptoms
- nail involvement is significant or progressive
- symptoms persist despite appropriate treatment and hygiene measures
- diagnosis is uncertain or rash spreads beyond the feet

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Foot rash consistent with possible tinea pedis: location [interdigital/plantar/lateral], symptoms [itch/scaling/maceration/fissuring], duration [x]. Red flags screened: [none / present]. Diabetes, immunocompromised status, skin integrity, and nail involvement reviewed.

**Impression:** Likely tinea pedis / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Foot hygiene and moisture-control advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
