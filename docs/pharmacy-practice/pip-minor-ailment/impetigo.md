# Impetigo

Status: Public-safe standalone draft. Requires clinical/local policy review before use as a protocol.

## Scope

Impetigo is a superficial bacterial skin infection that commonly presents with crusted erosions, vesicles, bullae, or honey-coloured crusting. It is contagious and is more common in children, close-contact settings, and areas of broken skin.

This page is intended as a public clinical reference and migration draft. It does not include patient identifiers, personal prescriber identifiers, signatures, registration numbers, or private workflow credentials.

## Assessment Framework

### Presenting Symptoms

Document:

- lesion location and number of lesions
- appearance: honey-coloured crust, vesicles, bullae, erosions, weeping, or excoriation
- duration and rate of spread
- itch, pain, tenderness, warmth, swelling, or discharge
- fever or systemic symptoms
- recent insect bites, eczema flare, trauma, shaving, or skin breakdown
- household, school, childcare, sport, or close-contact exposure
- previous episodes or recurrent infection

### Patient Context

Assess:

- age and frailty
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where treatment choice may be affected
- immunocompromised status
- diabetes or peripheral vascular disease
- eczema, dermatitis, scabies, or other skin barrier disruption
- MRSA history or recurrent skin infection history where relevant
- current medicines and allergy status
- previous treatment response and adverse reactions

## Red Flags Requiring Referral

Refer for medical assessment if any of the following are present:

- fever or systemic illness
- rapidly spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, or severe pain
- suspected cellulitis, abscess, or deeper infection
- extensive, widespread, recurrent, or treatment-refractory lesions
- lesions near the eye or involving high-risk anatomical areas
- immunocompromised status with worsening infection
- diabetes with foot/leg lesions or skin breakdown
- bullous lesions in infants or high-risk patients
- suspected eczema herpeticum, shingles, scabies, or other alternative diagnosis
- signs of dehydration or poor oral intake in a child or frail patient

Escalate urgently if severe systemic illness, rapidly spreading cellulitis, orbital involvement, or necrotising infection concern is present.

## Focused Assessment / Observations

When available and within scope, document:

| Finding | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Lesion morphology | Honey-coloured crusting supports impetigo; vesicles/bullae may alter risk assessment. |
| Extent and spread | Widespread or rapidly spreading lesions require referral. |
| Surrounding erythema/warmth | Suggests cellulitis or deeper infection. |
| Eye proximity | Periorbital infection risk requires caution. |
| Skin barrier condition | Eczema, insect bites, or excoriation can predispose to infection. |
| Systemic symptoms | Fever or malaise suggests more significant infection. |

## Differential Diagnosis

Consider:

- impetigo
- infected eczema or dermatitis
- herpes simplex infection
- eczema herpeticum
- shingles
- scabies with secondary infection
- contact dermatitis
- insect bite reaction
- cellulitis or abscess
- hand-foot-mouth disease where distribution fits

## Management Principles

### Non-Pharmacological Measures

Advise where appropriate:

- avoid scratching or picking lesions
- keep lesions clean and covered where practical
- hand hygiene and regular laundering of towels/linen
- avoid sharing towels, razors, clothing, or personal items
- avoid close skin-to-skin contact until lesions are improving according to local public health guidance
- manage underlying eczema/skin barrier disruption where relevant
- clean minor cuts/bites promptly to reduce recurrence risk

### Medicines / Product Selection

Treatment selection should follow local policy, formulary, product monograph, patient-specific contraindications, age restrictions, pregnancy/breastfeeding status, lesion extent, local resistance patterns, previous response, and scope of practice.

General public-facing approach:

| Presentation | Practical option |
| --- | --- |
| Localised mild lesions without red flags | Consider local minor infection pathway where appropriate. |
| Extensive, recurrent, or rapidly spreading lesions | Refer for clinician review. |
| Cellulitis, abscess, fever, or systemic symptoms | Refer urgently. |
| Lesions near the eye | Refer due to higher-risk location. |
| Immunocompromised or diabetic high-risk patient | Lower threshold for referral. |

Detailed product- or dose-specific tables should be reviewed against current local policy before inclusion in this public wiki.

## Counselling Points

Counsel on:

- contagious nature and hygiene measures
- avoiding touching, scratching, or picking lesions
- washing hands after contact with lesions or topical products
- avoiding shared towels, clothing, razors, or bedding
- covering lesions where practical
- completing any selected treatment according to local/product instructions
- reassessment if lesions spread, worsen, or do not improve as expected

## Safety-Netting

Advise the patient to seek medical attention if:

- fever or systemic symptoms develop
- redness, warmth, swelling, or pain spreads
- lesions become extensive, rapidly progressive, or recurrent
- lesions occur near the eye
- pus, abscess, or deep tissue infection is suspected
- the patient is immunocompromised, diabetic, frail, or otherwise high risk and symptoms worsen

## Documentation Template

Use local documentation workflows. A public-safe note may include:

**Assessment:** Skin lesions consistent with possible impetigo: location [x], morphology [crusting/vesicles/bullae/erosions], duration [x], extent [localised/widespread]. Red flags screened: fever/systemic symptoms, cellulitis, eye involvement, immunocompromised/diabetes risk [summary].

**Impression:** Likely impetigo / alternative diagnosis considered. Referral required: [yes/no and reason].

**Plan:** Hygiene and transmission-reduction advice provided. Product/medicine advice, if any, selected according to local policy and patient-specific factors. Safety-netting and follow-up advice provided.
